The question of _hijrah_(((hijrah))) (emigration) relates to the question of _wilayah_(((Wilayah))), with that breadth in which we have framed the question of wilayah here. We said that wilayah consists of forging a firm bond and connection among the elements of the believing ranks with one another, and of severing every form of attachment between the believing ranks and the non-believing ranks; and, at a further level, of forging a most powerful and strong bond between every individual of the believing ranks and that central point and concentrated authority upon whom the administration of the Islamic society rests — that is, the _imam_, the _wali_, the ruler, the leader.@@CX(Wilayah | binds believers together and to the divine wali)@@ Alongside this, we then discussed who the _wali_ and leader is within the Islamic society. The Qur'an gave us our answer:

— to which we pointed, in connection with the affair of the Commander of the Faithful, may God's blessings be upon him. If we have been able to understand wilayah with this breadth, and if we do not reduce the matter to the level of the subsidiary issues of wilayah and to the second degree of wilayah — as some, without due attention, do reduce it — then the question of hijrah turns out to be one of the corollaries of the question of wilayah. Why do we say it this way? Because if it is necessary that every human being live within the wilayah of God and of the _wali_ of God — which is what the principle of wilayah taught us — and if we have accepted that a human being must put all of his energies, all of his bodily, intellectual, and psychic vitalities to work under the will of the divine _wali_ and the governor who is _min qibal Allah_footnote:s28-2["From God."] (from God), and, in short, that with every element of his being a human being must be a servant of God and not a servant of the _Taghut_ — if we accept and embrace these points, then we have no choice but to accept this too: that if somewhere our existence, our being and all our energies and vitalities, were not under the command of the divine wilayah but rather under the command of the _Taghuti_ and satanic wilayah, then our divine commitment is that we free ourselves from the bonds and shackles of the wilayah of the Taghut(((Taghut))) — that we rescue ourselves, liberate ourselves, and go beneath the most blessed shade of the wilayah of God. Coming out of that oppressive wilayah and entering that just wilayah — its name is _hijrah_.@@CX(hijrah | moving from oppressive wilayah to just wilayah)@@ You see, then, that the question of hijrah is one of the matters that arises in the train of wilayah. This is why we placed the discussion at the very outset: "Concerning Wilayah" — that is, in connection with wilayah we mentioned three topics, and this is the fourth.

Why must a human being flee from beneath the wilayah of the Taghut and Satan? This question that I am raising is very much worth attending to. When I raise the question, I would dearly like you, the friends who are listening, to break down and analyze my question at once in the laboratory of your minds, in such a way that you can give it an answer in accordance with your Islamic and religious learning and accumulated knowledge. Then, if your answer turns out not to coincide with my answer — the answer that I will give — and is at variance with it, then there remains room for discussion between you and me. The question is this: Is it not possible to be within the wilayah of the Taghut and yet be a Muslim? Is it not possible for us to suppose a Muslim who is under the wilayah of Satan, yet who lives as a servant of the All-Merciful? Is such a thing possible or not? Is it possible that, in a setting where over the whole horizon and across all the regions of human life a non-divine factor is busy ruling — where a non-divine factor is administering the human body, where a non-divine factor is administering human thought, where that same factor is dragging the morale, the affections, and the sentiments of the members of society this way and that — while a human being lives gripped in the fist of the power of such Taghuti and satanic factors, that nonetheless he be a servant of God, be a Muslim? Is such a thing possible or impossible? I beg you to set about answering this. You need not answer aloud; prepare an answer to this question in your minds; see whether it is possible or not possible. In order for us to know whether it is possible or not, we must analyze and interpret this very question a little, so that the answer may become clear.

We ask: can a person be under the wilayah of Satan and be a Muslim, or can he not? These two questions are, in reality, a single question composed of two parts; let us properly break down these two parts and see what each means.

The first part is that a person be under the wilayah of Satan. What does it mean to be under the wilayah of Satan? What does wilayah even mean? If we place that meaning which we derived for wilayah from the verses of the Qur'an — a meaning which was supported by several Qur'anic verses — beside this phrase "the wilayah of Satan," then it becomes clear what the wilayah of Satan means. The wilayah of Satan(((Satan))) means this: that Satan — in that comprehensive sense of "Satan" of which I have spoken many times — be dominant over all the powers, capacities, initiatives, and works within a person's being. Whatever the person does is along the course that Satan has determined; whatever he thinks is on the path that Satan inclines toward or sketches out.@@CX(Taghut | wilayah of Satan dominates all of a person's powers and thought)@@ It is like a man who has fallen into the current of a river. A swift water is rushing down in the bed of a flood from the mountainside; a man falls into this river. Of course he does not wish to strike the huge, towering boulders and split his head open; of course he does not wish to be carried off by this water and dropped into a swamp; of course he does not wish to be choked and drowned amid the waves of this water — but, despite not wishing it, this swift current of water is carrying him off against his will. He flails his arms and legs too, clings to this side and that, grasps at everything — but the current of the water is a swift current; against his will it carries him away.

The wilayah of the Taghut and the wilayah of Satan is just such a thing. This is why the verse of the Qur'an says:

Have you not looked upon those people who were ungrateful for the bounty of their Lord? This bounty for which they were ungrateful — what was it? It could be anything. The bounty of power, which is a manifestation of the power of the Lord; worldly powers; the bounty of stewardship and administering the affairs of thousands of human beings; the bounty of having at one's disposal the capacities, thoughts, energies, and abundant powers of human beings — all of these are bounties; these are capitals that could have been a source of good for humanity. The human beings who came under the control of those people referred to in this verse — these human beings could have been great human beings, could have been the elite servants of God, could have reached the highest degrees of perfection; but these people were ungrateful for these bounties, did not put them to work in the way that they ought. Then the verse goes on to say: "_wa ahallu qawmahum dar al-bawar_" — and they too cast their own people into the abode of perdition; they drove their people and the people under their command into the realm of nonexistence, annihilation, and destruction. "_Jahannama yaslawnaha_" — and they drove them into Hell, into which they will be made to slide and be flung. "_Wa bi'sa al-qarar_" — and what an evil abode and place of settlement it is!

This verse was recited by Musa ibn Ja'far(((Musa ibn Ja'far))), may God's blessings and peace be upon him, to Harun(((Harun al-Rashid))); he made Harun understand that "you are that very one who will cast your people and yourself into the worst dwelling place, into the most calamitous of resting-places." Musa ibn Ja'far recited this meaning to Harun explicitly. He asked: "Are we then unbelievers?" — meaning, "do we then not believe in God, in the Prophet, and in the religion?" The Imam, peace be upon him, in answer recited this verse so as to make him understand that the unbeliever is not only the one who says plainly, directly, and explicitly, "No, there is no God; no, the Qur'an is a lie," or "the Prophet, for instance, is a legend." This is one type of unbeliever, and it is the best kind of unbeliever, the one who states his position explicitly so that a person knows him and properly arranges his own stance toward him. The worse unbeliever is the one who is ungrateful for these immense bounties at his disposal, who casts them into the wrong channel — who drags not only himself, but all the human beings under his command, into Hell.@@CX(Taghut | the worse unbeliever squanders God's bounties and drags others into Hell)@@ The wilayah of the Taghut is just such a thing.

The one who lives under the wilayah of the Taghut has, in reality, as it were no will of his own. I do not say he is utterly devoid of will — now that we have interpreted the verse of the Qur'an, the explanation of the matter becomes clear in the Qur'an — but he is being carried off in the current of the flood. He wants to flail his arms and legs, he cannot. He wants to turn back from the road of Hell; he sees that everyone all around is moving toward Hell, and they drag him along with them. Those places where there is a large crowd — have you been there? Sometimes you want to go this way; the crowd carries you off against your will like a wisp of straw. He wants to be good, to live a good life, to live as a human being, to remain a Muslim and die a Muslim — he cannot; that is to say, the social current is dragging and carrying him off, carrying him off in such a way that he cannot even flail his arms and legs. And even if he does flail, he has accomplished nothing other than the waste of a certain amount of energy. Not only can he not stir his limbs — what is more painful is that sometimes he cannot even comprehend.

These fish that they catch at sea — I do not know whether you have seen this or not. Sometimes thousands of fish are being dragged within a single net toward the shore; from out in the midst of the sea, from several kilometers out, this net is dragging them all forward, and they are unaware. If you were to say to that fish, "Where are you going?", it would think it was going toward some destination by its own choice; but in reality it has no choice; its destination is the very place that is the destination of that fisherman who owns the net. This invisible net of the ignorant order(((jahiliyyah))) drags a human being along so, drags him so toward those directions in which the steerers of that net incline, that the person does not comprehend where he is going. Sometimes he even imagines that he is heading toward the abode of felicity and salvation — heedless of the fact that no, he is heading toward "_Jahannama yaslawnaha wa bi'sa al-qarar_."@@CX(jahiliyyah | the ignorant order drags people unaware toward ruin)@@

Well, this wilayah is the wilayah of the Taghut, the wilayah of Satan. This was the first clause of the two clauses out of which the question was composed. We said: Can one live within the wilayah of the Taghut and live as a Muslim? Can one? Now we have understood, in summary, what living within the wilayah of the Taghut means. And if we wish to understand it in detail too, we can turn back again to history.

Look at how, in the time of the Banu Umayyah(((Banu Umayyah))) and the Banu 'Abbas(((Banu 'Abbas))), the world of Islam moved with such vitality; look at what an immense wave of knowledge and learning arose within the Islamic society of that day, what great physicians appeared, what great translators appeared in that era of dearth of linguistic knowledge and of general world-knowledge, who translated into Arabic the great works of the ancient cultures and disseminated them among the Muslims. In every field — history, hadith, the natural sciences, medicine, astronomy, and even the beautiful and delicate arts — the Muslims of that day became among the most outstanding exemplars; is that not so? — to such an extent that even now, when a man like "Gustave Le Bon"(((Gustave Le Bon)))footnote:s28-5[Gustave Le Bon (1841–1931 CE), a French sociologist and orientalist who carried out extensive studies on Muslims and Islamic civilization. "Adam Mez"(((Adam Mez)))footnote:s28-6[Adam Mez (1869–1917 CE), a German orientalist resident in Basel, Switzerland. He describes a great civilization in the fourth Islamic century. A French orientalist, a European orientalist, in general, when he looks back at those third, fourth, and second Islamic centuries, his eyes are still dazzled. Why is this? It is because of this: that strange activities, energies, and vitalities issued in that day from the Islamic society. But I ask you: did all this vitality and activity that was carried out in that day ultimately turn out to the benefit of the Islamic society and of humanity?@@CX(Taghut | scientific brilliance under tyranny did not benefit humanity)@@ I ask you.

Ten centuries are passing from that era. We have no fanaticism toward that era. Vis-a-vis the non-Muslim world we can say, "Yes" — it was the world of Islam that brought those universities into being, it was the world of Islam that constructed that philosophy, it was the world of Islam that did such and such in medicine and the natural sciences. But, between ourselves, in all truth and fairness: did all that power and energy turn out to be in the right place, at the right time, and to the benefit of humanity and of the Islamic society? After ten centuries, what does the Islamic society have of that legacy? And why does it not have it? Why does it not have it? Why has that scholarly and cultural wealth not remained for us? Why is it that we, as a society that ten centuries ago possessed all that radiance, today do not shine in the world and make no display? Why? Is it not because all that vitality and activity — though it was a human activity — was beneath the signet-ring of the Taghut? As the poet said:

It was these _Ahrimans_ (demons) who toyed with the Islamic society. If they did speak, if they translated, it was for the sake of writing in the translation, "In the reign of His Excellency So-and-so the umpteenth Abbasid caliph, Mansur or Harun or Ma'mun" — in order to raise up their own names. If, instead of all this activity in the natural sciences, mathematics, astronomy, literature, jurisprudence, recitation, and the rest, they had permitted the 'Alawi government to take charge, had permitted Imam Sadiq(((Imam Sadiq))) to take charge — had they allowed Imam Sadiq to take in hand the vitalities and energies — then even if, scientifically and from the standpoint of literature and of those matters that the world today preens and boasts about, they had fallen back a hundred years, it would still have been to the benefit of humanity. Humanity would have grown, Islam would have blossomed, energies and capacities would have been put to work along the right path. Of what use is it that they translate books, that they bring medicine and scientific progress to the utmost degree, but from the standpoint of morality and of social ethics they are so weak that their class discord is still to be counted among the legends of history?

Exactly like the filthy, disgraceful civilization of our own contemporary world. The great world powers preen over their scientific discoveries, preen over their mind-boggling inventions, raise the cocked corner of their cap of pridefootnote:s28-8[_Kulah-gusheh_ (the cocked corner of the cap): rank and majesty; a metonym for greatness and pride. This is from the scientific standpoint; but from the human and moral standpoint, even now, to this very day, they are living in the eras of thousands of years of pre-history. Even now, fabulous riches stand alongside fabulous hungers and poverties; even now there exists the enjoyment by a very small one percent in the world of privileges that exist at the expense of the millions of starving human beings in the deprived countries.@@CX(social class | scientific progress coexists with deadly class disparity)@@ They preen over this — and this is exactly the same condition as that great Islamic civilization in the second, third, and fourth centuries; it is the very same matter; in that day too it was just so. Vast scientific progress, but aristocratic excess and indulgence and heedlessness of virtues, of human qualities, and of authenticities; class discord in its severest form, deadly hungers in the face of deadly satieties — in that day too these were evident and to be seen. On account of what were these miseries? Why was it that the Islamic society of that day, with all its scientific vitality and activity, could not be a flower in the garden of virtues and human qualities? Why?

So then, within the wilayah of the Taghut, can one live as a Muslim? What does living as a Muslim mean, sir? Living as a Muslim means that all the resources, energies, powers, strengths, and capacities of a human being be wholly and without reserve at the disposal of God.@@CX(servitude | living as a Muslim means placing all of one's powers at God's disposal)@@ Everything of theirs: their wealth at the disposal of God, their lives at the disposal of God, their daily activity and movement at the disposal of God, their night's sleep at the disposal of God, their thought and reflection at the disposal of God. Can you give an example of this? Yes. We have examples both at the level of societies, in the form of a community and a civic order, and we have examples in groups; among the rebellious groups that came out of the Taghuti systems and fled and made _hijrah_ toward God — there we have examples.

The first — like the society of Medina(((Medina))), in the lifetime of the Prophet. In the lifetime of the Prophet, the society of Medina was a society of servants of God; it was a Muslim society. In the time of the Commander of the Faithful(((Commander of the Faithful))) too, it was more or less the same way, because the Commander of the Faithful, may God's blessings be upon him, was, from the standpoint of being a divine ruler and the _wali_ of God, no different from the Prophet; but he was the heir of a bad society. He was the heir of disarrays, the heir of disorders; and even the Prophet himself, had he been in the place of the Commander of the Faithful and come twenty-five years later, would certainly have faced the very same difficulties as the Commander of the Faithful. This is in communities.

And as for groups: the group of the Shi'ah around the Imams, peace be upon them, throughout history. But ordinary individuals — in general the individual cannot be a Muslim; cannot have his whole being, his resources, his energies, all his powers and forces and capacities under the command of God, while living within such a society as I described earlier — the Taghuti society. If a person lives within a Taghuti environment and a Taghuti system, some fraction of his being a Muslim is, after all, in the way of the Taghut; some portion of his life is, after all, in servitude to the Taghut; he cannot be one hundred percent a servant of God.@@CX(servitude | within a Taghuti system one cannot be wholly a servant of God)@@

A remarkable hadith, this. This hadith says that those people who live under the wilayah of the _wali_ of God are people of salvation, even if in personal and private affairs they sometimes have shortcomings, failings, and sins too; and those people who live under the wilayah of Satan and the Taghut are people of wretchedness and torment, even if in personal affairs, in private deeds, they are people of good works and good deeds too.@@CX(Wilayah | allegiance to a just or unjust leader decides salvation over private piety)@@ This is very remarkable. Of course, I have said that there are several phrasings, and all the phrasings yield this very same meaning. The hadith is in _Usul al-Kafi_. Just now I did not refer to it — it seems to me, since I am usually scrupulous about looking at the chains of transmission of the hadiths, I think its chain was a good chain too. Some time ago I saw it; just now I do not recall.

I always used to liken this hadith — I would say it is as though you boarded a car bound for a certain definite point, say, intending to reach Nishapur(((Nishapur))). If this car is heading toward Nishapur, you will certainly reach the goal. If this car, instead of going toward Nishapur, is going, say, toward Sarakhs or toward Quchan — well, it is certain that you will not reach the goal. Now, in that car that is going toward Nishapur and brings you to the goal, if in the car the people, with courtesy, kindness, and good conduct, with human manners, associate with one another — so much the better; and if they do not associate with one another with human manners and humaneness and goodness and beneficence — in the end they reach Nishapur, they reach the goal; even though along the way they did a little evil, those evils of course will have certain punishments, certain effects, certain consequences; they are compelled to bear those consequences too; but they reach the goal. Contrary to that: the car that is supposed to bring you to Nishapur is going to the point opposite Nishapur; if in this car all the individuals behave toward one another with courtesy, with refinement, with respect, deal with one another with good cheer, and they see that this car, instead of going to Nishapur, is heading toward Quchan, yet in the face of this event and this occurrence they show no reaction whatsoever — these are very good people, very kind to one another, but will they reach the goal or not? It is obvious — no.

In the first example, the driver was a trustworthy driver, a driver who knew the road — he was "_imamin min Allah_" (a leader from God) — he brought them to the goal, even though they were of bad character: "_wa in kanat al-ra'iyyatu fi a'maliha zalimatan musi'atan_" (even if the flock was, in its deeds, wrongdoing and evildoing). In the second example, the driver was not a driver who knew the road; he was not a trustworthy driver; he was a passion-worshipping driver, drunk, he had lost the road, he had business in Sarakhs and Quchan, he had given precedence to his own want over the wish of the people, he was heading there; these will certainly not reach the goal — even though inside the car the people were very kind to one another, very warm, very good-natured: "_wa in kanat al-ra'iyyatu fi a'maliha barratan taqiyyatan_" (even if the flock was, in its deeds, righteous and godfearing) — but in the end, "_la-u'adhdhibanna_" (I shall surely punish), they will fall into the torment of God, they will not reach the goal. Therefore, in a society administered by the wilayah of the Taghut — like that very car steered by the driving of an untrustworthy man who does not know the road — human beings will not reach the goal and cannot remain Muslims.@@CX(Taghut | under its rule people cannot reach the goal or remain Muslims)@@

"_Qalu_" — the angels say to them — "_fima kuntum_" — "In what condition were you? Where were you?" A person might imagine that the angel of heaven, when it sees that this man's condition is so wretched — like that man who has come, that physician, or that surgeon who has come to operate, say, on a patient, and sees that his condition is very bad, that this patient's condition is very lamentable and despair-inducing — says, "Wherever were you living? In answer they say — "_qalu_," they say — "_kunna mustad'afina fi l-ard_": "We, in the earth, among the people where we were living, were among the oppressed and downtrodden (_mustad'afin_); we were part of the will-less mass." The _mustad'afin_(((mustad'afin))) are that group within a society in whose hands the control of the society does not lie; these are the downtrodden. In the society's course and in its going, in its movement, in its repose, in its orientation, in its activity, they have no say.@@CX(mustad'afin | the downtrodden mass with no say in society)@@ As I said yesterday, it goes wherever the pullers of the rope desire. So they go, the poor wretches, having no say of their own as to where to go and what to do.

Imagine a handful of small elementary-school children, first grade — and not even at the age of seven, for seven-year-old children today have their eyes and ears far more open than this — imagine a four- or five-year-old child placed in a school, a _maktab_, like the _maktabs_ of old. I recall: when we used to come out of the _maktab_ toward home, in a group, we did not even understand at all where we were going. Children do not pay attention to what is where. A monitor, say — some older child with a stick — "Go this way, go that way, don't go under a car," or "Do go under the car!" — sometimes he even wishes that this would happen (laughter from the audience) — they do not at all pay attention to where they are going; suddenly they find they are in their own house, say; they find they are in the house of some other student, say. Now if this monitor takes a fancy to lead them around the street a little, they find they are at such-and-such a place, say.

The _mustad'afin_ of the earth are those who, within a society, have no news of the goings-on of the society. They do not know what is where; they do not understand where they are going; and from here where they are going, where they will end up, and who is carrying them, and how one might avoid going, and, now that we have not gone, what we must do next — they understand none of it at all, they do not pay attention at all; just so, they have hung their heads down, like — with all due distinction, with all due distinction, with all due distinction — the millstone horse.footnote:s28-17[A horse that, in the old mills, by its own movement along a fixed circular path, set the mill turning.] The horse, with all due distinction, of course (laughter from the audience). They have blindfolded its eyes; it keeps pacing, keeps walking. It just keeps going, going, going; it goes round, goes round, goes round. If this animal were capable of understanding anything, at dusk it would imagine to itself that "by now we must be around Paris and thereabouts, we have come such a long way" (laughter from the audience). Then, when they uncover its eye, it sees it is the very same place where it was at first dawn. It does not at all know where it has gone; it does not understand where it is going while it is moving.

The _mustad'afin_ in a society are these — the majority, the uninformed mass of the people. Note this point: since today is the last day of the discussion of recitation and our Qur'anic study, this topic remains roughly half-finished. Let me state this point: _hijrah_ from the abode of unbelief, from the wilayah of other-than-God, from the wilayah of Satan and the Taghut — to where? To the abode of emigration (_dar al-hijrah_), the abode of faith (_dar al-iman_), beneath the wilayah of God, beneath the wilayah of the imam; beneath the wilayah of the Prophet and the divine _wali_ one must make _hijrah_ — this is _hijrah_.@@CX(hijrah | is migration from the abode of unbelief to the abode of faith under God's wilayah)@@ Now, if such a region did not exist in the world, what is to be done? Sometimes it becomes necessary that a group of human beings, by their own _hijrah_, inaugurate the starting-point of _hijrah_, found the divine and Islamic society, bring it into being, create the abode of emigration; then the believers make _hijrah_ to that place.@@CX(hijrah | a vanguard may have to found the Islamic society others migrate to)@@ The upshot of the matter — this is the substance of the discussion concerning _hijrah_.
