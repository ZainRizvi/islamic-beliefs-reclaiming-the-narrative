The discussion about faith is, in reality, a preliminary discussion. In order to understand religion and to know the essential, foundational principles of the religion — so that a desire for the doctrinal belief of religion may be kindled within us, and so that we may set out, in a serious way, to grasp religion and to know religion — we need, for this purpose, to know the worth of faith(((Faith, iman))) and the quality of faith. Our discussion about faith was for this reason and to this end.@@CX(Faith | studied to know its worth as preliminary to grasping religion)@@

The two or three matters that were stated about faith were two or three fundamental and important matters. Among them was this: that faith must be conscious, not blind. Another was this: that faith must be coupled with commitment and action — that it must produce commitment and give birth to action, and not be a dry, empty belief in the heart and the mind.@@CX(Faith | must be conscious not blind, and coupled with action)@@ Another was this: that a committed believer is a believer only when his faith is not occasional, not opportunistic, not self-interested — but rather constant, present everywhere, embracing everyone, comprehensive in every dimension. These are the matters that were pointed out in connection with faith, and which it was necessary to know.

Before we enter into the substance of the topics we have in view — that is, the doctrinal teachings of Islam(((Islam))) — one more short discussion is necessary, which we are obliged to begin today, and tomorrow that discussion will continue. At first I imagined that we would finish this discussion in a single day; but once I delvedfootnote:s06-2[_ghawr_ (غور): to plunge in, to sink in — i.e., to delve deeply.] into it, I saw that it would inevitably take four or five days to complete. Today we will certainly discuss it, and tomorrow as well — provided we remain alive, it will probably not drag on to a third day. And that discussion is this: now, in order for us to know the value of faith and the result of faith, it is necessary that we become acquainted with the glad tidings and the promises that God has given to the believers. Let us see what God Almighty pledges in return to the believer, in exchange for his faith, in exchange for his righteous deeds, and his fulfillment of his commitments — what He pledges to him in reciprocation.@@CX(Faith | God's glad tidings and promises are its reward)@@

A human being who has grown accustomed to give-and-take, who has passed his life by way of exchange, likes to see what his exchange with God amounts to. He brings faith, and on the basis of that faith he becomes committed; in return he likes to know what commitment God takes upon Himself in his regard, what glad tiding and what promise He gives him. This is a matter that — from the standpoint of the believer, and from the standpoint of one who wishes to be firm-footed, steadfast, and unshakeable in the field of faith — is an interesting, sweet, desirable, and lovable subject, and it is a source of hope for the believer.

We gathered all the verses in the Qur'an(((the Qur'an))) that bear on faith and the believer. There are roughly seven hundred verses in the Qur'an concerning the believer and faith and certain other characteristics. We set about looking at these verses to see what, upon faith, God has made to follow. (I am describing the method of the work, so that the friends, the brothers and sisters who are familiar with the Qur'an, may learn how to find subjects in the Qur'an.) We searched through these six or seven hundred verses to see what promises, what glad tidings, what worthy, desirable, and longed-for outcomes God has appointed for the believers. We found that they are very many. In my view, there are perhaps around thirty or forty items that God Almighty, in the Qur'an, has made to follow upon faith. The believer enjoys these thirty or forty great privileges — and all of these privileges are great, all of them are important, all of them are necessary, obligatory, and essential for a human being to become happy.@@CX(Faith | thirty or forty privileges follow upon it in the Qur'an)@@ One of these thirty or forty subjects is the otherworldly Paradise(((Paradise))); that is one of them. One of these glad tidings is:

Before we set out these few subjects, some of these subjects that the Qur'an has promised to the believer have been noted here, and I will read them — from this very handout that is now in your possession, and which is also before mefootnote:s06-4[Refer to the photocopied handout at the end of the session.] — there are ten or eleven of them; I will read them out one by one, briefly, and explain them, so that afterwards we may reach the Qur'anic verses.

Here we have written: in order to benefit from comprehensive and complete happiness, what does a human being need? What does a human being need in order to be happy? Those things that a human being needs in order to be completely and comprehensively happy have, in their entirety, been promised to the believer and made to follow upon faith. These things that we have written here are ten or twelve subjects. See: is it truly the case that if even one of these subjects is lacking, a human being can feel happy? You see that, no, he cannot. Of course, there are other subjects too that play a part in a human being's feeling of happiness. And all of these subjects that are written here, and a great many other subjects besides, exist in the Qur'an in the form of a glad tiding and good news upon faith and to the believer. What conclusion do we draw? We draw the conclusion that faith — belief coupled with action, in the way that the Qur'anic culture defines it — is equivalent to all the conditions of happiness and felicity, as regards the needs and requirements that a human being might imagine for himself.@@CX(Keys to happiness | faith equals all the conditions of felicity)@@

The matter is not a matter of bias for the sake of a creed or of religiosity. What we have written here as the conditions of becoming happy — for a materialist(((materialism))), too, these very same things are the conditions of becoming happy.@@CX(Keys to happiness | the same conditions hold for the materialist, not a religious bias)@@ Now we will read them one by one and see whether a materialist, if he were to possess these, would feel happy or not. And after it becomes clear that these are the conditions of becoming happy, then we will return to the Qur'an, we will lend our ear to the heart-soothing speech of the Qur'an, and we will see that it has promised all of these to the believer, given the believer glad tidings of all of these, brought all of these as a gift for the believer —

— God does not give a false promise; though, of course, this part is connected to our particular religious way of thinking, namely that we believe in God.

What does a human being need? *One:* he needs to know the goal and the destination of happiness. He needs to know where he wants to arrive, to know for what goal he wants to strive, to see the point of completion and the end of the road from the very beginning, and to know the road to it. In addition to knowing and recognizing and understanding the goal, he needs to know by which road one must travel toward this goal in order to arrive, and to arrive sooner, and to arrive with certainty. Is not the recognition of the goal, the recognition of the end, the recognition of the destination, and the recognition of the road that leads to the destination, the very first and principal element of a human being's happiness? Here there is no difference between the godly and the materialist; the materialist, too, accepts this, agrees to it, feels it. In parentheses we wrote *"guidance"* — the first condition.@@CX(guidance | first condition of happiness, knowing the goal and the road to it)@@ Of course, this "first," and the "second" that we have numbered here, are certainly not meant in the sense of a logical sequence such that you might say this "second" could be placed first and the "first" could be placed second. Well, let it be so; we have now brought them in succession, and it is possible that other things too could be inserted in between these.

*Two:* that the veils of ignorance, of conceit, of fancy, and of everything that wraps the jewel of his insight and reason in a dark covering and strips from him the power of seeing and understanding — that these be removed. Many things prevent a human being from understanding. A person's conceit prevents him from understanding; ignorances prevent a human being from seeing and understanding; fancies and superstitions prevent a human being or a nation from grasping and understanding the truth; tyrannical systems prevent human beings from understanding and knowing. Various veils and obstacles, from within and from without, prevent a human being from putting to work the God-given jewel of intellect and reason and from knowing and understanding; they keep him in darkness, they seat the human being in a prison of darknesses, they hold him far from light, from the radiance of correct perception and understanding.@@CX(light/darkness | veils of ignorance, conceit, and tyranny imprison reason in darkness)@@ One of the pillars of a human being's happiness, and one of the elements of a human being's felicity, is this: that a human being be delivered from these darknesses, from everything that creates darkness for him, and find his way to the light and the radiance of truth, and that a beam of the light of truth shine upon his heart. In parentheses we wrote *"light."* So, first, guidance is necessary, in the sense that has been explained; second, light is necessary, in the sense that has, again, been explained — and these are two distinct things.@@CX(light/darkness | light, second condition, is distinct from guidance)@@

*Three:* that on his long road toward happiness — on this road that he is traversing toward that destination and the end of the road — he be delivered from the inner anxieties and temptations (pay attention) which are more exhausting than the external deterring factors. Sometimes they block your road and say, "Sir, we will not let you pass from here." Experience has shown, and history has told us explicitly, that when someone's road is blocked, the traveler becomes more eager in traversing this road, his enthusiasm increases, the fire of his ardor blazes more fiercely. If they say, "We will not let you go," the human being presses harder to go, to pass through. This is the external deterring factor. This deterring factor is outside the human being's existence. But sometimes it is from within that they rot the human being. They create doubt within him; they do not block the road — the road is open — but they strip from the human being the power to go, the will to go, the resolve to move, the possibility of striving; and this is worse. "Why are you going? What benefit does it have? Perhaps you will not arrive; perhaps a thief will reach you on the road; perhaps a wolf will come; on what warrant are you going? You need not go." Very coolly, sympathetically, in the manner of a wise old man, in the manner of a master — and the road is also open. This obstacle, this temptation, this anxiety, is by far more exhausting than that stick they place in the middle of the road, saying, "We will not let you go."@@CX(Itminan | inner anxiety and doubt are more exhausting than external obstacles)@@

This anxiety has existed for most travelers, most wayfarers of the roads of happiness throughout history. How much they pleaded with Moses(((Moses, Musa))), saying, "We fear that you have said something false, that a false promise has been given to you." In the Qur'an it says that the pressure and poverty were so great that even the elite would waver, asking:

— So when? So where is it? What happened? You see, these doubts and waverings and inner anxieties make even the elite slip. If a human being wishes to be happy and to reach the destination and the end of the road of happiness, one of his conditions, too, is this: that he be delivered from this anxiety, from this agitation, from this spiritual insecurity, from this lack of calm and this absence of assurance — that he be able to overcome this anxiety and agitation and inner temptation. This, too, is one of the things that brings a human being to happiness: that on his long road toward happiness he be delivered from the anxieties, the temptations, and the inner temptations that are more exhausting than the external deterring factors. Inside the parentheses we wrote *"assurance and security."*@@CX(Itminan | assurance and security, the condition of being delivered from inner anxiety)@@ You may put "safety" in place of "security"; it makes no difference. We chose "security" because it was the very Qur'anic expression. It would not be amiss for me to mention this sentence here — perhaps I have said it several other times as well: in the Du'a' Kumayl(((Du'a' Kumayl))) we recite, _"Ya Rabbi, ya Rabbi, ya Rabbi"_footnote:s06-7[Du'a' Kumayl, hadith no. 12. The Arabic phrases cited: _"Ya Rabbi, ya Rabbi, ya Rabbi… Qawwi 'ala khidmatika jawarihi, wa ashdud 'ala al-'azimati jawanihi."_] — O my Lord! O my Lord! O my Lord! _"Qawwi 'ala khidmatika jawarihi"_ — strengthen my limbs and members in Your service; _"wa ashdud 'ala al-'azimati jawanihi"_ — make my heart firm upon resolve. May I be able to resolve, may I be able to overcome weaknesses, doubts, uncertainties, temptations, and anxieties — for this is what halts a human being on the road, seats him long upon the road, and bars him from traversing the road.

*Four:* that he reckon his striving to be fruitful, that he be hopeful that this striving will reach somewhere. Those who are not hopeful that their striving and movement will lead to some result will assuredly not reach the destination of happiness and salvation. He should be assured that his striving is fruitful, should know that every act he performs leaves behind a positive effect, should know that every step he takes brings him one step closer to the goal.@@CX(Keys to happiness | one must reckon one's striving to be fruitful)@@ In a wilderness, if you know that the lodging is in this direction, you know where you are going; even though it has grown late, even though you have been left alone, you have fallen behind the caravan — steadfastly, firmly, with striving, with fervor, you move, you walk, you go forward. But if you have lost the road, you do not know whether to go this way or that way; in whatever direction you set off, you take a step, you see that you are sluggish — why? Because you do not know whether this striving will be fruitful. You reckon it possible that this very step, this one step, takes you farther from the lodging; and so you turn back from this way, you go again that way, you go again that way. Thus one of the conditions for a human being to be able to reach happiness — that wayfaring person, that striving person, that exerting person — his condition is this: that he reckon his striving and exertion to be fruitful.

*Five:* that his slips and errors be reparable and subject to pardon. This, too, is very important. In the course of his life and movement a human being has mistakes, has errors. If every error he commits were to remain as an unhealable wound, were to remain as an irreparable act, the human being would always be in anxiety lest he commit yet another error, and this further error throw him still farther from the goal and cast him aside from the road. He is always despairing of the past and always pessimistic about the future. But if he knows that his errors — on the condition that he himself sets about repairing them — are reparable; that his mistakes, conditioned upon his being himself remorseful of those mistakes, are subject to being overlooked — if he knows these things, his desire, his hope, his vigor, and his fervor will multiply several-fold. Inside the parentheses we wrote *"forgiveness and mercy."*@@CX(maghfirah | slips being reparable and subject to pardon is a condition of happiness)@@

*Six:* that in every state he enjoy a trustworthy support to hold onto.@@CX(Tawakkul | a trustworthy support to hold onto in every state)@@ He should know that everywhere, in all circumstances, there is a helper of whom he can avail himself — exactly like a person who has placed the complete map of the road in his pocket, set out upon the road, and is going along these roadways; he does not make mistakes, of course, but he also has no anxiety. He knows that if, perchance, at some time, at some place, he loses the road by mistake and goes astray, this map is at his side; he takes it out, looks at it, and avails himself of this map. Everywhere there is a handhold and a refugefootnote:s06-8[_mustamsak_ (root م‌س‌ك) and _musta'sim_ (root ع‌ص‌م): a handhold that one grasps; a refuge that gives shelter.] that he can grasp and avail himself of.

*Seven:* that in confronting enemies and hostilities he enjoy the help and aid of God. This, too, is another condition of happiness, felicity, and success.@@CX(Keys to happiness | the help and aid of God in confronting enemies)@@ Of course, the materialist does not believe in God; but with the materialist we do not bring up the name of God. We say, "Sir, in this material striving of yours, in this social striving of yours, in this jihad(((jihad))) you are carrying out — if you knew that there were a power, suppose, beyond the power of matter and nature, and that this power is with you and accompanies you, how would that be? Were you to have such a thing, were you to suppose for yourself such a help and aid and support, how would that be?" You see the spark fly from his eyes; he says, "That would be very good." How interesting it is for a human being to have a power beyond the power of matter and material things as his backer, so that when he faces enemies and their hostilities, their plots, their intrigues, their executioners' cruelties, he believes and knows that that power beyond matter is his helper and protector. Except that the materialist does not know the name of God, has no belief in God — although he also has no certainty that God does not exist. But the godly person, who has certainty in the existence of such a dominant and overpoweringfootnote:s06-9[_musaytir_ (root ص‌ط‌ر): dominant and overpowering.] might, beyond all these phenomena and relying upon it — see how much more swiftly and admirably he moves along the road of happiness.

*Eight:* that he have superiority and preponderancefootnote:s06-10[_rujhan_ (root ر‌ج‌ح): superiority, preponderance.] over the opposing fronts and ranks; that he know that, in the end, superiority and preponderance and preferability belong to him. This, too, in itself, has a remarkable effect on enabling a human being to traverse this road with greater ease.

*Nine:* that he be victorious over the enemies of his road and his goal, who are the obstructers and neutralizers of his striving. Should a person make all these efforts and then, in the end, be defeated?! That does not bring him to happiness. So one of the most important elements of a human being's happiness is this: that in the end he be victorious — is it otherwise? Do not the world's schools of thought strive for victory? So one of the elements and factors of human happiness — individual, social, and collective — is this: that in confronting enemies he be, in the end, victorious over them.@@CX(Keys to happiness | final victory over the enemies of one's goal)@@

*Ten:* that in the end he be released from all the hardships and pressures and bonds and barriers, and attain his object and aim, reaching that destination. Inside the parentheses we wrote *"triumph and prosperity"* — these very Qur'anic expressions.

*Eleven:* that in every state, on the road and at the lodging — both on the road to the goal and at the destination of the goal itself — he benefit from and enjoy the stores that have been made ready for the human being in this world. That the blessings of heaven and earth rain down upon him: the wheat of the earth, the drop of rain from heaven, the stores of the seas, the stores of the forests and the mines of the mountains, and all the vital materials and the non-vital materials necessary for the human being — and, above all, the source and mine of intelligence and perception and reason and aptitude and ingenuity of the human being — that all of these be opened up to him, and that he avail himself of all of them. This, too, is one of the things that has a part in a human being's happiness. And other things of this sort, which a human being might reckon to play a part in his own becoming happy.

And finally, after all these — which come to pass during the span of a human being's life and striving and wakefulness — afterward, when he dies, when this lamp is extinguished, when he becomes, to all appearance, the equal of an inanimate thing: then, far from ending, may the very thread of his benefit not come to an end. May his rest only then begin; may his receiving of reward and recompense only then begin; may it be reckoned the first point and the first step of his ease and enjoyment. A materialist, after he dies, holds that all his strivings have only a worldly result; beyond the world he has no hope of anywhere. To that very one we say, "Sir, after you have died and gone — granting the impossible, which here is not impossible — supposing that the very moment of your death becomes, freshly, the first moment of your ease, how would that be?" You see that this is the greatest pillar of happiness. And finally, that after the passing of the span of life and the ending of all strivings, he find himself met by a worthy reward, and repose in the Paradise of bounty and good pleasure.@@CX(Keys to happiness | death becoming the first moment of reward, the greatest pillar of happiness)@@

These are the conditions of happiness. For a human being to be happy, for a human being or a society to become fortunate, these are necessary. Now lend your ear to the speech of the Qur'an, which promises all of this to the possessor of faith — a faith that is committed and coupled with action. How is it? The Qur'an gives the believing people glad tidings of all those things that are reckoned to be the elements and constructive factors of happiness and felicity — these, and dozens of things besides these. It says these are for you. It promises guidance, it promises light, it promises safety and assurance and tranquility and repose of the spirit. It promises the fruitfulness and non-waste of strivings. And all of this — if we look at history and the past with a clear eye — we will observe in historical and human realities. And the way of God is also always one and the same.

Those who have brought faith and have done righteous deed — we explained "righteous deed," in parentheses, with a particular expression: righteous deed means the commitment commensurate with that faith. A human being's faith places a commitment upon his shoulders; the fulfillment of that commitment, the taking of that commitment upon one's shoulder, is the righteous deed.@@CX(Faith | righteous deed is the commitment commensurate with faith, fulfilled in action)@@ Those who bring faith, who attain that belief, and then also act in accordance with its commitments — _"yahdihim rabbuhum bi-imanihim"_ — their Lord will guide them on account of their faith. Faith itself causes them to find the road. To which road do they find it? Both to the goal and to the roads, to the means. Some people say, "Sir, how can we reach that desired destination?" When you probe this remark, you see that the faith of the first step is not in his heart; or if there is faith, action does not accompany it. If he acts, illumination and guidance will fall to his lot; he will also find the second step. "Set foot upon the road, and ask nothing at all; the road itself will tell you how one must go."footnote:s06-12[From a verse of 'Attar: "If you are a man of the road, you must go through blood; / and with foot fallen, headlong you must go. / Set foot upon the road and ask nothing at all; / the road itself will tell you how one must go." At the first step, none of the leaders and great ones and wayfarers and followers knew what the tenth step was. I sometimes give an example. I say: in a wilderness which, suppose, is tens of kilometers or more in length and breadth, on a dark and pitch-black night — no moon, no star — your honored self is going along the road alone, and a single small flashlight, a small bulb of the fifth size, a small candle, is also in your hand. They say to you, "Sir, with this candle you must go to the very end of that wilderness." You take a look and say, "Sir, this candle of mine illuminates no more than a radius of one meter; am I to traverse all these ten kilometers with this single candle? This candle of mine illuminates only one meter — am I to go ten kilometers?" This is a logic that an uninformed, inexperienced, unfamiliar person might have. What is its answer? What, in your view, is the answer to this blind logic? Is not the answer to this remark this: "Respected sir, is one meter around you lit, or not? Take one step forward — one more meter will be lit. If it is not, do not go. Go this one meter that is lit; take this one step that you can take and where you know you are placing your foot. If one more step is not lit before you, do not go; if it is, then go again. You will see that, gradually, to the very end of the wilderness it will become lit, and you will traverse this road and reach the lodging." Is it otherwise? Is it otherwise? _"Yahdihim rabbuhum bi-imanihim"_ — God, by their faith, will guide them; faith itself is the cause of a human being finding the roads.@@CX(guidance | comes incrementally to the one who strives, each step lighting the next)@@

So those who bring faith in God and become attached to and reliant upon Him — for faith in the heart alone is also not enough; one must grasp God, the skirt of God, that is, the skirt of God's religion and the godly road, become attached to it. _"Fa-amma alladhina amanu bi-llahi wa-'tasamu bihi"_ — they clung, they became attached; if it is thus, _"fa-sa-yudkhiluhum fi rahmatin minhu wa fadlin"_ — God will admit them into His mercy and bounty. After it: _"wa yahdihim ilayhi siratan mustaqiman"_ — He guides them toward Himself, leads them; and He guides by a straight and near road. This guidance is special to the believers. If you do not have faith — or if you have faith but do not have holding-fast, do not act upon your commitments before God — you will not find the road to God; the illumination of that guidance will not cast its radiance in your heart. God is the guardian and the comrade-in-the-same-front of the believers. I render _wali_(((Wilayah, wali))) as comrade-in-the-same-front, joined, on the same level, and I prefer it over "guardian" and "friend" and "helper" and these things that are customary. Because _wilayah_(((Wilayah))) means connectedness — two things that are bound and joined to each other: to this they say _wilayah_. God is the _wali_ of the believers, that is, He is joined with the believers. What does it mean that He is joined with the believers?
