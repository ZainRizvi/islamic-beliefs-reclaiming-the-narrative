== Session 14: The Philosophy of Prophethood

[.session-subtitle]
_فلسفۀ نبوت — delivered Wednesday, 10 Mehr 1353 / 15 Ramadan al-Mubarak 1394_

The discussion that we intend to pursue today from the Qur'an, God willing, is a discussion about prophethood. As you can see in these notes that are in your hands, and as you will see — or have already seen — the text, we have set before ourselves several topics in the field of prophethood, so that, relying upon and seeking help from the blessed verses of the Qur'an, we may, God willing, come to understand these several discussions. The brothers of course know that prophethood is one of the principles of all religions. If it can be called a principle among the principles of religion — indeed, one must say something higher than that. When I said "if it can be called a principle among the principles of religion," I do not mean by this that anyone would deny its being a principle. No; rather, it is something higher than a principle: religion has no meaning at all without belief in prophethood.@@CX(Prophethood | religion has no meaning without it)@@ Religion means that program, that path, that school, that creed which has come by way of a messenger from God the Exalted. So the coming of a messenger, and a coming from the side of God — this is among the essential elements of religion. The very subsistence of religion rests upon this. Therefore, in the field of prophethood, it is fitting that we discuss and speak about it as one of the important and fundamental matters of religion.

Of course, in the field of prophethood there is a series of discussions that are common and customary. Pick up any book that has been written about prophethood and look at it: you will see these discussions in it. Of all those discussions that are usually raised in the books about prophethood, we have taken up just one — only one of them — and that too with a particular taste, in this series of our discussions about prophethood. And that is precisely our discussion today, which is the subject of today's recitation. Otherwise, the rest of the discussions that are conducted in the field of prophethood, about prophethood, around prophethood, in the various books of theology (kalam), are in our view matters which — although in their own place they are correct points, indeed necessary points — being correct does not necessarily mean that I too must be compelled, at this time, under these circumstances, with these needs, to raise that correct point here. Many of the points scholars make are correct — and from here on, for a few words, what I am saying is a general teaching regarding all our intellectual and research work — we must see, among the correct things, which is necessary? Among the necessary things, which is more necessary? Among the more necessary, which is urgent? Among the urgent, which is pressing and vital? We should take up that first;@@CX(Leadership | prioritize the vital over the merely correct)@@ and once we are free of it, the later discussions, and so on in this order, we proceed this far, until at the end of the work we come to discussions that, although correct, their necessity and urgency are not felt to that degree.

Concerning the discussion of prophethood: it is true that the question of how much divine knowledge or human knowledge a prophet possesses is itself a discussion; or the question of knowing whether our Prophet — who did not write and did not read, as the speaking Qur'an states:

The Prophet neither wrote nor read. Was it that he did not write and did not read because he was unable to? That is, in the ordinary expression of today, was he entirely illiterate? Or rather, was he able, and yet did not write and did not read? Well, it is a discussion, after all. Or the question of which religion, which creed, among the religions and creeds of the world, the Prophet of Islam believed in and practiced before his prophethood and his Bi'thah — of course it is a discussion, but how necessary is this discussion for us? How necessary for us is the discussion before it? How necessary for us is the discussion before that one? It is not necessary at all.

Of course, when a person has learned everything related to prophethood and related to religion, there is no harm in his coming, at the end of the work, to understand which religion the Prophet adhered to before the Bi'thah. But we are still in the bend of a single alley.footnote:s14-attar["'Attar traversed the seven cities of love / We are still in the bend of a single alley" (Rumi).] Our society still does not know the meaning of prophethood, the meaning of the Bi'thah, the aim of the Bi'thah, the outcome and conclusion footnote:s14-conclusion[Conclusion, outcome.] of the Bi'thah, the path of the Bi'thah, and the watchword of prophethood; it does not even know that it is afflicted with this condition. The Muslim does not know the aim of the Muhammadan Bi'thah(((the Bi'thah))) — that is why his affairs are the way they are. If he knew the aim of his Prophet's Bi'thah, he would go after that aim.@@CX(the Bi'thah | Muslims' plight stems from not knowing its aim)@@ We are still in the first matters, the preliminary matters, the fundamental matters in the field of prophethood — and are we to grasp at and occupy ourselves with the secondary matters, those of the fourth and fifth degree? That is why, in our discussion of prophethood, we do not raise any of those discussions which the theologians have conducted and continue to conduct in their books. Here are you, and here are those books that the theologians wrote — go and read them. And we have no doubt about the necessity of those discussions, nor about their goodness; but again we have no doubt that their necessity is by far less than the necessity of many other discussions that ought to be raised today. That is why we do not discuss them.

The first matter that we discuss concerning prophethood is the philosophy of prophethood. Why must there be a prophet? Why must someone, from the side of the Lord, gird himself to guide humankind? Can man himself not do it? Are human knowledge and human thought not sufficient? Why a prophet? Why a messenger between the unseen (ghayb) and the witnessed (shuhud)? This is a question that we must know. If we do not know the philosophy of prophethood, the rest of the discussions about prophethood will be a series of discussions that are almost suspended in mid-air. So first we must know: prophethood for what? This is our first discussion today, in these notes that I wrote, which are in the gentlemen's hands; we have set this question down briefly, in a few sentences, very concisely, and the verses we recite today bear upon this matter.

Concerning the philosophy of prophethood we will not speak much; it is one word, and that one word is this: that the senses of man, the instincts of man, and the intellect of man fall short of guiding and taking the hand of man.@@CX(Prophethood | needed because senses, instinct, and intellect fall short)@@ One word. A series of creatures can be managed by their senses. Some animals we perhaps know to be of this sort, that they draw only upon their own senses — the outward senses. Most kinds of animals draw on their instincts in order to be guided; that is, the honeybee's instinct tells it: go and alight upon the flower, upon the fragrant flower, and suck from its perfume and its essence; then go to the house, the hive; build the houses in that six-cornered, hexagonal footnote:s14-hexagonal[(s-d-s) six-sided, hexagonal.] form; and there, with certain arrangements, enter the houses and exit; and have a queen and helpers footnote:s14-helpers[('-w-n) helpers, companions.] and guards — in short, a bee civilization. If you imagine that one day the bees sat down together and a great conference of the chiefs of the bees of the hives or regions was convened, and they said, "How is it better for us to build the house?" — one said octagonal, one said square, one said hexagonal; then they studied it and saw, no, the hexagonal is more suitable — if you imagine it happened this way, I must say you have made a mistake. No such situation occurred.

This is divine revelation (wahy)(((revelation, wahy))) to the bee. The divine revelation to the bee does not mean that the bee sits down and Gabriel(((Gabriel))) descends upon it; the bee's structure is like this, the bee's instinct is like this — there is an instinctual pull that draws the bee, compels it; it cannot do otherwise. Naturally and instinctually the bee is compelled, it is fashioned footnote:s14-fashioned[(f-t-r) created, made.] to build the house, to build it in this form, to find flower and plant from such-and-such a place and suck from them. If a bee were to deviate — if instead of flower and plant it were to smell and suck foul-smelling and rotten plants — they would behead it right there at the entrance of the hive. All this is their nature (fitrah)(((fitrah))) and their instinct.@@CX(fitrah | instinct suffices to guide the animal)@@

Instinct suffices for the bee. This gentleman Maurice Maeterlinck(((Maeterlinck, Maurice))) footnote:s14-maeterlinck[Maurice Maeterlinck (1862–1949 CE) was a Belgian poet, writer, and philosopher who won the Nobel Prize with his play _The Blue Bird_. He wrote essays entitled _The Life of the Bee_, _The Life of the Termite_, and _The Life of the Ant_.] sat for years and reflected on the life of bees and ants and termites; he sat and studied it. This very termite about which you complain so much — it has a certain arrangement inside those ceilings of yours; in your usurped house it has built an organized structure. You are not content for it to settle on the beam of your house and build a house there — it is usurped — but in this usurped house it does its work. It builds a house, in a particular shape, in a particular order. And the strange thing is this: that if you could — and if you had been able — to find one of the houses of the termites, or a hive of the honeybees, from the time of Noah's(((Noah))) flood, for example, or ten centuries before Noah's flood, and to place it beside one of the hives in one of the pleasant, fine-aired highlands of the Mashhad(((Mashhad))) of this present time, you would see that, to the breadth of a needle's point, this hive does not differ from that hive in its arrangements. Evolution, advancement, progress — these do not exist in the work of the honeybee, nor in the work of any other animal. What does exist is the revelation of nature, the revelation of the instincts, and the guidance of the quality of its creation and formation, which draws it, moves it, shows it the way, points out the obstacles to it, until it is able to do its own work. There is nothing other than instinct.

Man too uses instinct, but little. When you first came into the world, your state — with all due distinction — was the state of one of these very animals. It was by instinct, by the pull of nature (fitrah) and disposition, that you were able to find the store of your food in your mother's breast, and when they placed it in your mouth, to suck. No one had taught you to suck; you had not learned its practical, auditory, and visual instructions somewhere — this was your instinct. As you rose from the stage of childhood, gradually this tool, this eyeglass, this weapon whose name is instinct grew weak, became ineffective, became of little use; and something more effective, stronger, more powerful, by the name of the reason and intellect ('aql wa khirad)(((intellect, 'aql))) of man, took its place, and you became reasoning. You no longer work by instinct now; it is not instinct that tells you, "Sir, go and open your shop, or close it at such-and-such an hour, or answer the customer this way, or study the lesson this way, or teach in that manner" — these are not instinct, these are instructions that you took up from thought and from knowledge, learned, and found your way of life.

But does this intellect, this human thought, suffice to guide you and bring you to the destination of felicity? Is human reason adequate to guide him? Reason itself, if it is open, if it thinks, if it does not become bigoted, if it wishes to judge without ulterior motive, will say no — like a court that gives its own verdict that it has no jurisdiction over itself, a court that rules, "I have no competence to judge in this case." Sound human reason, free of ulterior motive, judges that "I am not competent to guide humanity independently." You want proof? We have two kinds of proof. One proof is that human reason is limited, not infinite, whereas the needs of human beings are infinite.@@CX(Intellect | finite reason cannot meet man's infinite needs)@@ How can a single intellect understand all the needs, so that, in the face of those needs, it might supply what would fill the place of those needs, or might enact a law? Human reason cannot do this work; it is too weak, too incapable, too inadequate to recognize all the pains and to provide the appropriate remedy for all of them.

The other proof is this: look at the historical and scientific realities. See, were the intellects able? Were intellects such as the intellect of Aristotle(((Aristotle)))footnote:s14-aristotle[Aristotle, titled the First Teacher, is one of the great philosophers of ancient Greece. He has been regarded as the founder of the science of logic.] and Plato(((Plato)))footnote:s14-plato[Plato, one of the great philosophers of Greece, was a student of Socrates. In a book named _The Republic_, he set forth the characteristics of an ideal society.] and Socrates(((Socrates)))footnote:s14-socrates[Socrates was one of the great philosophers of Greece, whom the court of Athens condemned to death on the charge of not believing in the gods of the temple and of propagating his views.] able to manage humankind?@@CX(Intellect | even the great philosophers could not guide humankind)@@ Plato the thinker, after he sits and thinks, consults, studies, researches, sketches an ideal city (a utopia) — this ideal city is good only for the mind and the inner strongbox of the honorable Plato himself, for the reason that for a single moment, in the world, this ideal city was never put into practice. And if you look today at Plato's ideal city, with the arrangements of the world of the present time, it appears to you as something unacceptable and ridiculous.

Look at the rational and philosophical schools, how they array themselves one against the other. See that humanity, unless it is connected to a source and a point higher, loftier, and deeper than human reason, cannot reach the way to guidance and felicity. This is the meaning of prophethood: man needs a force higher, a guidance stronger and deeper than the guidance of the senses, than the guidance of instinct, than the guidance of reason. This guidance comes — and what does it do? Does it come to compete with your senses? Does it come to oppose your instinct? Does it come to dash the head of reason against a stone? Never. It comes to guide reason, to nurture reason, to bring the buried reason out from beneath the tons of dust.@@CX(Prophethood | guidance nurtures reason rather than opposing it)@@

Our Commander of the Faithful, may God's blessings and peace be upon him — I misspoke — the Commander of the Faithful of all humanity (let us not confine the Commander of the Faithful to ourselves) — he is the teacher of humanity, the Commander of the Faithful is the great one of humanity — he says that God sent the prophets, according to what is in the Nahj al-Balaghah(((Nahj al-Balaghah))), so that they might urge human beings to remain faithful to the covenant and pledge of their nature (fitrah), and might bring to their remembrance the forgotten blessings, and then "and they might unearth for them the buried treasures of the intellects" (wa yuthiru lahum dafa'in al-'uqul).footnote:s14-hadith11[Hadith no. 11.] The intellects, the reasons, the understandings, the consciousnesses of the human societies which had been buried by the Pharaohs(((Pharaoh))) and the Nimrods(((Nimrod))) and the great ones and the powerful — the prophets come to bring out these buried treasures, to stir up these buried intellects.@@CX(Prophethood | prophets unearth the buried treasures of the intellects)@@ Pharaoh does not like the people to possess intellect; he does not like human beings to understand — because if they understand, his existence will become void and a fable, which we will see explained in the later discussions of prophethood, God willing.@@CX(Taghut | the tyrant suppresses the people's intellect to survive)@@ Pharaoh does not like the people to employ the force of intellect; because this is so, he buries the force of intellect. How he buries it is a matter for the later discussions, God willing. The prophets come to extract these buried treasures, these hoards, these hidden and concealed treasures; they refine them, and they place them in the people's hands.

So the prophets, with the force of revelation that they have, do not go to war against reason. The one who imagines that religion is at variance with reason either does not know religion or has no reason.@@CX(Faith | religion has no variance with sound reason)@@ Otherwise, one who possesses reason, one who has tested and tried reason and also knows religion, knows well that religion cannot, and does not, have any variance whatsoever with human knowledge and human reason — none at all. What religion says, sound intellects understand and approve. Those ignorant people who, in the name of defending religion, sometimes say, "Sir, you must not seek the rationale of religion; you must not seek argumentation in religion; you must not seek philosophy in the matter of religion" — they imagine that this saying (seeking philosophy, asking for philosophy) diminishes the worth of religion. They must know that it is not so. Such a thing is not the case. Sound religion, when it is presented before the complete intellect, has no variance and no conflict with it whatsoever. Today the great intellects of humanity understand the tawhid(((Tawhid))) of religion, the prophethood of religion, the prayer of religion, the fasting of religion, the zakat(((zakat))) of religion, the secondary rulings of religion.

When the human intellect and the experience of human knowledge recognizes alcohol, knows the harms of alcohol, understands its blow and injury to the body, to the nerves, to the spirit, and to the general condition of society — why should I not be able and not have the courage to recite the verse of the Qur'an with power?

That which you have heard that Imam al-Sajjad(((Imam al-Sajjad))), may God's blessings and peace be upon him, said — "The religion of God is not attained by the intellects" (din Allah la yusabu bi-l-'uqul)footnote:s14-hadith30[Hadith no. 30.] — its meaning is something else. That is, the divine creed cannot be discovered by reason. What does "cannot be discovered" mean? It means that if you have no narration (riwayah) telling you that the noon prayer is four cycles, you cannot understand by reason that the noon prayer is four cycles — and this is a very correct point too. If the Qur'an itself does not state the time of prayer:

If the Qur'an does not state this, you cannot understand by way of reason and thought and ordinary intellect when the time of prayer is. The Qur'an must state this; the hadith must state this; revelation must state this to us. This is what Imam al-Sajjad means; the outward of his statement too is just this — "The religion of God is not attained by the intellects." Its meaning is not that we cannot see the rulings of religion and the teachings of religion with the vision of reason and with the eyeglass of reason and human intellect — such that a group of ignorant people, in the name of defending religion, with fervor, with heat, cry out, "Sir, do not set forth a philosophy for religion!" Why should we not? Why should we not set it forth? Of course we always say, we repeat, we confess, that what we understand is not a thousandth of the deep teachings of religion; what we can apply and explain is far less than what may in reality be done and exist — there is no doubt in this. But I say one word, the next person says one word, the person after him says one word. Along the lengthwise line of human history, when you reckon, after two hundred years, five hundred years, you see that humanity, humankind, is far more believing, more confessing, more acknowledging of the depth of religion than in the age when these thoughts and these reflections and these applications had not yet come to it.

Therefore religion, when it comes, does not come to crush reason, does not come to abrogate footnote:s14-abrogate[(n-s-kh) to render void, to abrogate.] reason and drive reason out of life. So for what does religion come? It comes to guide reason, to take the hand of reason. Reason exists, but when caprice is beside it, it cannot judge correctly. Reason exists, but when greed is shoulder to shoulder with it, it cannot see correctly. When ulterior motive is beside it, it cannot understand correctly. Religion comes to take away from reason the caprices, the whims, the greeds, the fears, the ulterior motives; it strengthens the sound, complete reason, confirms it, so that it may understand well. And when you turn to Islam, you see that Islam, from beginning to end, is full of the manifestations of reason. In the Qur'an how much we have: "Will you not reason?" (a-fa-la ta'qilun); how much we have: "Will you not reflect?" (a-fa-la tatafakkarun); how much we have — so that you may understand, so that you may know — how much we have: "Indeed in that are signs for those endowed with understanding" (inna fi dhalika la-ayatin li-uli l-albab) — for the people of reason. In the narrations how much we have: "Indeed God has two proofs over the people" (inna li-Llahi 'ala l-nasi hujjatayn)footnote:s14-hadith42[Hadith no. 42.] — God has two proofs over the people: one the prophet and one the reason.@@CX(Prophethood | God's two proofs over mankind: the prophet and reason)@@ And again reason. About reason we will not speak more than this now.

In sum, keep this word in mind: the summary of the discussion comes to this, that man, without the guidance of revelation, without revelation coming to him and coming to his aid, cannot bring himself to the destination of felicity; and when revelation comes, it does not suppress reason, just as it does not suppress instinct, just as it does not destroy the outward senses. No; it comes to strengthen the outward senses, the human and mortal instincts, and the force of the intellect and thought of man; it refines them, purifies them, takes their hand, teaches them. This is the task of revelation. Therefore the philosophy of prophethood is this.

Because it is so — because we are deficient, because human knowledge and insight are not sufficient for our guidance — a hand must come out from the unseen and guide us. The place for a hand to come out from the unseen is here, not there where, when I have gone a little hungry, I do not go after a morsel of bread but wait for a hand to come out from the unseen. No — it is not for the place where, when I am confronted with a sin, I content myself with regret and sorrow and grief and wait for a hand to come out from the unseen for me; no — it is not for the place where I do not perform my divine duty, do not enjoin good, do not forbid evil, do not tread the path of God, and wait for a hand to come out from the unseen and do something footnote:s14-hafez["The city is empty of lovers; perhaps from some quarter / A hand may come out from the unseen and do something" (Hafiz).] — contrary to the verse of the Qur'an:

وَكُنَّا نُكَذِّبُ بِيَوۡمِ ٱلدِّينِ

"And we used to deny the Day of Judgment."footnote:s14-muddaththir2[Surat al-Muddaththir, the Blessed Surah / verse 46.]

[.verse-cite]
— Surat al-Muddaththir (74:46)

The verse of the Qur'an counts among the deniers of religion those who do not incite the feeding of the destitute. They do not stir up and rouse the people to feeding the destitute and — with a broader, deeper view, and an interpretation closer to the very text of Islam — to uprooting poverty and hunger. That person who does not conform himself to this verse takes not a single step on the path of eliminating hungers, advances not a single pace on the path of destroying the root of poverty, and sits waiting for a hand to come out from the unseen and do something. Such people must know that if a hand were to come out from the unseen, it would first strike upon their own brains, to throw these spoiled and void existences away, to bring them down. There is such a thing as a hand coming out from the unseen and doing something — but this is the point I made: it is necessary, for the guidance of man, that a hand should come from the unseen, and that is the hand of the prophet, the hand of prophethood; that is the skill of the messengership which comes and guides human beings, in just the manner we have stated: to rouse the force of reason within them.

Then several other matters arise here, several other matters are present here, of which I have briefly written down a list of some.footnote:s14-polycopy[Please refer to the mimeographed handout at the end of the session.]

One is this: what concept does prophethood have? What is its relation to the Bi'thah and being raised up (bar-angikhtagi)? We say the prophet was sent (mab'uth), was raised up; what does "raised up" mean? Is there a stirring (angizish) in prophethood? Is there a movement? This too is a discussion. In the discussions related to prophethood we do not encounter this discussion.

What is the starting point of the work of the prophets? From where do they begin reform and aid to human beings? How is the conclusion of their endeavor? Where do their works arrive? Just this — that they laid him against a tree and sawed him, and it was finished and over?footnote:s14-zakariyya[An allusion to the tradition of the prophet Zakariyya, who, according to narrations, was sawed in half inside a tree where he had taken refuge.] They cut off the head of Yahya(((Yahya))) before that tyrant (taghi)(((Taghut))), and it was finished and over? Is the end of the work of prophethood just this? Or no — another conclusion, another ending for this beginning is conceived and envisaged, and it is alluded to in the Qur'an, and there are matters which we will of course raise gradually, one by one, in the coming days, and set forth. Concerning the philosophy of prophethood, just this much that I have stated is, in my own view, sufficient. Of course, this was the briefest expression that could be set forth in this field. (You dear brothers listened well to this, God willing — for I see that you listen well — God willing the matter has been well grasped too. If anything in this field has not become clear to someone, here or there, he can ask me, or ask his neighbor at his side, so that this matter may become clear: what fundamentally is the philosophy of prophethood?)

كَانَ ٱلنَّاسُ أُمَّةٗ وَٰحِدَةٗ

"Mankind was one community (ummah)."footnote:s14-baqarah[Surat al-Baqarah, the Blessed Surah / verse 213.]

[.verse-cite]
— Surat al-Baqarah (2:213)
****

The people were all one ummah(((Ummah))) and one group. About this the commentators have an extensive discussion among themselves. Were they one ummah — a good ummah? Or were they one ummah — a bad ummah? On either supposition, the parties to the dispute raise an objection. Some say that "Mankind was one community" alludes to the primitive and initial communalism (ishtirakiyyat)(((communalism, ishtirakiyyat))) of the prehistoric eras. I say that there is no evidence for this meaning, except that the name of the communalist and primitive era is on people's tongues, and we feel it a pity that our Qur'an too should have no share of that cloak footnote:s14-proverb[It is a proverb, alluding to having a benefit and share of something.] — only that. Otherwise, "Mankind was one community" alludes to an era when human beings lived with the primitive and original communalism, as the gentlemen say, like — let me put it this way — animals, naked and bare and in herds, going about in the deserts and performing feats of strength, scraping stone with their claws and catching animals, eating them, and sometimes eating one another too. Should we take such a being, or ourselves, to be the referent in this verse "Mankind was one community"? No, no! We never attempt to attach something to our Qur'an from elsewhere. No; what we understand from our Qur'an, that is enough for us. If we understand it, we have done a great feat. Our Qur'an has no need that we should give it some marginal note or appendix from the sayings of others and the cultures of others, and exalt it thereby. No; the Qur'an does not want this kind of exaltation.

"Mankind was one community" can be given several meanings. Two of its meanings are present in _In the Light of the Qur'an_, which you will now consult and observe; and one meaning occurs to me that is other than those two meanings that I alluded to in this translation. "Mankind was one community" means that human beings and people were all in an equal and identical state, in respect of needs and in respect of capacities. All human beings were of one kind of need, and all human beings had one measure of capacity — and the social reality is always like this.@@CX(social class | mankind originally one ummah, equal in needs and capacities)@@ All human beings have reason, have thought, have intelligence, have a sixth sense, have outward senses, have inward senses. All human beings have hunger, have thirst, have sexual desire, want a house, want clothing, and the like. The needs are all equal, almost of one kind, and the capacities are similar. If one human being has been raised in a better upbringing, his capacity having blossomed, that is another matter. If some young nobleman has been born in an aristocratic house, with a private tutor at home teaching him at six or seven years of age to speak Indian and Chinese like a nightingale, while the child of some laborer in the kilns does not know even Persian properly at seven or eight years of age — that is another matter. This is no proof that this one's capacity was greater than that one's; no, this one's capacity was extracted, and the capacity of that poor one remained and was not extracted.@@CX(mustad'afin | inequality reflects unextracted capacity, not lesser worth)@@ The oil deposits that exist in regions — let us suppose northern, southern, in such-and-such a country — it is not certain that they are richer and more abundant than the oil deposits of some other point that has not been extracted. The oil of this one has been extracted; it comes into the lamps, its gasoline comes into the automobiles; everyone knows it too, its name is famous too. The oil of that one, poor thing, has not yet been extracted — it may even be richer.

So human beings, naturally and ordinarily, were one community (ummah wahidah), with identical needs, with equal and similar capacities, which we have specified in the translation.

فَبَعَثَ ٱللَّهُ ٱلنَّبِيِّـۧنَ

"So God sent the prophets" (fa-ba'atha Llahu l-nabiyyin).

[.verse-cite]
— Surat al-Baqarah (2:213)
****

Among these human beings of equal capacities, God the Exalted raised up the prophets. Among these of the same level, the Lord of the worlds singled out one human being — higher, stronger, deeper, more ardent, and of greater capacity — and raised him up. To do what? "as bearers of glad tidings" (mubashshirin) — bringers of good news — "and warners" (wa mundhirin) — and givers of warning. Of what do the prophets give glad tidings? They give the glad tidings of Paradise, they give the glad tidings of the felicity of this world, they give the glad tidings of the ideal city, they give the glad tidings of the establishment of security and peace and welfare, they give the glad tidings of the disappearance of poverty and despair and fear and insecurity and ignorance; they are bringers of glad tidings; and at last they give human beings the glad tidings of the establishment of a virtuous government and a righteous city, and after that the glad tidings of going to Paradise and connection to the good pleasure (ridwan) of the Lord — they give glad tidings. And they are warners; they frighten — they frighten of the fire of Hell too, they frighten of the narrowness of the bridge of Sirat too, they frighten of the wretchedness of this world too, they frighten of the domination of the demon footnote:s14-demon[An ugly and frightful being; in common parlance applied to satans and jinn.] of ignorance and poverty too, they frighten of the fall into the valley and the slope of corruption too, they frighten of the destruction of the human capacities too; they are bringers of warning, they are warners.

It is not all just that they should say "People, fear" and "People, glad tidings be to you." What do you have with you? A book from the side of the Lord. He sent down with them a book by the truth, in accordance with the truth. I have explained "the truth" (haqq) several times (now I can no longer explain it; I think that if I wished to explain "the truth" again, I would have to speak about that point alone for some ten or twelve minutes). In sum, that which conforms with the nature (fitrah) of the world, that which accompanies the natural course of the world — that is the truth. That which conforms with the nature of man and the disposition of the universe — that they call the truth. The book of the prophets too is the truth; it drives man forward on his natural path, on his innate course, on his ordinary bed and evolutionary course; it advances him, aids him. The book of the prophets carries the truth with it.

Until that book — "that it might judge" (li-yahkuma); that is, until the book judges among the people, in those things about which they differ with one another. After all, people are not free of difference; differences among human beings are a custom (sunnah), and their existence is necessary, and their absence is harmful. Know this: the existence of difference is good, for the reason that difference is a cause of evolution. In the matter of differences — of course this thing I said, that its absence is harmful, in this word I must reconsider; I do not know whether it is harmful or not. At any rate, I must elaborate and interpret. Its existence at any rate is beneficial; "its absence is harmful," I do not know — I take that statement back. The book comes to judge among the people, to render judgment, to rule, concerning the things about which they have differed. What do we understand here? We understand that the government which the prophets bring into being is not the government of the individual prophet, the government of a person, the government of despotism; rather, it is the government of law, the government of the Book.@@CX(Wilayah | the prophets establish the rule of law and the Book, not despotism)@@ When the prophet comes, he makes a society in which the ruler in that society, in meaning and in reality, is that very book — that is, the law.

"that it might judge among the people concerning that wherein they differed. And none differed concerning it except those who were given it" (wa ma ikhtalafa fihi illa lladhina utuhu).

[.verse-cite]
— Surat al-Baqarah (2:213)
****

Concerning this book no difference arose; they did not differ — "except those who were given it," except those people to whom the book had been given, those people for whom this very heavenly book came; they themselves differed concerning the heavenly book. What does this show us? It shows the existence of distortion (tahrif)(((tahrif))) in the heavenly religions, among the sayings of the prophets. When the prophets came, they brought the book, the law, the school; then those very people to whom this book and law and school had been given differed about it. They differed — meaning what? Meaning that one type spoke the truth, and one type spoke contrary to fact. So therefore there are those who are followers of a religion and who speak the word of religion, but speak contrary to fact — an allusion to the existence of abrogation (naskh)(((naskh))) and distortion (tahrif) in the religions.
