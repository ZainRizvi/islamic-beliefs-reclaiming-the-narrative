Dear brothers, with your attention please. We said that if we take Tawhid to be a belief that gives rise to action and that imposes commitment, then inevitably this belief lays upon our shoulders a whole series of obligations and commitments. And we said that we must understand these obligations and commitments. Of course, these commitments are not confined to the individual life of human beings; rather, the greater weight of these commitments falls upon social life, upon the social order and the form of society. That is, when Tawhid enters a society, the first thing it does is to construct that society in a form that befits this belief.@@CX(Tawhid | first shapes society, then the individual)@@ Once that work is done, only then does the turn come to ask what duties a person who professes Tawhid has as an individual.

In any case, these commitments must be recognized and known. We have given the name "the resolution of Tawhid" to the whole set of these commitments. We said that Tawhid hands us a kind of resolution — it places into our hands a set of contracts and obligations. In order that we may, God willing, live as believers in divine unity, it is necessary that we treat these commitments as active and binding. The first of these commitments was this: that servitude and obedience belong exclusively to God; this is the first commitment of Tawhid.@@CX(servitude | servitude and obedience belong exclusively to God)@@ We explained this point in the course of one or two — or two or three — recitations.

Today we come to the second commitment that Tawhid places upon the shoulders of the believer in unity — upon the monotheistic believer, the monotheistic society, the monotheistic world. This can be designated under the heading that we have written here: Tawhid and the negation of social classes.footnote:s12-2[Refer to the polycopy (handout) at the end of the session.] (In these written notes you have in hand, it is good that you put the number "2" at the top of the page. Because on that note concerning exclusive servitude and obedience, the number "1" had been written; here too you should put a "2" so that the sequence of the topics is clear. We will, God willing, present the third one later, and the fourth, and if there are more, then more.)

The negation of social classes — and in a brief phrase, whose details we shall present later, the monotheistic society, according to the terminology now familiar to your ears, is a classless society. It is a society in which the various groups of human beings are not separated from one another with respect to rights and privileges. All human beings live under a single roof of rights. Everyone lives and moves along a single course, with one kind of opportunity and one kind of rights. This is the society that Tawhid sets before the eye of our mind and our imagination, with regard to social stratification.@@CX(Tawhid's effects | the monotheistic society is a classless society)@@

When we turn back to history, we see that class division is among the chronic afflictions of history in all societies. Not only in the backward tribal societies, not only in the societies of lands remote from civilization, but in those very countries and lands that are the mother of human civilization and the cradle of human civilization — there, in those very places, class division shows itself to us from between the pages of history in its ugliest face and most repugnant form. Truly, among the great injustices of history and the great stains of shame upon human history is this very thing: class division.

What does "class division" mean? It means that the human beings who live in this society are not all alike. One group is condemned to suffer deprivation, to bear hardship, to serve the other groups — and must not even voice any complaint about this deprivation and hardship. Another group, by contrast, is to be well-provided, to be favored; the pleasure and enjoyment of life is to be theirs, they are able to make use of all the privileges, and there is to be no problem in this.

For example, in India(((India))) — and as an example, the best place one can cite is India. You know that India is the cradle of Aryan civilization, and the Aryan race(((Aryan race))), from the very beginning of urban settlement, from the very beginning when it formed a nation and an organized community, was in this very India. They say the Aryan race came from the northern lands and from Siberia(((Siberia))), and when they entered, they split into two branches: one group went toward India, the other toward Iran(((Iran))). And the people who were in India reached civilization sooner than those who came into Iran and settled into their lives.

India is the cradle of human civilization, or one of the cradles of human civilization, and there class division is in full uproar. They held that there were four principal classes — one class beneath the others, and hundreds of sub-classes in between these four principal ones. The details of this you can search for in the books concerning the history of religions — those who have a taste for it and an interest, let them search, and let them reflect a little, with deliberation and thought, on Tawhid and the Qur'an, and make the comparison.

They divided the people into four classes. The first class was the Brahmins(((Brahmins))), the religious and clerical men, who were the highest of the social classes. The second class was the warriors and the princes; they were in the second class. Of course, between these two classes there were abundant disputes and conflicts. Formerly the princes were the upper class and the clerics were the second class; later, owing to certain factors that arose and the struggles that took place, the clerics and Brahmins managed to appropriate to themselves the scholarly and upper class, and to place the warriors and soldiers and princes and chieftains in the second class.

Passing beyond the second class, there was the class of farmers and laborers, the craftsmen. These two upper classes did no work for society. What did the Brahmin do, except to recite a few incantations and litanies and wave his hands about? What did that chieftain and prince do, except to arrange for himself a little more land, a little more wealth, and a somewhat larger and broader domain of lordship? But the craftsmen and the farmers — they performed all the work of this vast land; they were the third class. The fourth class was the common people. The commonality, that is, ordinary folk — no doubt tradesmen, those engaged in society in small and manual tasks. Beneath these four classes there was yet another class, which they said was not of the Aryan race at all. These four classes were all of the Aryan race; the others they said were outside the Aryan race, and therefore were impure — the class of untouchables.(((untouchables)))

Of course, what I am now describing pertains to history — but a history that extended onward and onward and onward, right up to the twentieth century. In India, what I have just described to you remained in full force and full authority until a few years ago. Perhaps the gentlemen who have studied the affairs of India know that the class of untouchables and the defiled was abolished and eliminated by Gandhi,(((Gandhi))) the late leader of India, who said: "What is this 'untouchable'?!" The former prime minister of India himself — that is, Nehru(((Nehru, Jawaharlal)))footnote:s12-3[Jawaharlal Nehru (1889–1964) was among the leaders of the struggle for India's independence movement. After India's independence he was elected prime minister. His daughter, Indira Gandhi, was elected prime minister of India some time after her father.] — and now his daughterfootnote:s12-4["Daughter."] is the head of state — they themselves are among the Brahmins. Of course, since he was anti-religious and irreligious, he disliked being called "Brahmin" and "Pandit" — the special title of the Brahmins.

In any case, I mean to say that these are not really things belonging only to ancient history. Even after some fourteen centuries — or thirteen-and-some centuries — had passed since the rising of the sun of Qur'anic Tawhid, still in India, in that place which is the birthplace of human civilization, this class division existed.

Well, now my aim is to expound class division. They had four classes, and as we said, in between the classes — according to the account of John Noss,(((Noss, John)))footnote:s12-5[_Man's Religions_ (_A History of the World's Religions_), written by John Noss, in which he examines the rites and religions of the ancient world up to the present age in four parts: the primitive and past religions, the religions of India, the religions of the Far East, and the religions of the Near East.] who wrote a history of religions — there were tens, indeed hundreds, of subsidiary classes. And then, two persons from two classes had no right to marry one another, to speak with one another, to shake hands with one another, to sit side by side, to walk together, to be friends with one another — _wa qis ʿalayhi, faʿallala wa tafaʿʿala_ (and so on and so forth).footnote:s12-6[And such-and-such activities of this kind — _qis ʿalā hādhā_ ("measure [the rest] by this").] They were utterly separated from one another by a wall of steel.

Why should they be separated? What does the Brahmin have that I, the farmer, do not have? Or that I, the ordinary tradesman, do not have? Or what natural privilege does some great warrior enjoy that I, the ordinary commoner, do not enjoy? If this question were asked, do you know what they would say in answer? They would say: "Mind your own business!" Because in the very root of creation, in the very root of your coming-into-being, you were created as two groups, of two kinds. It has nothing to do with law and contract and the convention of Zayd and ʿAmr — you were created of two kinds. Because when Brahma,(((Brahma))) the great god, wished to bring forth creatures, when he wished to fashion the human being, he created the Brahmins from his own head, the chieftains and soldiers and princes from his own hands, the farmers from his own arms, and the people of the laboring class from his own feet; and the impure classes are not of Brahma at all, are not of that pure root, are not of this race.@@CX(shirk | belief in many gods justifies dividing humanity into classes)@@

When the fourth class was created from Brahma's foot, what right does it have to consider itself the equal and peer of the Brahmin, who was created from Brahma's head? You are not at all equal in creation; one group was created from a nobler origin, they are worthy of greater respect, their endowment is natural, their being-favored is innate; and the deprivation of the lower classes is likewise innate. This was the logic.

Do you imagine that in such a society it is at all possible for class division ever to be overthrown? Is such a thing possible? Why would it be possible? Who do you imagine could possibly abolish class division in a class-divided society? Is it the favored class? Does the well-provided class come forward and say, "For God's sake, I have relinquished my rights"? That is a vain fancy. Surely, if class division is to be overthrown in a society, it is the deprived class that must demand its own right, must claim the prosperity that belongs to it, and must protest against the excessive privileges of the upper classes — the lower class must protest. But the lower class, in such a society as I have depicted, is impossible to make protest. Why? Because it believes that they were created this way by their very essence, that the natural state is just so; it is not at all subject to change or alteration, nothing can be done about it — they were made this way, they were created this way.@@CX(social class | belief in innate hierarchy disarms the oppressed from protest)@@

When a person has believed that nature and innate disposition and temperament and the divine creation within him have created him in a particular form, it is impossible for him to expect that any form other than that should be hoped for him, or that he should enjoy privileges and rights that are not consistent with the structure of his constitution and his nature. He says: "This is my lot. The threadbare carpet of one whose fortune was woven black cannot be set right. From the first we were woven in black cloth, from the first we were spun of an inferior sort; nothing more can be done."footnote:s12-7["To the ear of the soul, a wayfarer brought a cry / from the Presence of the One: 'There is no god but God' — / that, O dear one, whoever is allotted abasement, / in truth attains not rank and station by force. / The threadbare carpet of one whose fortune was woven black / cannot be made white by the water of Zamzam and Kawthar." (Hafez)] Take some black object to the edge of a stream, to the shore of an ocean, and begin to wash it and wash it — when it is black by its very essence, when from the beginning its natural color has been black, is it possible for this color of it to disappear? Rather, its dust and dirt will be cleaned off, its blackness will become clearer, it will turn even blacker. Therefore, throughout history — as you have observed — in these societies, this great injustice existed, and there was no means of remedying it.

Of course, reformers would appear and change people's thinking. Pay attention to this point I am about to make — I will not state this meaning explicitly, but this is a sign of something peculiar to the religions. Reformers would come; first they changed the thought, first they transformed the culture, first they said that this philosophy is wrong, and on the basis of the wrongness of that philosophy they convinced the people that this state of affairs is tyrannical. The correct understanding of historical transformations is this. It is not correct for us to say that first the situation changed, that first the social form was transformed and then the philosophies and ideas were transformed — no. Reformers would come. This is our history, the history of the Orient.(((Orient))) That is to say, those who thought that way and assumed that way had, in our view, not read the history of the Orient — they had seen the history of Europe.(((Europe))) This is India, this is China,(((China))) this is Iran, this is Egypt.(((Egypt))) The reformers who arose, the religious reformers, first changed the ideas, transformed the philosophies; and then, once the ideas had changed and the philosophies had changed, only then did the ground become ready for the social condition and the corrupt social order to be transformed as well.@@CX(social movement | begins by changing ideas, not first the social order)@@ At any rate, throughout history it was this way.

You observed: here it was India; Iran too was like this, Egypt too was like this, China too was like this. Wherever you have any trace of a civilization, the situation was on this same pattern. What does that mean? It means it was said: since human beings have been created of two kinds, since they have been created from two natures and two dispositions and two temperaments — or more — therefore their social rights too must not be equal with one another. It is necessary that they not be alike; and if anyone said they ought to be alike, he has spoken wrongly. This is the way they used to put it.

Islam came and proclaimed that there are no "gods" — many gods are false; there is [one] God. Those who imagined that two gods had created two groups of human beings, with two kinds of distinctions and characteristics, have erred. There is one God, and He created the whole of the cosmos as well; the governance of the world and of its inhabitants is entirely in the grip of His power, and He created all alike. He fashioned all from one root, from one disposition, from one nature, from one material root and source.@@CX(Tawhid | one God created all humanity from a single root)@@ The verses of the Qur'an on this theme are abundant:

This is an Islamic teaching; in the shade of Tawhid it was taught to human beings, was established, was set down as a matter of record, that in the very fabric of society there is no class division. The society that Islam brings into being is not group-by-group, is not class-by-class; it is impossible for some people to enjoy rights that others do not enjoy. In that society it is not said that people were created from two roots, two sources, two stems, or more. In that society it is not said that one person was created from clay and that other one — let us suppose — was created from light. From the material standpoint, the material origin of all is one and the same thing.

Therefore Tawhid — in the sense of holding that there is one God, of regarding the governance and the creation and the bringing-into-being and the administration of the world as proceeding from one God — is the guarantor of the negation of social classes.@@CX(Tawhid's effects | destroys divisions based on class)@@

Of course, there is a point here, and it is this: that social classes have not always been in the form I described, that I expounded, regarding India. It is not the case that everywhere there is a philosophy of that sort. It may well be that in certain societies, among certain communities and nations, from the standpoint of the philosophy and the claims and the outlooks that are put forward among the people, everyone stands on a single level — yet in practice class division exists, just as it exists in our world today.

Today, look at the world: especially in the capitalist world,(((capitalism))) especially in those countries that have risen to a greater height in terms of capitalism, class division is conspicuously visible. Of course, there they have never said that the laborers and the employers were created from two origins and two roots of creation and formation — no. They do not say that the gentleman who owns some great company — for example, a certain cartel — and the laborer of some mine are of two stems, of two roots — no. But the practice that they carry out together, the regulations they lay down for these two groups of people, the mode of conduct they establish in society between these two kinds of human being, differs little from those people who believed that these were created from two roots and two origins and two stems. For one group, infinite opportunities; for the larger group, zero opportunities. One group exploits the wealth of the whole world to its own benefit; another group is not even permitted to eat alone the fruit of its own toil.

Class division in this sense too holds true, and this exists in the world today. Indeed, I want to say even more: this class division is more base and more insidious than the class division of that earlier day.@@CX(social class | modern hidden class division is more insidious than the openly declared kind)@@ Those people said outright: "Yes, we are different from one another." These people say: "We are brothers with one another, we are all on a single scale, we are indeed the protectors of your rights" — yet in practice we see that it is not so; in practice those same distinctions exist. Sometimes, in the matter of administering legal regulations, certain equalities and parities are observed — that So-and-so likewise committed such-and-such a sin and was subjected to such-and-such a penalty, even though he is of the upper class. But again, when we look, we see — no, the root of the matter is something else. We see that again those same class divisions, again those same social distinctions, remain in full force. In reality, the favors are confined to one particular group, and a majority are deprived; and the first thing of which they are deprived is a sound understanding and perception and full and correct development.

Islam has negated all of this. In summary, what I have drawn from the verses of the Qur'an, in the whole collection of this precious Book, on the theme of the negation of social classes — when I gave thought to these, I saw that there is a great deal of it, and I have written down a few here. The Creator and the Worshipped One and the Director of the affairs of all is God. This is an Islamic statement. The Creator of all is one — He is God. You may say, "What difference does it make?" It makes a great deal of difference. If we hold that the Creator is two — not the scholastic debate, which I do not wish to enter into, a hypothetical one — then that society and that philosophy of two-gods, its being of two gods, the first effect it produces is this: that two groups come into being in society. When we are believers in one God, its meaning is that the people of society are all of one rank, of one group, of one class, brothers and side by side with one another.

I am concerned with the implication of this statement; I am not concerned with the implications of that obsolete, ancient philosophy. I want you to take note of what it means when we believe that God is one. God is one and is not two — that is, the servants of God are on a single scale, they are not on two scales. Among the meanings of "God is one and is not two" is this one — not that this is its entire meaning. That is, the servants of God are not in two classes; they are in one class. Why? Because their God is one, their Creator is one.
