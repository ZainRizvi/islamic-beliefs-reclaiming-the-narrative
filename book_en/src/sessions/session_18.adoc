Our purpose in today's discussion is to set forth this point: what was the first melody of the prophets' call? In the light of the discussions of these past few days, for those who have paid attention and given thought, it has become clear to us what the prophets are about in this world, for what purpose they have come, how they continue their work, and by what road they secure their aim. These matters should have become clear in the course of the past discussions; and if you find that — God forbid — they have not become clear, you must refer once more to those written notes; and if even then there remained some ambiguity and it was not made clear, then the fault is mine for having written the notes either deficiently or obscurely. Otherwise, the following points ought to emerge from these notes: first, for what work the prophets come, and what is the benefit of that work for which they come, and how they secure that work for which they come. These were discussions that have already passed.

Today's discussion is this: the work that God's mighty prophets wish to accomplish — that is, the founding of a government, a society, and a monotheistic order, the overthrow of the order of ignorance and polytheism, and the bringing about of a tremendous resurrection within the fabric of society — these tasks, which are the aims of the prophets (peace be upon them), where do they begin them? The question of the point of beginning is an exceedingly important question. In all the activities that present themselves to a human being, or to a group, or to a society — that we should perform such-and-such an act, implement such-and-such a plan, carry out such-and-such a program; sometimes this matter arises for a group, sometimes for a handful of human beings attached to a certain idea or a certain creed, sometimes for a nation, and sometimes for an individual — among these ideas and important undertakings that arise, this question, too, is one of the most important: from where shall we begin this work? The point of beginning is exceedingly important.

If the point of beginning is chosen correctly and aptly, there is much hope for this work, this plan, and this program reaching its result; but if the point of beginning is chosen badly, it is not that the load will never reach its destination and the work will not be set in order — no; it is possible that it will, it is possible that the load will reach its destination, but it will arrive with difficulty.@@CX(Leadership | choosing the right point of beginning underwrites success)@@ The point of beginning is most important in this respect: that to a large extent, with a high percentage, it underwrites footnote:s18-1[From the root (k-f-l): guarantor, surety, one who pledges.] and guarantees the success of the work that is to begin from this point.

In the case of the prophets' work, first, the appraisal of their work requires that we know their point of beginning. For a complete knowledge of the prophets' work to come into our hands, how good it is that we should know this matter too — namely, from where they used to begin. Beyond this, it is instructive for us. If we have seen that the prophets all acted in a particular style and by a defined method, this can be a lesson for us, by virtue of our being followers of the prophets and successors of their schools and their way. From several standpoints, therefore, it is beneficial that we pursue this question.

God's mighty prophets — as we wrote for you in this paper footnote:s18-2[Refer to the mimeographed handout [polycopy] at the end of the session.] — their point of beginning consisted of the declaration of the kernel, the marrow, the foundation, and the spirit of their school. At the outset of the social and doctrinal revolution and resurrection, the prophets never flattered footnote:s18-3[From the root (j-m-l): to flatter, to use smooth and oily speech.] the people. It was never the case that for a period of time they kept the people confused with some other talk, with some other slogans, and that after attaining some measure of success they would then put forward the real slogan — no. From the very first, with sincerity, with uprightness and truthfulness, they declared their real and ultimate aim. And what was that? That aim consisted of Tawhid.@@CX(Prophethood | declare their ultimate aim of Tawhid from the very first)@@

As we explained in the past days, Tawhid is the whole of the school of the prophets (peace be upon them).@@CX(Tawhid | is the whole of the school of the prophets)@@ Tawhid and the knowledge of God are, on the one hand, the means of the perfection and exaltation of the human spirit — which is the lofty, ultimate aim of the prophets — and, on the other hand, the plan of Tawhid, in the sense of bringing about a divine environment, a divine society and order, a just order, a classless order, an order without exploitation, an order without oppression — it means the formation of just such an order; and this is the very fitting environment of which we were speaking yesterday, that is necessary and indispensable for the nurturing of the human being.

Tawhid is the whole of the school of the prophets. Belief in Tawhid, in the existence of God and the oneness of God, both secures the final and ultimate aim of the prophets, and also [provides] that environment which they hold to be necessary for the making of human beings, for the operation of the factory of man-making. Tawhid is the best and most eloquent slogan; for the reason that a monotheistic society is a society in which God alone is master and lord. No one other than God acts as lord in it. No being in a monotheistic society imposes upon, lays obligations upon, or legislates for others. No other being in a monotheistic society calls the people to obey itself — not even the Prophet; not even the Prophet, who is the representative of God.@@CX(Tawhid's effects | in a monotheistic society God alone is lord and none calls others to obey itself)@@

Of course it is plain that no prophet says explicitly to the people, "O people, be my slaves and my chattels"; rather, what is meant is that no prophet has the right even to call the people to his own command unconditionally — that is, to that which is the exclusive right of God. When the Prophet was like this — when the chosen servant of God has no right to take any liberty within the domain of God's rule and dominion, when the bearer of the Lord's revelation cannot summon the people to obey himself in disregard of God — then the case of everyone else is evident. The political powers, the tyrants of history, the bullies who throughout the span of human life, in this long, extended period, imposed [their will] upon the people, laid obligations upon them, loaded burdens upon their shoulders — all of these performed an act contrary to Tawhid. Tawhid negates all of this; this is the meaning of Tawhid. The meaning of Tawhid is this, and if someone does not grasp this from Tawhid, it is certain that on this question he either has not studied or has not had the requisite understanding. This is a very noteworthy and important question. Often it is said to us: "Sir! If you have an objection to some of the religious propaganda, why do you not take it up with the propagandists themselves?" The answer is this: where are we to find the propagandists? Those whose manner of delivery and exposition we have a criticism of — where are we to find them? How much enforceable guarantee does our friendly, well-meaning advice have?

Let me say this to you, however: the gentlemen who are here, you mostly know me; you are mostly familiar with my thoughts, with my discussions. It may be that there is a small number of you for whom this gathering of ours has novelty; otherwise, most of you who are here have been [attending] our discussions for a long time — in that mosque, in this mosque. I firmly believe in the authenticity of religious propaganda, and in the authenticity and necessity of the standard-bearers of religion — that is, the august clergy of the Shīʿa. I love the clergy very much, and I believe in the necessity of the cleric. I believe that if the clergy — though, of course, the word "clergy" [rūḥāniyyat] is a mistaken word; since it is common and current, I use it — by "clergy" I mean the scholarly and religious community of the Shīʿa, this very [body] that our dear clerics today belong to, in this guild, in this class, in this stratum — I believe that if this did not exist, if the scholarly and religious community of the Shīʿa did not exist today in the world of Islam, the affairs of the Muslims would become even more pitiable than they are. And, in short, it is necessary that a group be girded for understanding and expounding the teachings of Islam, and that is precisely this scholarly and religious community. A certain young man, a certain tradesman, a certain person who has not made this work his special field — of course he may sometimes think, investigate, and even express it or write it down, and may God confirm and preserve those who render these services to Islam — but he is not a permanent [worker]; he is an amateur footnote:s18-15[Non-professional, beginner.] at this work. A professional is needed. It is necessary that there be someone whose profession is this, whose specialty is this, whose work is this — and that is the scholarly and religious community of the Shīʿa, that is, the clerics.

Therefore the existence of these people, the authenticity of these people, the necessity of these people — for you gentlemen who are here, my young friends, who know that I do not speak out of bigotry but speak out of realism — let there be no room for doubt. The existence of an entity called the clergy is necessary; it is among the most necessary of necessities. But we also have this right, and we grant it to ourselves, to say this matter to those who today stand in this path, in this garb:footnote:s18-16[From the root (k-s-w): garment, covering.] while we acknowledge that a number [of them] act exactly as we would wish — a number of the clerics and propagandists truly act as they should and perhaps as they do — there are also some who simply do not in any way attend to the fact that today their point of beginning is not the point of beginning of the prophets' work. Matters of the tenth rank, of the eighth rank, of the fifth rank, have, for them, more value and worth than the question of Tawhid — than the exposition of the Quran, than the principles of religion, than the lofty teachings of Islam.

He is ready to discuss for hours the question of how Nakīr and Munkar(((Nakīr and Munkar))) [the two angels of the grave] come when they come into the grave: do they stand on a person's right side, or on his left side, or do they stand facing him, or what are these [things]? — a question whose knowing or not knowing has not a particle of effect on a person's being a Muslim, has not the slightest effect on action and on the fulfillment of our Islamic commitments. And of this sort of question there are very many. These they bring into the reckoning footnote:s18-17[From the root (ʿ-d-d): number, count.] of the necessities of religion as well; like the first-rank matters of religion, they put these forward, but they are never ready to give thought to this question: that Tawhid — Tawhid's proposal for the form of society, for the social order, what is it? Does it have a proposal or not? Our message is that these tasks must be placed in the first rank.@@CX(Tawhid | its proposal for the form of society must be placed in the first rank)@@

The lesson we take from the work of the prophets and from the point of beginning of the prophets' call is, among others, this: that we too must make our own point of beginning the very point of beginning of the prophets. If we cannot accomplish the prophets' resurrection, at least we can say that the prophets' resurrection is this, that the aim of the prophets is this, that their road is this; we can explain it — this work we can indeed do. If there is a discussion about the Prophet of the End of Time, preference is given to matters of the fourth and fifth rank in the life of that great one — for example, the question of the Messenger of God's casting no shadow [is taken up]. There is a narration in the _Khiṣāl_ of Ṣadūq that the Messenger of God (may God's blessings be upon him and his family), when he walked, used to see from behind him; but, of course, the late Ṣadūq himself says in the footnote that what is meant is that the Messenger of God was so alert, so intelligent and watchful, like a person who continually sees what is around him. This man — his opinion is this. Now, I am not concerned with whether this opinion is correct or not correct; but they are ready to put forward this matter, with its opinion, with its criticism, with so-and-so traditionist's view about it, with another's view about it — to discuss it; whereas, as to what the noble Prophet had come for at all, what his proposal was concerning the form of society, what the Prophet said concerning government, what the Prophet's view was concerning the manner of human upbringing — did he consider individual upbringing sufficient, or did he hold social upbringing to be necessary? Concerning these things — that which is not raised at all — this is exactly it.

Today is not a day on which the world of Islam can bear delay in these matters.
