The theist says that beyond what we see, there is a reality higher and more magnificent than what we observe, and that were it not for that reality, these phenomena would never have taken form. The materialist(((materialism))) says: no; we cannot believe in, or commit ourselves to, anything other than what we see. In our laboratories and experiments, however much we have searched, we have found no sign and no trace of this being you speak of. Let the dispute between the materialist and the theist remain for the books and discussions devoted specifically to that subject.

Our belief is that the materialists of our age — and we are not concerned here with Democritus(((Democritus))) of history, that other materialist atheist of ten centuries ago, Democritusfootnote:s09-2[Democritus (Persian: _Dimokrat_ / _Zimoqratis_), 460–370 BCE, the famous Greek philosopher. He held that things are composed of small, indivisible parts called atoms. He did not believe in the existence of a soul and considered it bound up with the human brain; he thought that the only thing that exists is the atom and matter, and so he is called a materialist.] or some such scholar who lived twenty centuries, thirty centuries ago; we have no quarrel with him, he died and even his bones have turned to dust — the materialist of _our_ time, we hold, if he says there is no God, if he believes that beyond this world no other reality exists, in truth says this because he has suffered an intellectual and spiritual disillusionment with the school of theism.@@CX(materialism | rooted in disillusionment with theism, not in proof)@@ Because he believes that the building of today's world, the administration of human beings, the establishment of justice, and the removal of discrimination are impossible except in the shadow of a materialist, materialisticfootnote:s09-3[Materialism, or _madi-gari_, is a worldview in which everything beyond matter and the sensible is denied and existence is taken to be equivalent to matter.] mode of thought — for this reason he turns his back on the school of the theists. If we look carefully at the intellectual condition of those who, over the last fifty or sixty years, have embraced some of the "isms" of our time, if we study it properly, we will find that the matter is exactly as we have said. It is not that they bear some obstinate grudge against God, nor that they reject or refuse to accept God because they lack a convincing rational argument for His existence. Most often there is no rational argument on the other side of the question either; there is no argument for negating God — none at all, neither now nor in the past.

You will not find a single person who says, "I say there is no God for _this_ reason" — not one! Among all the materialists of the world, from the first day until now, there is not one who says, "I prove there is no God by this argument." Anyone who has something to say on this matter says, "It has not been proven to me that He exists; I did not understand, I did not accept the argument for His existence." So it is not on account of _not_ having a rationally satisfying philosophy, nor is there a rationally satisfying philosophy on the materialist side either. What there is, in the case of the materialist of our age — and this is a subtle point — the cause of his inclination toward what he calls the materialist school is that he imagines that today this materialist school can better administer the world. He says it can better eliminate oppression, better destroy discrimination and inequality, better uproot, dissolve, and annihilate tyranny and despotism. He says religion cannot do these things. Why does he say religion cannot do these things? Because of religion — of the common, prevalent notion of religion — he knows nothing except what he has observed in the hands of the people of the street and bazaar, in its traditional and imitative form. In short, he has no knowledge of religion, no information about it. If you ask him what religion is, he names a series of outward manifestations and says, "This is religion"; and because these are an opiate, because these are the brother and equal of oppression and the oppressor, because these cannot loosen a single knot in the people's tangled affairs,footnote:s09-5[An allusion to Hafiz: "Will it be, perhaps, that in the taverns they will untie / the knot of our tangled affairs?"] therefore — abandon it.

It is clear that when a person is faced with such a logic, the best and most correct answer is this: yes; if you have found a religion that colludes with the oppressor, that cooperates with the despot, that does not stand for one moment alongside the oppressed, that does not untie one knot in the people's tangled affairs, that has not the worth of a poppy seed, of a mustard's atom,footnote:s09-6[I.e., something utterly trifling.] for the people's today and tomorrow — then on our behalf you are authorized: if you have found such a religion, wherever you found it, reject it, do not accept this religion for a single moment. Because if a religion is from God, it is not like this.@@CX(right and falsehood | a religion that colludes with oppression must be rejected)@@

A religion that God sends is not just anything; it has a sign, it has a distinguishing mark, it bears a standard seal. If this standard seal matches a given religion, we accept it; if it does not match, we do not accept it. (When I speak about prophethood and the Prophet — God willing, in the series of Quranic discussions — perhaps I will convey this meaning there in greater detail.)

"We sent Our messengers with clear proofs" — We sent forth Our prophets with luminous proofs, "and We sent down with them" a body of thought and the practical instruments — instruments by which they could judge and adjudicate among the people in their disputes — toward the people. Why did We do these things? "_We_ sent" — not just one prophet, not just Moses, not just the Seal of the Prophets, not just Jesus; "We sent Our messengers" — all the prophets were sent with this purpose and with this idea and with this aim. What is that aim? "So that the people may uphold justice" — so that human beings may live on the basis of equity, justice, and fairness. _This_ is religion.@@CX(Prophethood | all prophets sent so people may uphold justice)@@ If you have seen a religion moving in the opposite direction to the philosophy of the religions, know that it is either not divine or it has been corrupted. If you have seen it moving contrary to the philosophy and the line of march of the divine prophets and the divine messengers, know with certainty that it is not the offspring of the Lord's revelation and of the prophets. This is quite clear.

So, O materialist! — the address is to them — O materialist! O you who say, "I have seen religion to be incapable of administering human societies" — we ask: which religion? The religion of Islam? True Islam? The Muhammadan revelation? The sovereign Alawid way of life? Have you seen _these_ to be incompatible with the administration of human affairs? Come, prove it; where is the incompatibility? An Islam that comes and removes discrimination, that negates class division(((social class))) in the very heat of the world's class division, that pours together the wealth that has been unjustly distributed and redistributes it per capita, that gives human beings equal opportunities and means, that takes governance out of the hands of human tyrants and entrusts it to the just law of God;@@CX(Tawhid's effects | negates class division and redistributes wealth)@@ that honors the base human being, the human being who is slapped about, the enslaver and the enslaved, the human being who — for the sake of a single word, for the sake of a single bravo, for the sake of a single worthless coinfootnote:s09-8[_Fils_ — black, worthless coin.] — would commit the basest of crimes; that elevates this base, abject, despised human being, raises him up, makes him noble, adorns him with moral and human virtues — and secures all of this in the shadow of a just and firmly grounded order.

Nor is the prophet's training a matter of individual cultivation — taking each person one by one by the hand and seating him in a corner of a private chamber, whispering incantations into his ear so that he may become upright, so that he may become a true human being; it is not like that either. Nor is it preaching, with the prophet sitting and preaching to the people — "people, this is bad, do it that way." It is a _social order_.@@CX(Goal of Islam | a social order, not mere individual cultivation or preaching)@@ The prophet, in the ignorant atmosphere of that day, cast the foundation of Islamic society as firm as steel, in a determinate shape and mold. Then he brought human beings into this mold, brought human beings into this path and this channel. And bringing them into this path _was itself_ their becoming true human beings; the two were one. So if you say that the Islamic religion — which is the truly Islamic religion, with these characteristics — if you say it this way, that it is not on a par with human progress, with justice, with the establishment of security, and with the meeting of human needs — no; we do not accept this, this is unfairness, this is unfairness.

If a scholar who grew up in the hollow milieu of Christianity(((Christianity))), who has seen of religious manifestations nothing but the false intercessions of Christ(((Jesus Christ))), the forgiveness of sins, and the selling of deeds of paradise for money — if _he_ gave himself the right to say this about religion, you, who live a hundred years, eighty years, fifty years after him, and who live in an era in which Islam is more or less showing itself, and has displayed and is displaying the sweetest and most precious human manifestations on the horizon of the world — _you_ have no right to say this. Well, this was, in summary, a set of generalities concerning the belief in Tawhid, in the form of a vision, in the sense of an answer to a single question: is there a God or is there not? Is there anything beyond this material world? Is there a hand of power or not? In answering this question I said that the theist speaks this way, the materialist(((materialism))) speaks that way — and set aside the war of theist and materialistfootnote:s09-11["Set aside" — meaning to let it go; from this verse of Hafiz: "Excuse the war of the seventy-two sects, all of them; / since they did not see the truth, they took the road of fable."] and leave it for the books; what little of it pertained to us, we have said in a word or two.

Now, a very important point. (If today I am unable to say everything I have written here, that is no obstacle, because this discussion will be continued — God willing, we will be with you tomorrow as well, if God wills. These matters which, as a preliminary and as the generalities of the subject, everyone must know — those I must state. Because it is Friday, some of you gentlemen may have been able to come today and may not be able to come tomorrow.) There are certain matters that are necessary for everyone in the domain of Islamic thought; everyone must know these, everyone must understand them. One of them is this: I say, gentlemen, when you raise the question of Tawhid, do not raise it as a dry, simple answer to a scientific and cerebral question; rather, raise it as a matter whose knowing or not-knowing is vital, decisive — raise it that way.@@CX(Tawhid | must be raised as a vital, decisive matter, not a dry cerebral question)@@ Now I will explain.

Suppose at one time you are going along some road; with a friend, with a fellow-traveler and companion, you are moving and going along a road, and a discussion arises between you. You say, "Sir, the lands on both sides of this road are, in my view, salt-marsh; no crop at all can be obtained here." He says, "No, sir, the lands on both sides of this road are, in fact, perfectly suited for the cultivation of such-and-such." You say something, he says something; you offer a proof, he offers a proof. Well, how important is this discussion? You are going on, after all. The gentlemen's automobile is passing along this road at a hundred and twenty. You are not now going to sit down and test its soil, nor are you going to buy a piece of these lands so as to come back later and grow beets, nor are you going to answer some testimony or inquiry made about these lands; this question and answer has no effect for you whatsoever. Just this much: it is an answer to a question, a reply in the face of some matter that has been raised, and nothing more — nothing else at all. Whether you said and proved that this soil is barrenfootnote:s09-12[_Lam-yazra'_ — unfit for cultivation.] and yields nothing, or he proved that no, on the contrary, excellent and remarkable beets come up and grow green here — whichever of you proves his claim, your road, your work, your present motion undergoes no change. It is not that, if it is proven to be barren, you say, "Well, now that it is barren, let us turn back"; no — you had no business with this place. Or

if it is proven that this place is fertile, you say, "Very well, now that it is fertile, come, let us pray two units of prayer at the foot of this fertile land"; no — it has no effect at all. Whether you prevail and prove your claim, or he prevails and proves his claim, it has no kind of effect on your motion, on this journey you are making, on your future, on your companionship — on anything. This is one kind of discussion, one kind of question, one kind of answer to a question.

At another time it happens that the two of you are seated in the car, this same car at this same speed is moving along this same road, and suddenly your friend says, "In my view, this road we are traveling reaches the north — whereas your destination is the south." You say, "No, sir, this road we are traveling reaches the south." He says no, you say no; this discussion flares up between the two of you. If he proves his claim, the head of the car must be turned around, you must go from the other direction — you had no business this way. If you prove your claim, with the very same course, the very same method, only a bit faster, you must take your road and go forward. Right now this hesitation that has arisen between you, and the two opinions that have appeared — its first effect is that the driver's foot goes to the brake, easing off a little: "Let us see now where we are going; in the end will we reach the destination or not?" This is called a question-and-answer and a discussion whose answer and whose result are _decisive_. The discussion of Tawhid is like this.

The way ordinary, common people, or the socially idle and the irresponsible and uncommitted, raise the question of Tawhid is different from the way a committed person ought to have the question of Tawhid raised for him. An uncommitted, irresponsible person raises Tawhid this way: is there a God or is there not? Well, now if there is, what shall we do? If there is not, what shall we do? What effect does it have on the condition of life, what change or alteration does it create in the social order? If there were a God, what would happen to the capitalist system of such-and-such a great power or such-and-such a superpower? A president who comes to power in a certain country — if he believed there is a God, how would he act? If he believed there is no God, how would he act? Does it make any difference at all? A knowledge of God and a worship of God in which the acceptance of one side leaves no difference and no effect upon the fate of cartelsfootnote:s09-13[A cartel refers to companies active in the same field that, by agreeing among themselves on the division of the market, the volume of production, and the price of the goods, monopolize the market for that commodity for themselves.] and trustsfootnote:s09-14[A trust arises from the union of several companies producing a similar commodity. In a trust, the shares of the member companies are separate and distinct, but the resources of all are placed at the disposal of the trust.] and capitalisms and discriminations — that worship of God, that belief in Tawhid, is like the belief in the fertility of this land we are passing by; it has no benefit, no effect, for us. What benefit is it that some political leader of a certain country believes in God, while for him the worship of God is merely an answer to a

dry, cerebral question and nothing higher? Being a worshiper of God, being a monotheist — _then_ is it important, effective, beneficial, necessary, vital for a political leader, for an ordinary person, for a society, for a nation, for a community and a group — when Tawhid is raised, understood, and grasped for the sake of its effects, for the sake of what follows from it, for the sake of the order it proposes, for the sake of the form of life it presents — for the sake of _these_ things of it. In our view this is a very important matter.

It comes into our imagination that Tawhid is something we need only clarify in our heads, establish as settled; and that when we come to life, this Tawhid no longer has any effect in life; and if it does have an effect, it is in personal life, not in social life. "Even if I am a monotheist, I will have the same capital and the same automobile and the same company and the same factory and the same condition of relationship with the worker and the same condition of relationship with the land as I would have had if I were not a monotheist." Look at the capitalist(((capitalism))) countries of the world, at these enormous powers whose name and fame have filled East and West: take two capitalists, two great merchants, two great factory-owners, two of these "sultans of industry," as they call themselves; suppose one believes in God and one is a materialist — what difference is there in their conduct? Now, if the one who believes in God went to church on Sunday

and gave a few coppersfootnote:s09-15[_Shahi_ — a small unit of currency, equal to one-twentieth of a rial, current in the Qajar period and the early Pahlavi era.] to the monk there to forgive him some of his sins, to smooth and level out a few kilometers of the road to paradise for him — but what is its effect in his life? In the condition of his factory? In his relations with the worker? In his relations with the people? In the manner of his wealth-accumulation, in his spending of wealth, in his amassing of wealth, what effect does it have? This Tawhid is not much different from polytheism.@@CX(Tawhid | a belief with no effect on life is little different from shirk)@@

The Tawhid to which Islam summons is a Tawhid higher than the level of an answer to a question, to an inquiry. So what is it? Islamic Tawhid is an inspiration in the domain of governance, in the domain of social relations, in the domain of the course of society, in the domain of the aims of society, in the domain of the people's duties, in the domain of the responsibilities that human beings bear before God, before one another, before society, and before the other phenomena of the world. _This_ is Tawhid. Islamic Tawhid is that same "alif" after which come "be" and "pe" and "che," all the way to "ye." It is not that you say, "God is one and not two," and the matter is finished. "God is one and not two" means that, in the entire region of your own existence — you individually and your society as a whole — no one but God has the right to rule.@@CX(Tawhid | only God has the right to rule over individual and society)@@ "God is one and not two" means that all the wealth you possess — you and all other human beings — belongs to God; you are no more than borrowers

and trustees. Who now is ready to become a monotheist? You hold the money in trust and that is all; you are a borrower. If, sir, some money of your friend's were deposited in your hand as a trust, what would you do? You wait for him to give the order. "Sir, give ten tomans of this money to that child, to that old man, to that stranger, to that relative; throw ten tomans of this money into such-and-such a fund; burn ten tomans of it altogether" — you wait for the handwriting of the owner of the money, do you not? Is it other than this? Do you regard yourself as having any right, any ownership, in this money held in trust and deposit? "Wealth is the wealth of God; He has placed it as deposits among the people."footnote:s09-16[Hadith no. 37: "_al-Mal malu Allah ja'alaha wada'i'a 'inda al-nas_" — "Wealth is the wealth of God; He placed it as deposits among the people."] — God has entrusted wealth as a deposit and trust into the hands of human beings. This is the corollary of Tawhid.@@CX(Anfal | wealth belongs to God; humans are only trustees)@@

If you hold to Tawhid, then class division and discrimination in society have no meaning — no meaning at all.@@CX(Tawhid's effects | destroys class division and discrimination)@@ The society that has a top and a bottom, that has an upper and a lower, that society is not a Tawhidi society. Tawhid says, "All of you are from Adam, and Adam is from dust"footnote:s09-17[Hadith no. 2: "_Kullukum min Adam wa Adam min turab_" — "All of you are from Adam, and Adam is from dust."] — all are the children of Adam(((Adam))), and Adam is from dust. Your nearness to God and your superiority lie in piety(((taqwa))), and nothing else; whoever is more mindful of God's command, he is the higher.@@CX(taqwa | the sole ground of superiority and nearness to God)@@ Otherwise, in a society in which there exist a thousand grounds for discrimination — you say, "Sir, so-and-so is like this," and the reply comes, "Well, so-and-so is among the nobility, he has a different reckoning" — in a society that has nobles and non-nobles, in a society in which

the people's enjoyments differ severely from one another and the privileged one regards it as his right; in a society in which the servants of God are not all on one level and some are still servants of others — in this society there is no Tawhid. When Tawhid comes to a society, all the servants are placed on one level. Meaning what? Meaning that all become servants of God — as I said yesterday. (On this sheet which relates to yesterday, we wrote this sentence.) The beings of the world, human and otherwise, are His subjugated servants,footnote:s09-18[From the root (q-h-r): vanquished, defeated.] and in servitude to Him all are partners and equals. This I explained in detail yesterday — that no one and no thing, under the heading of sonship, spousehood, or peerage, is outside the circle of servitude to God. Within the domain of servitude, it no longer has any meaning that one group of servants should again bind a chain around the necks of another group; that has no meaning at all. Servitude to God means freedom from servitude to other-than-God; the two simply do not go together.@@CX(servitude | servitude to God is freedom from servitude to other-than-God)@@ It has no meaning that someone should be a servant of God and also a servant of other-than-God. What is the servitude of [created] servants? The servitude of servants is this: that when, in one of the campaigns between Iran and Rome, one of the past kings of Iran — before the Sasanians, from the Achaemenids(((Achaemenids))) — when he gives the order for all to come, that old man comes and says, "Sir, my three sons are coming, they are with you in the field; let this one son of mine stay beside me, for I am old, I am decrepit; allow this one to remain and not come with you." In the assembly they say nothing to him; they drive him out of the assembly. Then the next day, when the troops are moving in columns of four, the horses and soldiers behind one another, as they reach the gateway, those three brothers suddenly see their fourth brother cut into two pieces, one piece installed on this side of the gate and one piece on that side of the gate — so that old men will no longer say, "Let our young men not come, and let them not fight for the power of Darius(((Darius)))," and will not throw themselves into water and fire. This is the servitude of man. When human beings in a society did not even have the right to _want_ what they want — I do not say to _say_ it; when human beings in a community had no right

to be partisans and seekers of justice, to be enemies of discrimination; when human beings had no right to love and want freedom for themselves, but accepted suffocation and strangulation as the natural condition and set their seal of approval upon it — when human beings lived in such a society, this is the highest and ugliest and bitterest of the kinds of servitude. This is the worst of the kinds of servitude. Why? Because it is fraudulent.footnote:s09-21[From the root (z-w-r): hypocritical, deceitful.] Those who used to go and seize a handful of innocent human beings in some place, shave their heads, and sell them in other lands — they performed an open and overt act; but to play with human beings in this way, to disregard a human being's thought and want and will and decision in this way, to trample him — this was it.

He said, "We have come to free you from the worship of [created] servants" — that is, to free the people from the worship of you, O Yazdgird! to free others from the worship of your governors, your village-heads, your commanders, here and there — "_min 'ibadat al-'ibad_." Then where shall we take them? They were not your servants, so what shall they be? Loose and lawless? No — they shall be servants of God. To be a servant of God means to be free, to be a master, to move toward perfection, to use and benefit from the means of perfection to whatever extent you wish — just as it was in the Islamic society. In the Islamic society, the people were servants of God, not servants of powers. Even

at the time when the course of the Islamic society had deviated and was not a hundred percent Islam, it was so. Even in those very years when the conquest of Iran was taking place, the effect of the prophetic and Quranic upbringing was present in the people. A political ruler says from the pulpit, "If I become crooked, set me straight," and a desert Arab rises from the foot of the pulpit and says, "If you do not become straight and you become crooked, with this sword we will set you straight." Did the soldiers and police fall upon his head? Did they throw him into prison as one creating disorder in the situation, did they do away with him, destroy him? Never. He had said something true, he had said something logical. Freedom not in the sense of unbridled license, but freedom in the sense of obedience to a sound human law — so that, in the spirit of man, in that order and in that society, a human being does not carry anyone's burden on his shoulders, not even the ruler's burden. If the ruler spoke from God, spoke by the inspiration of God, he is an Islamic ruler, and his word is accepted. If he did not speak by divine inspiration, his word too is rejected.@@CX(Wilayah | a ruler is obeyed only insofar as he speaks by God's inspiration)@@

The limited and narrow enclosure of the world! In a society in which the people do not live with a sound vision, from whichever side they look, before their gaze there is nothing but the world and its worldly gains; from whichever side the people look, nothing is at issue for them except worldly pleasures and the self-conceits of the world; whatever he looks at, he sees his own small and worthless

animal endeavors and his small, base, paltry, fleeting, and momentary interests. In a society over which Yazdgird ruled, it was not that all the people were content with Yazdgird; there were also many discontented. But because the eyes of these discontented were near-sighted, because the horizon of their vision was narrow and small, they saw that if they showed the slightest discontent and displeasure with Yazdgird, this very meager, base life would also be taken from them — these two extra mouthfuls of food, these two extra days of repose,footnote:s09-22[I.e., sleeping, resting.] these two extra kicks in the alley and the street, this would be taken from him; and because he set great store by this, because he loved this very meagerness so much, he was not ready to undertake any action for freedom, for human honor and authenticity and virtue. What was the cause? His horizon of vision was small, narrow, and limited — the narrowness of the world.@@CX(Tawhid's effects | a narrow worldly horizon breeds fear and submission to oppression)@@

But when a human being became a Muslim, everything is for him a preliminary, a means. For what? A means to what? A means of reaching a vast world — I do not mean the world after death — the world of man's own thought, vision, and sight, which is as wide and expansive as God. Everything is a means for man, so that man may be able to gain the pleasure of God. The life of the world, the money of the world, the comfort of the world, the affections of the world have no value and authenticity for him. It acquires value for him only

"_fi sabil Allah_" — when it is in the way of God. But if this affection, this wealth, this rank, this life, this child, this honor, this dignity were not, and were not placed, in the way of God and in the way of duty, then it has no price and no value for him. The world and the hereafter are sewn together in the Islamic mode of thought; and for a Muslim, the world has no end. In the view of that person who is the servant of servants and the slave of deficient beings, the world is limited; but for this one, the world is vast. Death is a small window: when you look from this window, on the other side are gardens and orchards and worlds and universes and realms; therefore he sees that the most that can happen is that he reaches this window, passes through this window — it does not matter; death is no problem for him.

It states a correct vision in the domain of Tawhid. Of course there are further aspects and angles that have not come into this, and which — God willing — I hope we can arrange in due order in the writings of the coming days. But in any case, what has been written here is one dimension of Tawhid that reflects and states precisely what is intended in the domain of Tawhid, what is intended by the religions and the Qur'an in particular.

Suddenly it transfers the discourse to a scene of the Resurrection. It describes that moment when the creatures have been gathered, mustered together at the Resurrection(((Resurrection))) — the disbelievers and the evildoers and the servants of other-than-God and the servants of God and all the existing phenomena of the Resurrection, those that have been described to us and those that have not — all are gathered. The instruments of God's punishment and the instruments of God's mercy and grace are there too; though as to the quality of these instruments, you and I cannot yet rightly grasp or imagine — in this world it cannot rightly be understood what news there is there. In general we know only this much: that the means that are to be foreseen there for torment, for punishment, for wretchedness are all present and prepared. The good servants of God and the bad servants of God are there; then the tyrants and the oppressors suddenly see that all power at the Resurrection belongs to God. It is a very strange thing.
