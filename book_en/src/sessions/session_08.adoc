In brief, what we are setting out to do is this: to single out the verses about tawhid(((Tawhid))) in the Qur'an(((the Qur'an))), to translate them here for you, and to let you yourselves deduce, first, what tawhid means from the Qur'an's standpoint, and second, what commitment and what practical conduct in life it implies.@@CX(Tawhid | studied through the Qur'an to derive its practical commitments)@@

And of course you have not forgotten — and must not forget — our earlier discussions, where we said that faith in any idea, in any principle among religious ideas and principles, must, first, be conscious; it must rest upon understanding, awareness, and insight, not be blind. And second, faith must be accompanied by commitment. That which we are to believe in is, inevitably, something that lays a commitment upon our shoulders in our lives, in our conduct — whether individual conduct or social conduct, whether it concerns our own person, or our own community, or humanity at large, or the future of history. Inevitably, it places a commitment upon our shoulders.@@CX(Faith | must be conscious and must impose commitment on conduct)@@

With this introduction in mind, when we enter the discussion of tawhid, this discussion will naturally be, for us, a discussion that imposes commitment. We will examine tawhid in such a way that, first, we may arrive at tawhid consciously; and second, that we may see what commitments tawhid places upon our shoulders. Is tawhid merely a kind of irresponsible, commitment-free understanding — a knowing of some truth without that knowing imposing any obligation upon us? Or is it otherwise? Is tawhid a form of awareness and knowledge such that, following upon this knowing, duties, obligations, and responsibilities devolve upon the human being? When we turn to the Qur'an, we will find the answer to this question.@@CX(Tawhid | is not commitment-free knowledge but entails duties and responsibilities)@@

Therefore, the verses we have in view today concern tawhid from one particular standpoint. Tomorrow, the verses will again concern tawhid, but from a different standpoint; and on the coming days, again there will be verses about tawhid, each from a standpoint of its own. And I do not claim that, with a discussion lasting a few days about tawhid, drawing upon the verses of the Qur'an, I will be able to set forth all the dimensions of Quranic and Islamic tawhid. Certainly, if some people, with a more precise eye, with greater clarity of vision, and with a more leisurely study, were to turn to the Qur'an, they would find a boundless ocean in the field of tawhid. To the measure that our gathering's time allows — for even all that we ourselves know about tawhid cannot be set forth in summary form in this gathering — to the extent that the capacity of our time and the scope of our assembly permit, I will speak.

In any case, we have considered tawhid from two standpoints — though, in the course of arranging the material, other branches may perhaps come up that need to be mentioned. First, we have considered tawhid within the worldview(((worldview))) of Islam. There is, most certainly, tawhid within the worldview of Islam. What is tawhid in the worldview of Islam, and what does it mean? And the verse of the Qur'an, the eloquent and articulate language of the Qur'an — how does it expound the tawhid that figures within the worldview of Islam? The second discussion we put thus: tawhid in the ideology of Islam. Of course, I will give a brief explanation of the term "worldview" for those who do not know it, and of the term "ideology"(((ideology))) for those who do not know it; for tawhid is part both of the worldview of Islam and of the life-constructing ideology of Islam.@@CX(Tawhid | belongs both to the worldview and to the ideology of Islam)@@

Beyond these, tawhid manifests and shows itself in every one of the subsidiary ordinances of Islam. And wherever you observe a ruling, a law, or a command put forward in the name of religion, in which tawhid limps — in which there is something anti-tawhid, and tawhid is absent — know that this is not from Islam. For tawhid is like a spirit within the body of all the ordinances of Islam. It is like a fine, subtle air present in all the parts of this edifice and frame whose name is Islam. It is like pure, clean, clear, fresh blood throughout this body and frame whose name is Islam and religion — it is even present in all the capillaries. You cannot find a single ruling in Islam that does not bear some color and trace of tawhid.@@CX(Tawhid | is like a spirit present in every ordinance and ruling of Islam)@@ Therefore today's discussion is about tawhid in the worldview of Islam.

What does the worldview of Islam mean? When you, as a human being, turn to your own thought, to your own perception, concerning the universe, the human being, and the world, you arrive at certain conceptions. It may be that you have not given thought to this; but the one who does give thought to it — when he thinks about the world, thinks about the human being, thinks about the relationship between the world and the human being, thinks about something beyond nature and beyond the human being and the world — arrives at a chain of conceptions and ideas. This is what is called a worldview. Every school of thought has a particular vision, a particular standpoint, a particular conception, a particular understanding of the world; this particular conception of the world, this special understanding of the universe, this manner of seeing the universe — its name is worldview.@@CX(Islamic worldview | a school's particular conception and understanding of the universe)@@ It is among the terms that have only in the last several years come to be seen and noticed in Persian(((Persian language))) expression. "Worldview" does not mean breadth of outlook, in the sense commonly used in colloquial expression — "so-and-so is a person of worldview," meaning he is broad-minded — no; worldview does not mean this.

In brief, worldview can be defined thus: worldview means a human being's conception of the world, a human being's apprehension of the world, a human being's understanding of the world or of the human being. We said "a human being's conception"; but you may, in place of "a human being," put "a school," "a creed," "a religion," "a social practice" — it has a conception of the world, and this conception is what is called its worldview. Islam has a worldview; Islam has a conception of the world. Islam's conception of the world, to the extent that it bears upon our discussion of tawhid, I will set forth briefly.

Islam holds that the whole of this aggregate whose name is "the world" — from top to bottom, from the most insignificant and lowly beings to the great and conspicuous beings; from the basest living or lifeless thing to the noblest and most powerful of living beings possessed of intellect, that is, the human being — all of it, every part of it, every corner of this universe, is a servant, a thrall, a creature of, and dependent upon, an exceedingly mighty power.@@CX(Tawhid | in Islam's worldview every being is a dependent servant of one mighty power)@@ Beyond this surface that you and I see, beyond what the keen-sighted spectacles of empirical science can reach, beyond all the phenomena that can be sensed and touched, there is a truth higher than all truths, more exalted, nobler, dearer; and all these phenomena of the universe are made, fashioned, and brought into being by the hand of His power. To that higher power we give a name; its name is "God," its name is "Allah."(((Allah)))

So the world is a truth that has no independence in itself; it did not bring itself into existence; it did not well up from within and from inside itself. Rather, a powerful hand created and brought into being these various phenomena — which, with every passing day that knowledge advances, show themselves more and more. It is a powerful hand that created that tumult in the heart of the atom, and that, in the highest unknown worlds — the galaxies, and what lies beyond the galaxies, where it may be that there are things to be discovered billions of times greater than all we have discovered to this day — brought all of this into being. This factory has a maker; this apparatus has an originator; it is not a matter of chance, it is not self-existent.@@CX(Tawhid | the world has no independent existence but was created by a powerful hand)@@ Islam regards the world as just such a thing.

These are the materials of the Islamic worldview in the field of tawhid, which I am stating in just this way, little by little, briefly. (Commit them to your minds, so that, God willing, if tomorrow's written text is ready, in the writing too we shall set down something in brief.) That God who is higher than the world and the worldly, that hand of power that turns all the beings of the universe — He made them and He, moment by moment, has kept them enduring; this powerful hand — whose name is Allah, whose name is God — is characterized,footnote:s08-3[From the root _(w-ṣ-f)_: possessor of an attribute.] in an original and essential manner, with all the good and goodness-creating attributes: that is, He possesses knowledge, He possesses power, He possesses life, He possesses will and resolve, and all that springs and derives from these attributes. His life was not taken from anyone; His knowledge was not acquired or obtained from anywhere; and so on.

He is the Sovereign of the universe. Who are the inhabitants of the universe? What are the particles of the universe in relation to Him? Once the particles of the universe have been made and fashioned by Him, did they then become separate — like a child that separates from its mother and is thereafter independent? No; the matter is not at all like that. At every moment, for their remaining and their being, they are in need of Him — in need of His power and His will; and all are His servants. All beings are His servants, His thralls, His artifacts, His creatures. He can dispose over all of them; He created all of them with a particular order, brought all of them into being with regular and precise norms and laws. Today, science is discovering these laws — though, of course, my discussion is not in this field. That is, the argument for the existence of God and the proof of the Maker is not part of my discussion; there are abundant books in this field — go and read them.

Nevertheless, it would not be amiss to recall this one statement, on the authority of several scholars of the empirical sciences — not philosophers, but scholars whose work has to do precisely with the laboratory, with industry, with construction, and with such things. In this book — which two or three accomplished Iranian translators have translated, and which is the work of several non-Iranian scholars and authors, titled _The Proof of the Existence of God_footnote:s08-4[_The Proof of the Existence of God_, compiled by John Clover Monsma, is a book comprising forty essays by scholars of the modern sciences on the proof of the existence of God. This book was translated by Messrs. Aḥmad Ārām, ʿAlī-Akbar Mujtahidī, and Sayyid Mahdī Amīn.] (it is a good book, it would not be amiss to consult it) — several scholars of today's empirical sciences say that, with the advance of knowledge, with penetration into the inmost nature of creatures and beings and the discovery of regular laws, we come to understand that the world has an originator. From the sheer extent to which we see that order exists, the sheer extent to which orderliness and discipline are at work in the affairs of the universe, we understand that the world has a Creator and a Maker. Well, then: all the beings of the universe are His servants, His originated ones, all under His authority and within the grip of His power — and the human being too, like the rest of the other beings.

In the worldview of Islam, tawhid is just such a thing; tawhid means that the world has an Originator and a Maker, and, in a manner of speaking, has a pure and subtle spirit.@@CX(Tawhid | a Muslim sees the universe not as independent but as dependent on a higher power)@@ The world has an originator, and all the parts of this universe are reckoned as servants, thralls, and beings under the authority of that God and that Originator. Tawhid in the worldview of Islam is this; that is, when a Muslim looks upon this universe from the standpoint of Islam, he does not see this universe as an independent being, but rather sees a being dependent upon a higher power. What effect does this have? What benefit does it have? It has a remarkable effect; in the subsequent discussions that we hold about tawhid, it will then become clear what effect this vision, this particular worldview, this perception and conception of the universe, this apprehension of the world and the worldly, has upon the building of life.

We turn to the verses of the Qur'an, and we see that the Lord of the universe has mentioned precisely this same matter in the Quranic verses. From two places in the Qur'an we have selected verses to be recited and translated today. The first is from Sūrat al-Baqarah, the verse famous as Āyat al-Kursī. The first part of Āyat al-Kursī is in this field, which we will now recite, translate, and reflect upon. It is verse 255.

"Allah" — this is a name for which an equivalent, by way of exposition and elucidation, cannot be found. What is "Allah"? It is God. And who is God? He is that being of whom it is said: _Lā ilāha illā Huwa_ — there is no deity but He. Understand _ilāh_ as "object of worship." _Ilāh_ means any being before whom the human being submits in the manner of sanctification,footnote:s08-6[From the root _(q-d-s)_: to deem pure / hallow.] of veneration and reverence — hands over his own free will to him, entrusts the reins of his life to him, sets him up as one given a free hand and unrestrainedfootnote:s08-7[Without restraint or bond.] over his own life. This, in Quranic terminology, is called _ilāh_. Those who make the desire of the self the holder of the reins of their lives — their _ilāh_ is the desire of their self. Those who give a free hand to a rebellious, transgressing human being in the affairs of their lives — their _ilāh_ is that very Satan. Those who submit, unconditionally and unreservedly, to hollow customs and beliefs — their _ilāh_ is that very hollow custom and belief. Whatever, in the human being's existence and in the human being's life, has a free hand unconditionally, and rules and dominates — that is an _ilāh_.@@CX(servitude | whatever rules a person's life unconditionally becomes that person's ilāh)@@

Here it says: _Lā ilāha illā Huwa_ — there is no _ilāh_, no object of worship, but He. What does "there is not" mean? Does it mean there is none in the world? There were thousands of objects of worship in the world; three hundred and sixty of them were hung and set up within the House of the Kaʿba(((Kaʿba))) itself — three hundred and sixty idols. And just as many living idols, too, were ruling in the world. How then is there no _ilāh_! So this that says _Lā ilāha illā Huwa_ — "there is no _ilāh_ but He" — means there is no real _ilāh_, no rightful _ilāh_, no true _ilāh_ but Him; that is, anyone other than Allah whom you have accepted in divinity — in that sense of worship-worthiness which I described — you have committed a sin, you have performed an act contrary to right. For other than Allah, no one is worthy of being an object of worship and of divinity.@@CX(Tawhid | there is no true ilāh but Allah; worshipping any other is a wrong)@@

_Allāhu lā ilāha illā Huwa_ — what is the distinctive quality of this Allah, this unique and single _ilāh_? He enumerates some of His qualities: _al-Ḥayy_ — He is the Living. All the others are dead; the others are dead beings and corpses; He is the Living. The lifeless ones — that is plain; but the living ones too are dead. A living thing that on one day did not exist and on another day will not exist; a living creature whose very life is constantly under threat; a living being whose life, with the movement of a single hair, with the stirring of the brain, is sometimes threatened with annihilation and destruction — what kind of life is this? The everlasting Living, the original Living, the true Living, is the one to whom life truly belongs, and which is His gift, His bestowal, His grant to all living things — and He is God. _Al-Ḥayy_ — the Living. _Al-Qayyūm_ — the Enduring and Sustaining; the one whose life is permanent, eternal, and perpetual; the one by whose life the living are alive, and if He were not, if He did not will, if He did not so resolve, not a single living thing and not a single manifestation of life would remain in the world. _Al-Qayyūm_.

_Lā taʾkhudhuhū sinatun wa-lā nawm_ — neither drowsiness nor sleep takes hold of Him; light sleep — let alone heavy sleep. It does not seize Him away from Himself. What does this mean? It means that a moment of heedlessness, a moment of inattention, has no way into His being and into His life. The other beings, the false lords, are heedless — of themselves, of the people under their authority, of the world that is in their grip; they are heedless from head to foot, constantly in heedlessness; and even where they claim awareness and knowledge, their awareness and knowledge is false. What prevails over them is heedlessness and ignorance.

So the other gods are heedless from head to foot.@@CX(Tāghūt | the false lords are heedless and drag their followers into ruin)@@

The One whom heedlessness does not seize is the real ruler of this world; that is, Allah. _Lā taʾkhudhuhū sinatun wa-lā nawm._ Do not imagine that _sina_, meaning drowsiness — light sleep — and _nawm_, meaning ordinary or heavy sleep, being removed from God and negated of Him, served no purpose here. They did. In the subsequent discussions we will see why. For every allusion in tawhid, every fine point in tawhid, is an allusion to the negation of the divinity of other-than-God, and an allusion to the deficiencies of other-than-God. Everything that is affirmed of God is precisely those things that are negated of the claimants to divinity. What is said in tawhid is precisely those things that must be reflected in the practical life of the monotheists and the worshippers of God.@@CX(Tawhid | every attribute affirmed of God is denied of the false claimants to divinity)@@ All the qualities and fine details of tawhid must have their instances in the lives of the monotheist people — which comes up in the subsequent discussions and recitations. Therefore, _lā taʾkhudhuhū sinatun wa-lā nawm_ is an allusion to the heedlessness, the slumbers, the lapses of the false lords and lordlings.

_Lā taʾkhudhuhū sinatun wa-lā nawm; lahu mā fī al-samāwāti wa-mā fī al-arḍ_ — His is whatever is in the heavens and whatever is on the earth; it belongs to Him, it is His servant. _Man dhā alladhī yashfaʿu ʿindahū illā bi-idhnih_ — who is there that can intercede before Him, mediate, except by His permission? We know of no other power that, even to the measure of the power of a single intercessor, could stand up before God. If anyone intercedes for others, he still does so by the permission of God. The prophets who intercede; the saints, the Imams, the righteous, the believers, and the martyrs who intercede before God — they do not do so except by the permission of God. Therefore they too are not powers set up against the power of God, not shops set up against God. It is not that they too are a separate establishment and apparatus set up against the establishment and apparatus of God — no; they are servants of God, only servants who are objects of God's grace and love.@@CX(shafāʿah | intercessors act only by God's permission, not as a rival power)@@

_Man dhā alladhī yashfaʿu ʿindahū illā bi-idhnih_ — who is it that can intercede before Him except by His permission and leave? _Yaʿlamu mā bayna aydīhim wa-mā khalfahum_ — whatever is before them and behind them, He knows; that is, He encompasses the whole life of human beings and of all beings. _Wa-lā yuḥīṭūna bi-shayʾin min ʿilmihī illā bi-mā shāʾ_ — they do not encompass, their knowledge does not extend to anything of the knowledge of the Lord, except what He Himself wills.

See how He divides the whole of the universe into two ranks. One rank: the rank of God; the other rank: the rank of the other beings. In this other rank, all the particles of the universe are servants of God in a single degree. I mean to say that, with respect to servanthood to God, there is no distinction between any two beings in the universe. In this respect — that they are servants of God, that they are under the power and within the grip of God — they are all alike, all of them!@@CX(servitude | all beings are equal in their servanthood before God)@@ Even the greatest being of the universe, the dearest and most precious human being of history — that is, the sacred person of the Noble Prophet(((Prophet Muhammad))) (God's blessings be upon him and his household) — with respect to being a servant of God, being under the authority of God, being a slave of God, is like all the other particles of existence. To the same degree that they are under the authority of God, to that same degree the Prophet too is under the authority of God. It is not that a certain prophet, a certain station, a certain great human being, because he is great, because he is select, because he is beloved of God, because he is dear to God, should have some store of power set up against God — no; they are bound-handed servants, submitted to the Lord, and their very greatness lies in just this.@@CX(servitude | even the Prophet's greatness lies in being fully a servant of God)@@

In the _tashahhud_(((tashahhud))) of the prayer you recite: _Ashhadu anna Muḥammadan ʿabduhū wa-rasūluh_ — you bring being a servant of God forward first; I bear witness that Muḥammad is His servant — His slave — and His Messenger. You first mention being a servant.@@CX(servitude | the tashahhud names the Prophet a servant before naming him Messenger)@@

_Wasiʿa kursiyyuhu al-samāwāti wa-l-arḍ_ — His throne of power has spread wide over the heavens and the earth. _Wa-lā yaʾūduhū ḥifẓuhumā_ — the safeguarding of the heavens and the earth is not heavy or burdensome upon Him. _Wa-Huwa al-ʿAliyyu al-ʿAẓīm_ — He is the Most High and Most Honored in rank; He is the Great and Majestic.

From these verses, what do you understand from the whole? Apart from the fine details that are in the verse, apart from the subtleties and intricacies that are in every single sentence — of which some part you may understand, or I may understand, and there are tens or hundreds of other subtleties and fine points that no one understands except the chosen servants of God. From the phrase _Allāhu lā ilāha illā Huwa_, Imam al-Sajjād(((Imam al-Sajjād))) understands subtleties and fine points that you and I do not understand. Apart from these fine points, apart from these subtleties, apart from the social or doctrinal matters that are present here — from the whole of it, what do you understand?

From the whole, you grasp well the vision of Islam concerning God; and what is it? It is this: that throughout the entire domain of existence there is one power, named God; one center of power, one focus of knowledge, life, and force, named God. And on the opposite side, all the phenomena, facing that mighty and majestic power in a state of destitution,footnote:s08-10[From the root _(s-k-n)_: poverty, indigence.] in a state of servitude, in a state of servanthood — all of them, all of them. And there is no difference, either, among the phenomena of the universe with respect to servitude before that center of power. From a tiny particle to a mighty galaxy, from a believing human being to a disbelieving one, from a worthless being to a worthy human being — all, before that being, are bound-handed, submitted, slaves, servants, thralls; whatever expression you wish to use.

Of course, understanding this subject has effects in the recognition of the ideology of Islam, in the recognition of the practical programs of Islam for society. By way of example — I can now point this out so that a flash of lightning may strike in your minds, that you may know we do not wish to engage in philosophical hairsplitting — once we have understood that all human beings are, in sum, equalfootnote:s08-11[Equal, on a par.] before this power and this center of force, it then no longer makes sense for the emperor of Rome(((Rome))) — Valerian,(((Valerian)))footnote:s08-12[He was among the great emperors of Rome, who subjected the Christians to much persecution and torment. This king, toward the end of his life, was taken captive in a war with the Sasanians. The Christians called this captivity a divine retribution upon Valerian, who had persecuted the Christians.] for instance — in a state of pride and arrogance, to have his statue built there with a slave, a bondsman, fallen at his feet.@@CX(Tawhid | knowing all are equal before God dissolves the tyrant's claim to superiority)@@ We ask: why? On what grounds? Has this exalted-stationed emperor found, apart from the rank of the servants of God, some other rank? And has this human being who has now fallen at the emperor's feet, who is prostrating himself before him, who is bowing and abasing himself before him — has he found, apart from the rank of servants, some lower, baser rank? Are not the two of them both attached to one and the same rank? Then why this situation?
