== Session 28: Concerning Wilayat (3): Hijrah

[.session-subtitle]
_در پیرامون ولایت (۳): هجرت — delivered Wednesday, 24 Mehr 1353 / 29 Ramadan al-Mubārak 1394_

The question of _hijrah_ (emigration) relates to the question of _wilāyah_, with that breadth in which we have framed the question of wilāyah here. We said that wilāyah consists of forging a firm bond and connection among the elements of the believing ranks with one another, and of severing every form of attachment between the believing ranks and the non-believing ranks; and, at a further level, of forging a most powerful and strong bond between every individual of the believing ranks and that central point and concentrated authority upon whom the administration of the Islamic society rests — that is, the _imam_, the _walī_, the ruler, the leader. Alongside this, we then discussed who the _walī_ and leader is within the Islamic society. The Qur'an gave us our answer:

[.verse,quran,surah="al-Māʾidah",s=5,a="55"]
[quote]
____
*Qur'an, Sūrat al-Māʾidah (5:55):* "Your guardian (_walī_) is only God, and His Messenger, and those who believe — those who establish the prayer and pay the alms while bowing."footnote:s28-1[Sūrat al-Māʾidah, verse 55.]
____

— to which we pointed, in connection with the affair of the Commander of the Faithful, may God's blessings be upon him. If we have been able to understand wilāyah with this breadth, and if we do not reduce the matter to the level of the subsidiary issues of wilāyah and to the second degree of wilāyah — as some, without due attention, do reduce it — then the question of hijrah turns out to be one of the corollaries of the question of wilāyah. Why do we say it this way? Because if it is necessary that every human being live within the wilāyah of God and of the _walī_ of God — which is what the principle of wilāyah taught us — and if we have accepted that a human being must put all of his energies, all of his bodily, intellectual, and psychic vitalities to work under the will of the divine _walī_ and the governor who is _min qibal Allāh_footnote:s28-2["From God."] (from God), and, in short, that with every element of his being a human being must be a servant of God and not a servant of the _Tāghūt_ — if we accept and embrace these points, then we have no choice but to accept this too: that if somewhere our existence, our being and all our energies and vitalities, were not under the command of the divine wilāyah but rather under the command of the _Tāghūtī_ and satanic wilāyah, then our divine commitment is that we free ourselves from the bonds and shackles of the wilāyah of the Tāghūt — that we rescue ourselves, liberate ourselves, and go beneath the most blessed shade of the wilāyah of God. Coming out of that oppressive wilāyah and entering that just wilāyah — its name is _hijrah_. You see, then, that the question of hijrah is one of the matters that arises in the train of wilāyah. This is why we placed the discussion at the very outset: "Concerning Wilāyah" — that is, in connection with wilāyah we mentioned three topics, and this is the fourth.

Why must a human being flee from beneath the wilāyah of the Tāghūt and Satan? This question that I am raising is very much worth attending to. When I raise the question, I would dearly like you, the friends who are listening, to break down and analyze my question at once in the laboratory of your minds, in such a way that you can give it an answer in accordance with your Islamic and religious learning and accumulated knowledge. Then, if your answer turns out not to coincide with my answer — the answer that I will give — and is at variance with it, then there remains room for discussion between you and me. The question is this: Is it not possible to be within the wilāyah of the Tāghūt and yet be a Muslim? Is it not possible for us to suppose a Muslim who is under the wilāyah of Satan, yet who lives as a servant of the All-Merciful? Is such a thing possible or not? Is it possible that, in a setting where over the whole horizon and across all the regions of human life a non-divine factor is busy ruling — where a non-divine factor is administering the human body, where a non-divine factor is administering human thought, where that same factor is dragging the morale, the affections, and the sentiments of the members of society this way and that — while a human being lives gripped in the fist of the power of such Tāghūtī and satanic factors, that nonetheless he be a servant of God, be a Muslim? Is such a thing possible or impossible? I beg you to set about answering this. You need not answer aloud; prepare an answer to this question in your minds; see whether it is possible or not possible. In order for us to know whether it is possible or not, we must analyze and interpret this very question a little, so that the answer may become clear.

We ask: can a person be under the wilāyah of Satan and be a Muslim, or can he not? These two questions are, in reality, a single question composed of two parts; let us properly break down these two parts and see what each means.

The first part is that a person be under the wilāyah of Satan. What does it mean to be under the wilāyah of Satan? What does wilāyah even mean? If we place that meaning which we derived for wilāyah from the verses of the Qur'an — a meaning which was supported by several Qur'anic verses — beside this phrase "the wilāyah of Satan," then it becomes clear what the wilāyah of Satan means. The wilāyah of Satan means this: that Satan — in that comprehensive sense of "Satan" of which I have spoken many times — be dominant over all the powers, capacities, initiatives, and works within a person's being. Whatever the person does is along the course that Satan has determined; whatever he thinks is on the path that Satan inclines toward or sketches out. It is like a man who has fallen into the current of a river. A swift water is rushing down in the bed of a flood from the mountainside; a man falls into this river. Of course he does not wish to strike the huge, towering boulders and split his head open; of course he does not wish to be carried off by this water and dropped into a swamp; of course he does not wish to be choked and drowned amid the waves of this water — but, despite not wishing it, this swift current of water is carrying him off against his will. He flails his arms and legs too, clings to this side and that, grasps at everything — but the current of the water is a swift current; against his will it carries him away.

The wilāyah of the Tāghūt and the wilāyah of Satan is just such a thing. This is why the verse of the Qur'an says:

Another verse of the Qur'an says:

*Qur'an, Sūrat Ibrāhīm (14:28–29):* "Have you not regarded those who exchanged the bounty of God for ingratitude and settled their people in the abode of perdition — Hell, which they shall enter? And what an evil place of settlement!"footnote:s28-4[Sūrat Ibrāhīm, verses 28 and 29.]

Have you not looked upon those people who were ungrateful for the bounty of their Lord? This bounty for which they were ungrateful — what was it? It could be anything. The bounty of power, which is a manifestation of the power of the Lord; worldly powers; the bounty of stewardship and administering the affairs of thousands of human beings; the bounty of having at one's disposal the capacities, thoughts, energies, and abundant powers of human beings — all of these are bounties; these are capitals that could have been a source of good for humanity. The human beings who came under the control of those people referred to in this verse — these human beings could have been great human beings, could have been the elite servants of God, could have reached the highest degrees of perfection; but these people were ungrateful for these bounties, did not put them to work in the way that they ought. Then the verse goes on to say: "_wa aḥallū qawmahum dār al-bawār_" — and they too cast their own people into the abode of perdition; they drove their people and the people under their command into the realm of nonexistence, annihilation, and destruction. "_Jahannama yaṣlawnahā_" — and they drove them into Hell, into which they will be made to slide and be flung. "_Wa biʾsa al-qarār_" — and what an evil abode and place of settlement it is!

This verse was recited by Mūsā ibn Jaʿfar, may God's blessings and peace be upon him, to Hārūn; he made Hārūn understand that "you are that very one who will cast your people and yourself into the worst dwelling place, into the most calamitous of resting-places." Mūsā ibn Jaʿfar recited this meaning to Hārūn explicitly. He asked: "Are we then unbelievers?" — meaning, "do we then not believe in God, in the Prophet, and in the religion?" The Imam, peace be upon him, in answer recited this verse so as to make him understand that the unbeliever is not only the one who says plainly, directly, and explicitly, "No, there is no God; no, the Qur'an is a lie," or "the Prophet, for instance, is a legend." This is one type of unbeliever, and it is the best kind of unbeliever, the one who states his position explicitly so that a person knows him and properly arranges his own stance toward him. The worse unbeliever is the one who is ungrateful for these immense bounties at his disposal, who casts them into the wrong channel — who drags not only himself, but all the human beings under his command, into Hell. The wilāyah of the Tāghūt is just such a thing.

The one who lives under the wilāyah of the Tāghūt has, in reality, as it were no will of his own. I do not say he is utterly devoid of will — now that we have interpreted the verse of the Qur'an, the explanation of the matter becomes clear in the Qur'an — but he is being carried off in the current of the flood. He wants to flail his arms and legs, he cannot. He wants to turn back from the road of Hell; he sees that everyone all around is moving toward Hell, and they drag him along with them. Those places where there is a large crowd — have you been there? Sometimes you want to go this way; the crowd carries you off against your will like a wisp of straw. He wants to be good, to live a good life, to live as a human being, to remain a Muslim and die a Muslim — he cannot; that is to say, the social current is dragging and carrying him off, carrying him off in such a way that he cannot even flail his arms and legs. And even if he does flail, he has accomplished nothing other than the waste of a certain amount of energy. Not only can he not stir his limbs — what is more painful is that sometimes he cannot even comprehend.

These fish that they catch at sea — I do not know whether you have seen this or not. Sometimes thousands of fish are being dragged within a single net toward the shore; from out in the midst of the sea, from several kilometers out, this net is dragging them all forward, and they are unaware. If you were to say to that fish, "Where are you going?", it would think it was going toward some destination by its own choice; but in reality it has no choice; its destination is the very place that is the destination of that fisherman who owns the net. This invisible net of the ignorant order drags a human being along so, drags him so toward those directions in which the steerers of that net incline, that the person does not comprehend where he is going. Sometimes he even imagines that he is heading toward the abode of felicity and salvation — heedless of the fact that no, he is heading toward "_Jahannama yaṣlawnahā wa biʾsa al-qarār_."

Well, this wilāyah is the wilāyah of the Tāghūt, the wilāyah of Satan. This was the first clause of the two clauses out of which the question was composed. We said: Can one live within the wilāyah of the Tāghūt and live as a Muslim? Can one? Now we have understood, in summary, what living within the wilāyah of the Tāghūt means. And if we wish to understand it in detail too, we can turn back again to history.

Look at how, in the time of the Banū Umayyah and the Banū ʿAbbās, the world of Islam moved with such vitality; look at what an immense wave of knowledge and learning arose within the Islamic society of that day, what great physicians appeared, what great translators appeared in that era of dearth of linguistic knowledge and of general world-knowledge, who translated into Arabic the great works of the ancient cultures and disseminated them among the Muslims. In every field — history, hadith, the natural sciences, medicine, astronomy, and even the beautiful and delicate arts — the Muslims of that day became among the most outstanding exemplars; is that not so? — to such an extent that even now, when a man like "Gustave Le Bon"footnote:s28-5[Gustave Le Bon (1841–1931 CE), a French sociologist and orientalist who carried out extensive studies on Muslims and Islamic civilization. His works contain comparatively fewer bigoted opinions. _The Civilization of India_, _The Civilization of Egypt_, _Arab Civilization in Andalusia_, and _The Civilization of the Arabs_ are among his books.] the Frenchman, or some other writer and orientalist whose eye is dazzled only by these very outward appearances, looks back, he regards the second, third, and fourth Islamic centuries as the centuries of Islam's radiance. "Adam Mez"footnote:s28-6[Adam Mez (1869–1917 CE), a German orientalist resident in Basel, Switzerland. His book was translated into Arabic and published under the title _al-Ḥaḍāra al-Islāmiyya fī l-Qarn al-Rābiʿ al-Hijrī_ (Islamic Civilization in the Fourth Hijrī Century).] wrote a book called _The Civilization of Islam in the Fourth Islamic Century_; he conducts a remarkable lament over this civilization, which in his view is immense. He describes a great civilization in the fourth Islamic century. A French orientalist, a European orientalist, in general, when he looks back at those third, fourth, and second Islamic centuries, his eyes are still dazzled. Why is this? It is because of this: that strange activities, energies, and vitalities issued in that day from the Islamic society. But I ask you: did all this vitality and activity that was carried out in that day ultimately turn out to the benefit of the Islamic society and of humanity? I ask you.

Ten centuries are passing from that era. We have no fanaticism toward that era. Vis-à-vis the non-Muslim world we can say, "Yes" — it was the world of Islam that brought those universities into being, it was the world of Islam that constructed that philosophy, it was the world of Islam that did such and such in medicine and the natural sciences. But, between ourselves, in all truth and fairness: did all that power and energy turn out to be in the right place, at the right time, and to the benefit of humanity and of the Islamic society? After ten centuries, what does the Islamic society have of that legacy? And why does it not have it? Why does it not have it? Why has that scholarly and cultural wealth not remained for us? Why is it that we, as a society that ten centuries ago possessed all that radiance, today do not shine in the world and make no display? Why? Is it not because all that vitality and activity — though it was a human activity — was beneath the signet-ring of the Tāghūt? As the poet said:

It was these _Ahrimans_ (demons) who toyed with the Islamic society. If they did speak, if they translated, it was for the sake of writing in the translation, "In the reign of His Excellency So-and-so the umpteenth Abbasid caliph, Manṣūr or Hārūn or Maʾmūn" — in order to raise up their own names. If, instead of all this activity in the natural sciences, mathematics, astronomy, literature, jurisprudence, recitation, and the rest, they had permitted the ʿAlawī government to take charge, had permitted Imam Ṣādiq to take charge — had they allowed Imam Ṣādiq to take in hand the vitalities and energies — then even if, scientifically and from the standpoint of literature and of those matters that the world today preens and boasts about, they had fallen back a hundred years, it would still have been to the benefit of humanity. Humanity would have grown, Islam would have blossomed, energies and capacities would have been put to work along the right path. Of what use is it that they translate books, that they bring medicine and scientific progress to the utmost degree, but from the standpoint of morality and of social ethics they are so weak that their class discord is still to be counted among the legends of history?

Exactly like the filthy, disgraceful civilization of our own contemporary world. The great world powers preen over their scientific discoveries, preen over their mind-boggling inventions, raise the cocked corner of their cap of pridefootnote:s28-8[_Kulāh-gūsheh_ (the cocked corner of the cap): grandeur and pomp; a metonym for greatness and pride. "The sultan's worth and majesty lost nothing / from his attention to the cottage of a peasant; / The peasant's cap-corner reached to the sun / when it cast its shade upon his head, with a guest like you." (Saʿdī)] to the very sun — "We discovered such-and-such a drug, we produced such-and-such a formula, we accomplished such-and-such a feat." This is from the scientific standpoint; but from the human and moral standpoint, even now, to this very day, they are living in the eras of thousands of years of pre-history. Even now, fabulous riches stand alongside fabulous hungers and poverties; even now there exists the enjoyment by a very small one percent in the world of privileges that exist at the expense of the millions of starving human beings in the deprived countries. They preen over this — and this is exactly the same condition as that great Islamic civilization in the second, third, and fourth centuries; it is the very same matter; in that day too it was just so. Vast scientific progress, but aristocratic excess and indulgence and heedlessness of virtues, of human qualities, and of authenticities; class discord in its severest form, deadly hungers in the face of deadly satieties — in that day too these were evident and to be seen. On account of what were these miseries? Why was it that the Islamic society of that day, with all its scientific vitality and activity, could not be a flower in the garden of virtues and human qualities? Why?

Even now, when we range over the history of the second and third Islamic centuries, those whose names we can recall together with honor and pride, those whose names we can deliver to the world's high stations on the list of the honor-creating men of the world, are those who fought severely against that very civilized order. Those whose names we today can deliver with pride to the list of the honor-creating figures of the world of humanity are these men — and these, on that day, had no connection whatsoever with that splendid civilization that Mr. Gustave Le Bon recalls and names; rather, they were the very opposite of that civilization. So you see that when the wilāyah of the Tāghūt and of Satan holds sway over a society, over human beings, and holds the reins of their affairs in its hand, their energies are set to work, their capacities are set flowing — but in what manner? In the manner in which they have been set to work today in the civilized world, and in the manner in which they were set to work in the world of Islam ten centuries, eleven centuries ago. In the manner that, by the logic and authentic values and the human standards and measures, is not worth a copper penny — this is the wilāyah of the Tāghūt.

So then, within the wilāyah of the Tāghūt, can one live as a Muslim? What does living as a Muslim mean, sir? Living as a Muslim means that all the resources, energies, powers, strengths, and capacities of a human being be wholly and without reserve at the disposal of God. Everything of theirs: their wealth at the disposal of God, their lives at the disposal of God, their daily activity and movement at the disposal of God, their night's sleep at the disposal of God, their thought and reflection at the disposal of God. Can you give an example of this? Yes. We have examples both at the level of societies, in the form of a community and a civic order, and we have examples in groups; among the rebellious groups that came out of the Tāghūtī systems and fled and made _hijrah_ toward God — there we have examples.

The first — like the society of Medina, in the lifetime of the Prophet. In the lifetime of the Prophet, the society of Medina was a society of servants of God; it was a Muslim society. There, every step you took was in the way of God. There, even its Jew and its Christian, if he lived under the command of Islam, his life was an Islamic life. The Jew under the _dhimmah_footnote:s28-12[_Dhimmah_ lexically means safe-conduct and covenant. To the People of the Book who commit to the conditions of _dhimmah_ and are under protection on the part of the Islamic government, the term _ahl al-dhimmah_ (the protected people) is applied.] too, the Christian under the protection of Islam too — in the Islamic society they move along the path of Islam. From the standpoint of personal acts, he is a Jew; but from the standpoint of being a member of society, he is a Muslim. Far more a Muslim than that Muslim who lives within an ignorant order. In the time of the Prophet, money in the way of God, the sword in the way of God, the tongue in the way of God, thought and reflection in the way of God, every task of which a human being is capable was in the way of God; the affections and sentiments in the way of God; this is for a society. In the time of the Commander of the Faithful too, it was more or less the same way, because the Commander of the Faithful, may God's blessings be upon him, was, from the standpoint of being a divine ruler and the _walī_ of God, no different from the Prophet; but he was the heir of a bad society. He was the heir of disarrays, the heir of disorders; and even the Prophet himself, had he been in the place of the Commander of the Faithful and come twenty-five years later, would certainly have faced the very same difficulties as the Commander of the Faithful. This is in communities.

And as for groups: the group of the Shīʿah around the Imams, peace be upon them, throughout history. The month of Ramadan has come to an end, and we have not reached a detailed discussion of the Imamate; otherwise, in continuation of the discussion of wilāyah, I would have discussed the Imamate. Then, had I been able to conduct this discussion, I would have told you what kind of a unit the Shīʿah were in the time of the Imams. We would have shown what the Imam's, peace be upon him, relations and dealings with the Shīʿah were, and what the Shīʿah's relations and dealings with the society surrounding them were; this would have become clear. For now I will mention it in summary. The Shīʿah, outwardly, lived within the Tāghūtī system, but inwardly they moved precisely in the direction opposed to the Tāghūtī system. Like that very small group who were with Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī, may God's blessings and peace be upon him, at Karbalā. These had truly cleft the flood and come against the very direction in which the flood was steering them. So, then, in history we have certain examples — both in society and in groups. But ordinary individuals — in general the individual cannot be a Muslim; cannot have his whole being, his resources, his energies, all his powers and forces and capacities under the command of God, while living within such a society as I described earlier — the Tāghūtī society. Such a thing is impossible. If a person lives within a Tāghūtī environment and a Tāghūtī system, some fraction of his being a Muslim is, after all, in the way of the Tāghūt; some portion of his life is, after all, in servitude to the Tāghūt; he cannot be one hundred percent a servant of God.

A noble hadith has been related in _al-Kāfī_ in several wordings. The book _Uṣūl al-Kāfī_, which is among the most reliable and most ancient of Shīʿī books, has related this hadith in several phrasings; the one that I shall recite is one of those several phrasings. The gentlemen can refer to _al-Kāfī_, the _Kitāb al-Ḥujjah_; the title of its chapter, apparently, is this: _Bāb man dāna Allāh ʿazza wa jalla bi-ghayri imāmin min Allāh_footnote:s28-13[One who obeys God while under the sway of a ruler who is not from God.] (the chapter on whoever serves God while under a leader who is not from God). It says, on the authority of the Imam, peace be upon him, and the Imam on the authority of God, that God Most High has said:

[.hadith]
[quote]
____
«I shall surely punish every flock (_raʿiyyah_) in Islam that gave its allegiance (_dānat bi-wilāyah_) to an unjust leader (_imām jāʾir_) who is not from God, even if the flock was, in its own deeds, godfearing and pious; and I shall surely pardon every flock in Islam that gave its allegiance to a just leader from God, even if the flock was, in itself, wrongdoing and evildoing.»footnote:s28-14[Hadith no. 32.]
____

A remarkable hadith, this. This hadith says that those people who live under the wilāyah of the _walī_ of God are people of salvation, even if in personal and private affairs they sometimes have shortcomings, failings, and sins too; and those people who live under the wilāyah of Satan and the Tāghūt are people of wretchedness and torment, even if in personal affairs, in private deeds, they are people of good works and good deeds too. This is very remarkable. Of course, I have said that there are several phrasings, and all the phrasings yield this very same meaning. The hadith is in _Uṣūl al-Kāfī_. Just now I did not refer to it — it seems to me, since I am usually scrupulous about looking at the chains of transmission of the hadiths, I think its chain was a good chain too. Some time ago I saw it; just now I do not recall.

I always used to liken this hadith — I would say it is as though you boarded a car bound for a certain definite point, say, intending to reach Nishapur. If this car is heading toward Nishapur, you will certainly reach the goal. If this car, instead of going toward Nishapur, is going, say, toward Sarakhs or toward Quchan — well, it is certain that you will not reach the goal. Now, in that car that is going toward Nishapur and brings you to the goal, if in the car the people, with courtesy, kindness, and good conduct, with human manners, associate with one another — so much the better; and if they do not associate with one another with human manners and humaneness and goodness and beneficence — in the end they reach Nishapur, they reach the goal; even though along the way they did a little evil, those evils of course will have certain punishments, certain effects, certain consequences; they are compelled to bear those consequences too; but they reach the goal. Contrary to that: the car that is supposed to bring you to Nishapur is going to the point opposite Nishapur; if in this car all the individuals behave toward one another with courtesy, with refinement, with respect, deal with one another with good cheer, and they see that this car, instead of going to Nishapur, is heading toward Quchan, yet in the face of this event and this occurrence they show no reaction whatsoever — these are very good people, very kind to one another, but will they reach the goal or not? It is obvious — no.

In the first example, the driver was a trustworthy driver, a driver who knew the road — he was "_imāmin min Allāh_" (a leader from God) — he brought them to the goal, even though they were of bad character: "_wa in kānat al-raʿiyyatu fī aʿmālihā ẓālimatan musīʾatan_" (even if the flock was, in its deeds, wrongdoing and evildoing). In the second example, the driver was not a driver who knew the road; he was not a trustworthy driver; he was a passion-worshipping driver, drunk, he had lost the road, he had business in Sarakhs and Quchan, he had given precedence to his own want over the wish of the people, he was heading there; these will certainly not reach the goal — even though inside the car the people were very kind to one another, very warm, very good-natured: "_wa in kānat al-raʿiyyatu fī aʿmālihā barratan taqiyyatan_" (even if the flock was, in its deeds, righteous and godfearing) — but in the end, "_la-uʿadhdhibanna_" (I shall surely punish), they will fall into the torment of God, they will not reach the goal. Therefore, in a society administered by the wilāyah of the Tāghūt — like that very car steered by the driving of an untrustworthy man who does not know the road — human beings will not reach the goal and cannot remain Muslims.

What are they to do? What are they to do? Here the verse of the Qur'an gives the answer. The verse of the Qur'an gives the answer to this "what are they to do"; it says:

[.verse,quran,surah="al-Nisāʾ",s=4,a="97-100"]
[quote]
____
*Qur'an, Sūrat al-Nisāʾ (4:97–100):* "Indeed, those whom the angels take in death while they were wronging themselves..."footnote:s28-15[Sūrat al-Nisāʾ, verses 97 to 100.]
____

Those whose souls the angels seize while these wretches have been wronging themselves; those people who wronged their own souls, their own future, their own everything, and are now dying — the angels of God, the agents of the Lord, are seizing their souls. "_Qālū_" — the angels say to them — "_fīma kuntum_" — "In what condition were you? Where were you?" A person might imagine that the angel of heaven, when it sees that this man's condition is so wretched — like that man who has come, that physician, or that surgeon who has come to operate, say, on a patient, and sees that his condition is very bad, that this patient's condition is very lamentable and despair-inducing — says, "Wherever were you living? Why is your condition like this?" I draw this analogyfootnote:s28-16[_Istīnās_ (from the root _a-n-s_): familiarization; a metonym for bringing a concept near to the mind.]: that the angels, out of the wretchedness of this poor man's state, out of the badness of his soul, out of the misery and torment that await him, are astonished, and say to him, "Wherever were you living, you poor wretch? Where were you that you wronged yourselves so, and are now departing this world, departing this world as a wrongdoer against your own soul?" "_Qālū fīma kuntum_" — "Where were you? How were you?"

In answer they say — "_qālū_," they say — "_kunnā mustaḍʿafīna fī l-arḍ_": "We, in the earth, among the people where we were living, were among the oppressed and downtrodden (_mustaḍʿafīn_); we were part of the will-less mass." The _mustaḍʿafīn_ are that group within a society in whose hands the control of the society does not lie; these are the downtrodden. In the society's course and in its going, in its movement, in its repose, in its orientation, in its activity, they have no say. As I said yesterday, it goes wherever the pullers of the rope desire. So they go, the poor wretches, having no say of their own as to where to go and what to do.

Imagine a handful of small elementary-school children, first grade — and not even at the age of seven, for seven-year-old children today have their eyes and ears far more open than this — imagine a four- or five-year-old child placed in a school, a _maktab_, like the _maktabs_ of old. I recall: when we used to come out of the _maktab_ toward home, in a group, we did not even understand at all where we were going. Children do not pay attention to what is where. A monitor, say — some older child with a stick — "Go this way, go that way, don't go under a car," or "Do go under the car!" — sometimes he even wishes that this would happen (laughter from the audience) — they do not at all pay attention to where they are going; suddenly they find they are in their own house, say; they find they are in the house of some other student, say. Now if this monitor takes a fancy to lead them around the street a little, they find they are at such-and-such a place, say.

The _mustaḍʿafīn_ of the earth are those who, within a society, have no news of the goings-on of the society. They do not know what is where; they do not understand where they are going; and from here where they are going, where they will end up, and who is carrying them, and how one might avoid going, and, now that we have not gone, what we must do next — they understand none of it at all, they do not pay attention at all; just so, they have hung their heads down, like — with all due distinction, with all due distinction, with all due distinction — the millstone horse.footnote:s28-17[A horse that, in the old mills, by its own movement along a fixed circular path, set the mill turning.] The horse, with all due distinction, of course (laughter from the audience). They have blindfolded its eyes; it keeps pacing, keeps walking. It just keeps going, going, going; it goes round, goes round, goes round. If this animal were capable of understanding anything, at dusk it would imagine to itself that "by now we must be around Paris and thereabouts, we have come such a long way" (laughter from the audience). Then, when they uncover its eye, it sees it is the very same place where it was at first dawn. It does not at all know where it has gone; it does not understand where it is going while it is moving.

The _mustaḍʿafīn_ in a society are these — the majority, the uninformed mass of the people. Of course, in societies other than those administered by a sound system — not those societies that set a value on the jewel of humanity; not those societies that hold a dignity for the human being and for the human will; not that society whose leader is the Prophet, of whom, although he is the Prophet, the Qur'an says:

[.verse,quran,surah="Āl ʿImrān",s=3,a="159"]
[quote]
____
*Qur'an, Sūrat Āl ʿImrān (3:159):* "And consult them in the affair."footnote:s28-18[Sūrat Āl ʿImrān, verse 159.]
____

— even though he is the Prophet, even though he has no need to consult the people, he is nonetheless commanded to consult the people, to hold them dear, to deem them great, to give them personality. Not these societies — such a society does not have such a mass; but in those societies administered by an individualistic system, by a tyrannical system, by an ignorant system and the like, the majority of the people are _mustaḍʿafīn_.

They say: "_kunnā mustaḍʿafīna fī l-arḍ_" — "We were among the _mustaḍʿafīn_." They dragged us along so, carried us off, flung us down, trampled us, befouled us, and we did not understand; and now we are dying too. This is the excuse and answer of the _mustaḍʿafīn_. See what the angels say to them now. "_Qālū kunnā mustaḍʿafīna fī l-arḍ_" — they say, "We were oppressed in the earth; we were among the weak, the overpowered, the will-less, the powerless." The angels reply. See — it becomes clear that the logic of the angels is exactly equal to the reasonable, intelligible human logic; the human intellect too says this very thing. As that servant of God said seven or eight hundred years ago:

[quote]

_Set not your heart upon any one beloved, nor upon any one land,_ +
_For land and sea are wide, and human beings are many._

Saʿdī's view too was one with the view of these angels. The angels of the Lord say — "_qālū_," they say — "_a-lam takun arḍu Allāhi wāsiʿatan fa-tuhājirū fīhā_": "Why, man, you were oppressed there — but was the earth of the Lord confined to that one place? Was the whole world summed up in that one society in which you were living in a downtrodden way? Was the land of God not spread out, that you might go forth from this prison, go to a point of freedom? — to a point where you could worship God, to a point where you could put your energies to work along the right path, to a point where you would not be downtrodden? Was there no such place in the world?" "_Qālū a-lam takun arḍu Allāhi wāsiʿatan_" — "Was the earth of God not broad and wide?" "_fa-tuhājirū fīhā_" — "that you might make _hijrah_ in the earth of God and go to another place?"

They have no answer left to give. What can they say, the poor wretches — it is well known that a sound argument has no rejoinder. This is why the Qur'an, regarding the outcome and end of these wretches, speaks thus: "_fa-ulāʾika_" — those _mustaḍʿafīn_, those whose power and will were in the hands of the Tāghūts, who were carrying them off to Hell — "_fa-ulāʾika maʾwāhum jahannamu wa sāʾat maṣīran_": those, then — "their abode" — where is their dwelling-place? — "Hell, and what an evil journey's end" and final destination it is for a human being. This too is the end of the affair.

Of course, there is an exception. Not everyone is able to make _hijrah_; not everyone is able to rescue themselves from those bonds of the ignorant system. A certain number are incapable; a certain number are old; a certain number are children; a certain number are women for whom the possibility of this undertaking does not exist. Hence these are excepted: "_illā l-mustaḍʿafīna_" — except the oppressed and downtrodden, "_mina l-rijāli wa-l-nisāʾi wa-l-wildāni_" — among the men, women, and children — those weak and incapable ones who, "_lā yastaṭīʿūna ḥīlatan_" — who have no recourse, of whom no undertaking is possible, "_wa lā yahtadūna sabīlan_" — they find no way toward the region of light, toward the region of Islam and the worship of God; they find no way, they can do nothing — "_fa-ulāʾika_," then, these who can do nothing, "_ʿasā Allāhu an yaʿfuwa ʿanhum_" — it is to be hoped that God will pardon them. See, these are the very words of the Qur'an: it is to be hoped that God Most High will pardon them; this is how their case stands. "_Wa kāna Allāhu ʿafuwwan ghafūran_" — and God is ever Pardoning, possessed of forgiveness.

Then, so that those to whom the address of _hijrah_ is directed should not feel, should not imagine, that _hijrah_ will be a source of wretchedness and loss for them — "How will it turn out? Can we? Can we not? Will we reach some place, or not?" — in answer it says: "_wa man yuhājir fī sabīli Allāh_" — that one who makes _hijrah_ in the way of God — "_yajid fī l-arḍi murāghaman kathīran wa saʿatan_" — finds in the earth a vast field of movement and a spacious land. He sees, "What a flight one can make in the world! What a heart's-content soaring of wing one can do in the Islamic society! Until now our wing and our flight, when we soared very high, reached only to the ceiling of the cage. What horizons there are, how vast! In Mecca, the poor wretch prayed his prayer by force; if he was very keen, in the Masjid al-Ḥarām he prayed a two-_rakʿah_ prayer and then took a thorough beating into the bargain. The utmost of being a Muslim was this, no more than this. Then, when he made _hijrah_, when he came to the free land, to the free air, to the Islamic society, beneath the wilāyah of God and of the Messenger of God, he saw, 'What a place this is! Here they vie with one another toward good works (_yatasābaqūna ila l-khayrāt_); here, by the verse of the Qur'an and by piety and worship, the rank of individuals is distinguished and made known. Whoever moves more for God, worships more, strives more, performs _jihād_ more, gives more in charity — he is the more honored in this society.'"

Yesterday, if they had found out that one had given a single dirham in the way of God in Mecca, they would have heated the iron skewer red-hot and burned him with fire under torture. When you come and make _hijrah_ in the way of God toward the city of the Messenger (_Madīnat al-Rasūl_), O Muslim of the early era of Islam, then you see what a field of movement it is, what a place for flight it is, how a human being can soar his wing to his heart's content. "_Wa man yuhājir fī sabīli Allāh_" — whoever makes _hijrah_ in the way of God — "_yajid fī l-arḍ_" — that is, toward the divine and Islamic society — finds in the earth, in the way of God, "_murāghaman kathīran wa saʿatan_" — a vast field of movement, a place of abundant flight, and a spaciousness, an opening-up, a breadth.

Now, if you moved in the way of God from Mecca, from the abode of unbelief (_dār al-kufr_) toward the abode of emigration (_dār al-hijrah_); and along the way God took your soul, you died — how is it here? They say, "Then your wage and reward is with God, because you did your own part. The movement that it was incumbent upon you to perform issued from you."

Note this point: since today is the last day of the discussion of recitation and our Qur'anic study, this topic remains roughly half-finished. Let me state this point: _hijrah_ from the abode of unbelief, from the wilāyah of other-than-God, from the wilāyah of Satan and the Tāghūt — to where? To the abode of emigration (_dār al-hijrah_), the abode of faith (_dār al-īmān_), beneath the wilāyah of God, beneath the wilāyah of the imam; beneath the wilāyah of the Prophet and the divine _walī_ one must make _hijrah_ — this is _hijrah_. Now, if such a region did not exist in the world, what is to be done? Must one remain in the abode of unbelief? Or must one set about creating an abode of emigration? The Prophet himself, too, was among the emigrants, was he not? The Prophet too made _hijrah_; but before the Prophet made _hijrah_, an abode of emigration practically did not exist; the Prophet, by his own _hijrah_, created the abode of emigration.

Sometimes it becomes necessary that a group of human beings, by their own _hijrah_, inaugurate the starting-point of _hijrah_, found the divine and Islamic society, bring it into being, create the abode of emigration; then the believers make _hijrah_ to that place. The upshot of the matter — this is the substance of the discussion concerning _hijrah_.
