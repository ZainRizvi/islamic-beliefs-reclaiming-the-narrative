Now, the point that we wish to establish and explain today by means of these verses is this. After we came to understand that every Muslim, and everyone who claims to be a servant of God, must regard the master of his own life — the ruler and the one in charge of all the activity and striving of his entire lifespan — as coming from God, must seek that from God, must surrender his head to the _wali_ whom God appoints and designates, and must give the hand of obedience to the _wali_ of God. In short: in all the activities of life, one must recognize as governor and ruler only God, and whoever God has chosen to be His own deputy.@@CX(Wilayah | only God and His appointed wali may rule one's life)@@ Of course, we have already discussed this question too — who exactly are those whom God has chosen to be His deputies. We said that they are the prophets, and after the prophets, the _awliya'_ — the very ones whom you also know by the name of _awliya'_; that is to say, the divine rulers and governors.@@CX(Wilayah | God's deputies are the prophets and after them the awliya')@@ However, we said that the _wali_ and the divine ruler is designated in one of two ways: either with both name and description, or else not designated by name but designated by a description. These were the matters that became clear in the course of yesterday's discussion, and perhaps somewhat in the day before that as well.

The matter under consideration today is this: if someone does not accept the _wilayat_ of God and goes under the command of other than God, then, first, what is the ruling concerning this person himself? Second, what is the name of this act? Third, what is the outcome of this deed? These are the discussions that we said belong to the topics surrounding _wilayat_. Although, once we have accepted _wilayat_ in our minds and embraced it, it will then come to be counted among the established principles of practical Islam. Even though, within the framework of the discussion about the _principle_ of _wilayat_, these issues become secondary, peripheral, and subsidiary matters — yet in themselves they are matters of principle.

In the Noble Qur'an, every _wilayat_ other than the _wilayat_ of God has been introduced as the _wilayat_ of the Taghut. Whoever is not under the _wilayat_ of God is under the _wilayat_ of the Taghut.@@CX(Taghut | every wilayah that is not God's is the wilayah of Taghut)@@(((Taghut))) What does Taghut mean? Taghut is from the root _tughyan_.(((tughyan))) _Tughyan_ means to rebel, to transgress — to go beyond that natural and innate boundary and circle of a human being's life.@@CX(Taghut | meaning, from tughyan, to transgress one's natural boundary)@@ For example, suppose that humanity came into being in order to reach perfection; whoever causes humanity to fall short of reaching perfection is a Taghut. Suppose human beings ought to live by the religion of God — this is an innate matter, natural and in accordance with the very disposition of human beings. If someone brings people up in such a way, does something to them, exercises such control over them that they come to live by a religion other than God's, this person is a Taghut. A human being must always be in earnest effort and striving to bring his own existence to fruition. Any factor that encourages and incites a human being toward lack of seriousness, toward not striving, toward laziness, toward ease-seeking, toward seeking comfort and safety — that is a Taghut. Human beings must be obedient to the command of God; anything that draws a human being aside from the command of God, takes him out of it, causes a human being to rebel in the face of God — that is a Taghut.

So Taghut is not a proper name — as some imagine, that Taghut is the name of a particular idol. Yes, it is the name of an idol, but that idol is not one specific idol.@@CX(Taghut | not a proper name but any idol — self, money, comfort, a person, a law)@@ Sometimes that idol is you yourself; sometimes that idol is your money; sometimes that idol is your ordinary, comfort-seeking life; sometimes that idol is your object of desire; sometimes that idol is the person into whose hand you placed your own hand, closing your eyes and bowing your head so that he may take you wherever he takes you. Sometimes this idol is gold and silver — it is inanimate, it is metal; sometimes it is a human being — it is alive; it is a social order; it is a law. Taghut, therefore, is not a proper name. (Of course, in connection with the social classifications I mentioned in the discussion of prophethood — the way one infers it from among the Qur'anic verses — alongside the _mala'_(((mala'))) and the _mutrafin_(((mutrafin))) and the _ahbar_(((ahbar))) and the _ruhban_,(((ruhban))) the Taghut is the rank above all these. That is a different interpretation and a different place, which we are not discussing for now.) Therefore, anyone who has left from under the _wilayat_ of God has inevitably entered into the _wilayat_ of the Taghut and of Satan.@@CX(Wilayah | leaving God's wilayah means entering the wilayah of Taghut and Satan)@@(((Satan)))

What is the relationship between Satan and the Taghut? Do they have a relationship with each other? Yes — and they have more than a relationship. Satan is the very Taghut; the Taghut is the very Satan. The verse of the Qur'an says this. I want to recite the verse only for a verbal inference, so that it may become clear that Taghut is equivalent to Satan. The verse of the Qur'an says:

Then He says: "So fight against the allies of Satan" — fight, oppose, and combat the _awliya'_, the comrades, the adherents, and the dependents of Satan; "indeed, the plotting of Satan is weak" — for the scheme of Satan, the contrivance of Satan, the trickery of Satan, is weak.footnote:s27-1[Surat al-Nisa' / verse 76.]

In this verse you see that Taghut is named in place of Satan, and Satan in place of Taghut.@@CX(Taghut | identical with Satan — Qur'an names one in place of the other)@@ Satan is every element that, from outside the human being's existence, drives him toward wicked deeds, toward corrupting deeds, toward degradation, toward baseness, toward abjectness, toward oppression, toward evil, toward crookedness — this is called Satan.@@CX(Satan | defined as any element that drives a human toward corruption and evil)@@ We have satans among humankind, satans among the jinn; we have a satan among kin and women, a satan among the great, a satan among strangers, a satan among the emotions — this is what is called Satan. One of the instances and examples of it is Iblis,(((Iblis))) who, in the face of Adam,(((Adam))) the Chosen of God, stood up in opposition and independence, made his claim of superior knowledge, and said those words. Whatever curses you and I utter throughout our lifetimes, we curse that poor wretch, the first Satan — whereas Satan was not only him; perhaps it did not begin with him, nor did it end with him. There are satans without number in the world. They can even be touched, they come within reach, they come within sight; a human being is sometimes even in their company. Satan is Satan. Whatever drags a human being from the path of God toward corruption and wickedness and evil and degradation is Satan.

Every _wilayat_ that is not God's is a satanic and _taghuti_ _wilayat_. Whoever does not live under the command of the true _wali_ must know that he is living under the command of the Taghut and of Satan. You may ask: Sir, what harm is there in living under the command of Satan and the Taghut and submitting to his command? This too is one of the points of today's verses. In this regard the Qur'an gives us several answers.

The first answer is this: if you submit to the _wilayat_ of Satan, Satan will gain mastery over all the creative, generative, fruit-bearing energies of your existence. That is the first point. If you turn your neck toward Satan and the Taghut, so that he casts the rope of his _wilayat_ around your neck, you will no longer find any deliverance from his hand. Whatever exists within you — of strength, of initiative, of constructive activity, and of radiant manifestations — will come under the grip of the Taghut and of Satan. And once your entire existence has come into his grip, he can then easily drag you and take you along the very road that he himself wants, to the very place that he himself wants, by the very means that he himself wants. And it is plain that Satan and the Taghut do not guide a human being toward light, knowledge, ease, welfare, and spirituality. These things are not his aim. For Satan and the Taghut, his own personal interests are the primary aim, and he wants to secure them; so he puts you to work in the path of his own personal interests.@@CX(Taghut | gains mastery over all the creative energies of one who submits to it)@@

If you pay attention, this sequence I have laid out — these words and these sentences, which are carefully calculated, with a meaning beneath every sentence — you will see how greatly it corresponds with the historical realities, from the beginning of recorded history down to today. If you have submitted to the _wilayat_ of the Taghut, all your forces, energies, initiatives, and talents fall into the grip of the Taghut; and once they have fallen into the grip of the Taghut, they are not put to work for your benefit, because your benefit is of no concern to the Taghut and Satan. For Satan, he himself is what matters; his interests are what matter; his path is what matters. If, on that path and for those interests, you are sacrificed, then you are sacrificed; if you are led astray, then you are led astray. If, to secure those interests, it becomes necessary for you to be sacrificed, then you must be sacrificed; if it becomes necessary for you to be led astray, then you must go astray. He is Satan, after all; the power is in his hands, and you, who have given yourself into his hands, are dragged wherever it pleases him.footnote:s27-2[_A rope the Beloved has cast around my neck / He draws me wherever it pleases Him._ (Persian couplet quoted in the source.)]

This is the verse of the Qur'an — this verse that I am reciting for you from Surat al-Nisa' — a verse very worthy of attention and reflection. Even I myself, although I do not have the habit — that is, I am less in the habit of reading the Qur'an without attention and reflection — at times, when a greater reflection is applied and I find and sense something fresh in the Qur'an, I am all the more grieved that we still reflect so little upon the Qur'an. This verse, which we have written down here, which we have rendered, and which I will also explain now:

This — for this world of yours — and for that world of yours, what is the consequence? "And We will burn him in Hell"; from here, once you have closed your eyes, you will go straight to the Hell of the Lord's wrath and the everlasting torment of God. When a human being looks at history, he sees that it is exactly so. These matters are matters that are very important; these are important social matters. We have worked very little on the Qur'an from the standpoint of these matters, and we have applied them very little to the history of Islam.@@CX(the Qur'an | should be read for its social and historical meaning and applied to history)@@ And how good it would be if people who possess the gift of reflection — those interested in the Qur'an, and especially in the social and historical matters of the Qur'an — would reflect and ponder more; and then, if they would apply these to historical realities, it would become clear which interpretations and historical accounts conform to reality. This historical piece is an interpretation of this verse.

Of course, you know it is not my custom in gatherings of this kind to recount history; today I want to recount a bit of history. The city of Kufa(((Kufa))) is one of the most remarkable cities in the history of Islam. You hold several kinds of memories of Kufa. What I am about to say is nothing new concerning Kufa. You remember that Kufa is the place that the Commander of the Faithful, among all the Islamic lands and the cities attached to the great Islamic realm, chose for his caliphate. This is one positive point, as the gentlemen say. Again you remember that the people of Kufa took part in the wars of the Commander of the Faithful: they brought the Battle of the Camel(((Battle of the Camel))) to its conclusion; they finished the Battle of Nahrawan;(((Battle of Nahrawan))) in the Battle of Siffin(((Battle of Siffin))) too it was the tribes around Kufa and certain other tribes — and these very fighting men of that city — who brought that war to its conclusion, to where it reached. Again, in another place, you remember that it was these very people of Kufa about whom the Commander of the Faithful would complain and grumble: why is it that when I say, "Come to battle," you do not come? Again, it was these very people whose notables wrote letters and went into the service of Imam Hasan al-Mujtaba(((Hasan al-Mujtaba, Imam))) (the blessings of God be upon him): "Sir, come; we will hand this city over to you completely, at your disposal" — yet he did not come. Again, it was these very people whose notables wrote a letter to al-Husayn ibn 'Ali(((Husayn ibn 'Ali, Imam))) (peace be upon him): "Indeed, we have no Imam"footnote:s27-5[Hadith no. 27 — «انه لیس علینا إمام» ("Indeed, we have no Imam over us").] — we have no Imam, we have no leader, we have no ruler, and God has set this tyrant in authority over us; so you come. And they were telling the truth: Sulayman ibn Surad,(((Sulayman ibn Surad))) Habib ibn Mazahir,(((Habib ibn Mazahir))) Muslim ibn 'Awsaja,(((Muslim ibn 'Awsaja))) and others.footnote:s27-6[These three were counted among the notables of the Shi'a of Kufa. Habib and Muslim reached Karbala and were martyred fighting alongside Imam Husayn; Sulayman later launched the uprising of the _Tawwabun_ (the Penitents).] And again, it was these very people who came, in a most unequal and unbalanced battle, and lined up against al-Husayn ibn 'Ali (peace be upon him), and that catastrophe took place. Again, it was these very people who, a little later, brought about an episode in history that is among the very rare and glorious examples in the history of Islam: the episode of the _Tawwabun_ (the Penitents).(((Tawwabun))) They went out with the intent of going to sacrifice their lives and to repent for not having reached the episode of 'Ashura'((('Ashura'))) and the event of Karbala.(((Karbala))) And again, it was these very people among whom the seed of most of the revolts of the Umayyad(((Umayyads))) and the Abbasid(((Abbasids))) periods was sown, and sprouted, and came to fruition. How many martyrs they gave, how many slain they gave, how many splendid, brilliant, glory-bringing, glory-displaying deeds they brought forth from themselves! And again, it is these very people in whom — in certain affairs — you find slackness and laziness and weaknesses and the spiritual and intellectual incapacities that meet the eye.

What is this, then? Did these people have two kinds of character, two spirits, two faces? The very ones to whom Zaynab the Great(((Zaynab the Great))) says: "O people of Kufa, O people of deceit and treachery,"footnote:s27-7[Hadith no. 47 — «یا اهل الکوفة یا اهل الختل و الغدر» ("O people of Kufa, O people of guile and treachery").] recalling them as a treacherous folk.footnote:s27-8[Root gh-d-r: "wily, treacherous." (Lexical note.)] This is a problem; the matter of Kufa is a problem. In my view, a study of Kufa and a discussion of the psychology of the city of Kufa in history would be a very interesting discussion — if someone were equal to this task. Let the specialists, the sociologists, the psychologists sit down and talk about Kufa, think about it, discuss it, and see what a strange creature this is: in one place, all that astonishment in displaying the great manifestations of humanity; in one place, all this slackness and laziness and incompetence. What is it, then? Are the Kufans two classes? Does the society of Kufa have two aspects and two faces?

Kufa is a region that was so shaped and so tempered under the command of the dignified, eloquent words of the Commander of the Faithful, that whatever it nurtures, whatever it brings up green, is a sapling fit to be cultivated. This sowing-ground — its every shoot, its every nurtured thing, its every seedling — is altogether of fertile soil and life-giving climate; the very ground and air are human-making. That is why the great epic-creating men of the history of Shiism(((Shiism))) are, more than anywhere else, even more than Medina,(((Medina))) in Kufa. On account of what? On account of the teachings and the instilling of the Commander of the Faithful during those very four years. It is no trifle that someone like 'Ali ibn Abi Talib((('Ali ibn Abi Talib))) should govern in a city. It is true that this government, in the span of four years, at the level of the Islamic world was a failure; but at the level of Kufa it was certainly a success. In the society of Kufa it surely had wondrous and remarkable effects. Kufa thus became a cradle of Shiism, the birthplace of Shiite authenticities and virtues. Is that right?@@CX(Leadership | true leadership tempers a whole society, making Kufa the cradle of Shiism)@@

But wherever there is a birthplace of authenticities, it is not necessary that everyone who is there be authentic and virtuous and ideal people. One class of people are always, in a passionate and tumultuous society, the showcase of the passion and tumult of that society. Among millions of people, sometimes several tens of thousands, even several thousand human beings, perform some heroic deed of their own such that the name of those several million becomes fruitful in the world for heroism and passion-creating and epic-creating. There was one class among the people of Kufa who were striking. Not a class in the sociological sense, no — rather a stratum, an assembly, a group, a streak of people who were like this. But the mass of the people, the common people, were like the people everywhere else; they were no worse. Like the people of Mashhad, like the people of Tehran, like the people of Isfahan, like the people of Medina, like the people of everywhere else. But because that small group, in that corner of the Islamic realm — namely Kufa — were a source of dread and terror to the governments of the time, the governments would always appoint the worst elements, the vilest governors, the basest of their own men, their lackeys and their executioners, over that city; and they would do such things to those people — whether by way of severity and suppression, whether by way of poisonous propaganda, whether by way of spreading poverty and destitution among them — that the mass of the people would, unconsciously and unawares, develop a greater pull toward corruption and degradation.@@CX(Taghut | tyrants corrupt the masses by suppression, propaganda, and engineered poverty)@@

For whose sake did they do this? For the sake of that fighting group, that elite group, that existed in this city and did not exist in other cities. For the sake of crushing the ground that those pure, pure-natured, great human beings might have been able to make use of. So that they could not make use of it, the rulers would go and ruin that ground entirely. They would carry out poisonous propaganda, would put the people under pressure, would put them under suffocation, would weaken the people financially — in short, by all sorts of means they would place the people of the city of Kufa in conditions in which the people of other cities were not. And it was precisely on account of this that the common people, under the influence of the activity of the oppressive, treacherous, tyrannical apparatuses, performed certain deeds that were unbecoming; but their source was not the wickedness of the people of that city. This is a brief account concerning Kufa. In this field, if some people are able to think and study, in history, in my view they will come upon interesting things.

I want to explain the verse for you. Hajjaj ibn Yusuf(((Hajjaj ibn Yusuf))) was sent to Kufa by the Umayyad caliph 'Abd al-Malik,((('Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan))) because he knew that no one but Hajjaj was equal to the task of dealing with the rebellious, epic-creating people of Kufa. He sent the most cruel and the basest of his own lackeys, and that was Hajjaj ibn Yusuf. Hajjaj ibn Yusuf came; he entered the city in the middle of the night. No one realized that Hajjaj had arrived. The previous governor, too, the people had apparently expelled, or something like an expulsion. He had brought with him a hundred swordsmen, or thirty or forty men. (Today we want to recount history. When I recount history I tire less. These are the last days of the month of Ramadan; I am tired, you are tired too; we want to recount history.) He came and stationed his men around the mosque of Kufa, where the murmuring of the worshippers at the prayer-niches, the night-vigil keepers, and the pious could be heard. He himself slipped into a corner, off to one side, among the crowd. He gave the necessary instructions to his slaves and lackeys.

The people in the mosque of Kufa, at the first call to the dawn prayer, came and gathered to pray the dawn prayer with the imam. They came for worship and prayer. But it turns out that, as they should have, they were not worshipping with awareness. The proof of it is the very thing I am about to relate. Hajjaj ibn Yusuf came — without the people realizing that he had entered the mosque, or for what he had come and what his intention was. He came and slipped among the crowd, without attracting anyone's attention, and suddenly threw himself up onto the pulpit; he went up there, to the top. And you have seen how large the mosque of Kufa is. The people did not notice at first. And here he was, seated on the pulpit, without uttering a single word. Suddenly one person raised his head and saw that Hajjaj was on the pulpit — of course, he did not recognize him. He said: Who is this on the pulpit? He had also fashioned a strange appearance: he had wrapped around his head a turban of silk, red in color, and he had also bound a veilfootnote:s27-9[A _litam_ — a face-cover or veil.] over his face — that is, he had unwound the _taht al-hanak_footnote:s27-10[The continuation of the turban which, after being passed under the chin, is thrown over the shoulder.] of his turban and bound it up to the bridge of his nose, in such a way that only his eyes were visible. They saw that it was a strange figure: an armed man with a sword and a cloak and a red turban, seated this way on the pulpit, in the mosque of Kufa; and the silence drew the people's attention. This one said to that one, that one said to this one; suddenly all of this crowd that was scattered in the mosque — their eyes fell upon him and their attention was drawn. And little by little they were drawn toward the pulpit, to see who this was.

Pay close attention to what the verse of the Qur'an says: "We give him over to what he has turned to." It says that whoever turns aside from the path of faith and the believers — the bond that he has cast around his own neck, We draw tight around his throat. Well — you were a Muslim; on the pulpit of your own mosque a man you do not know is seated. Why do you go there and sit down? You should have gone up and said, "Sir, who are you? Introduce yourself!" The first person, the second person, the third person should have questioned him. Had they asked this question, the situation would have been different. They displayed slackness. They displayed a state of will-lessness and weakness of soul. They sat down so that he might speak.@@CX(Faith | requires vigilance — weakness of will lets the Taghut seize a community)@@

Then, when he saw that all the people were looking at him, he said: It seems the people of Kufa do not know me. The people glanced at one another — well, it was obvious they did not know him. He said: I will now introduce myself to you. He removed the turban from his head. This veil too was removed. He cast a glance at the people and recited a line of Arabic verse:

He said: O people of Kufa, I see heads that, like ripe fruit, hang upon the necks, and the time for their plucking has come. I see that it is necessary for a number of heads to be parted from these bodies. The people were all the more terrified by his pronouncements, which were hollow, which were empty.@@CX(Taghut | the tyrant's power is hollow; fear of it is the people's own undoing)@@ Well — Hajjaj had not gone to Kufa with an atom bomb; and even if he had had an atom bomb, he would not have detonated it, because if he had detonated it, no one would have remained for him to rule over.
