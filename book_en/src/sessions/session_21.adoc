== Session 21: The Outcome of Prophethood (2)

[.session-subtitle]
_فرجام نبوت (۲) — delivered Wednesday, 17 Mehr 1353 / 22 Ramadan al-Mubarak 1394_

[role="verse quran",surah="Ghāfir",s=40,a="51-54"]
[quote]
____
*Qur'an, Sūrat al-Muʾmin / Ghāfir (40:51–54):* "Indeed, We will surely help Our messengers and those who believe, in the life of this world and on the Day when the witnesses arise — the Day when their excuses will be of no benefit to the wrongdoers; and theirs is the curse, and theirs is the evil abode. And We had certainly given Moses guidance, and We bequeathed to the Children of Israel the Book — a guidance and a remembrance for those endowed with understanding."footnote:s21-1[Qur'an, Sūrat al-Muʾmin (Ghāfir), verses 51–54.]
____

Yesterday we said that God's promise to the prophets and messengers — His promise concerning their mission among the people — is a promise full of hope and glad tidings. That is to say, God the Exalted has promised to grant His prophets, and those who bear the trust of the prophetic mission, and likewise everyone who calls toward religion, truth, and reality — victory over their enemies both in this world, and in the next world a recompense, a reward, and a return. And the whole of what can be said about the victory of the prophets, peace be upon them, I summarized into two points.

The first point is this: when we examine the chain of the prophethoods of history from the first to the last, we see that, taken as a whole, these teachers of mankind succeeded and triumphed. It is true that some of them, in the course of their call — or even up to the very end of their call — met with certain failures and ingratitude on the part of the people. But when we reckon the matter as a whole, we see that the dear prophets, from the first of them to the last, accomplished the very work they had set out to accomplish.

I compared the prophets to teachers who wish to educate a student from beginning to end, one after another. Six teachers, ten teachers, fifteen teachers — from the lower grades up to the higher grades — they want to keep passing this child along, hand to hand, so that he may rise from the depths of ignorance to the summit of knowledge. Even though the first-grade teacher places before the feet of this child only a small portion of this road, of this path, and helps him in traversing it; even though the first-grade teacher does not live to see this student reach the peak of his perfection, and passes away before that — what is our judgment regarding the success or failure of that first-grade teacher? Because he was not able to bring this child all the way to the final stage, or because he was not able to see this student reach the final rank, do we say he failed? No. He did his own work; he handed the trust to the next person only after he had fulfilled his own share of the responsibility.

Another example we have always given in this regard is this: We say that a very heavy, exhausting load is to be carried from here to the far end of the mosque. This load is such that a single person cannot lift it onto his shoulders and carry it by himself, without anyone else's help, from here to the far end of the mosque. And it is also not possible for all of the people together to help and lift this load all at once and carry it to the end. One way remains, and it is this: that one of these several strong men who are gathered here, ready to lift this load, picks it up from here and carries it forward one meter; then a second person comes, takes the load from the one-meter mark and carries it to the two-meter mark; the third person does the same; the fourth the same; the twenty-seventh, the twenty-eighth, the thirtieth, the thirty-somethingth — and this load will reach the far end of this hall. For instance, from where I am now standing to over there is a distance of about thirty-three or thirty-four meters, say. This much distance, this road, was traversed; this load was carried. But each of these men who lifted this load was able to advance it only one meter — and with that single meter of advance, it may even be that he gave up his very life. So heavy is this load that if a person wishes to push it forward even a single meter, he is compelled to sacrifice his own life on this path — and indeed he did sacrifice it. Yet the sum total that we have here is this: one precious life given as a ransom for one stage of this road being traversed, and for one stage of the load being brought nearer to its destination.

The prophets are those very robust, powerful men, each of whom carried this load forward by one meter. Noah, peace be upon him, came and pushed this load — the load of guiding mankind and bringing the human race to the summit of culture and virtue — forward by one meter. Even though in that single meter he endured torments, even though he preached for nine hundred and fifty years and had only just advanced a little, even though in the end he gave his life over this work — did he, in the end, fulfill his duty or not? Did he bring this load one meter nearer to its destination, or not? You see that he did. After Noah, the prophet who comes after him takes this load from him and likewise advances it one meter. A third comes, and he too advances it one meter. The Seal of the Prophets, may God's blessings be upon him and his household, graces us with his presence and is commissioned to his mission; he takes this load — which had reached a certain appointed limit — and sets it upon a road where it rolls along on its rails and will move forward: humanity has reached maturity. Therefore the prophets, from the first to the last, succeeded.

And then, too, at the conclusion of the work — the last divine emissary, of whom we also wrote in these papers footnote:s21-2[Refer to the mimeographed handout at the end of the session.] — the matter of the appearance of the Awaited One (the Wali of the Age), may God's blessings be upon him, is itself a glad tiding to all the godly and to all the "national-minded" of the world in this regard. The last appointee of God, whom we consider to be our own Imam of the Age, and whom all the "national-minded"footnote:s21-3[_Milliyyūn_ — "the national-minded," partisans of the nation (literally, supporters of the _millah_/community).] of the world await — he will come and bring this load all the way to its final point. Whose work is the Imam of the Age continuing? He is continuing the work of Noah, the work of Abraham, the work of Moses, the work of Jesus, the work of our own Prophet, and the work of the pure Imams from the household of the Prophet. Had this load not reached its end, Noah would have failed. But because this load will reach its end — because he too had a share in bringing this load to its end — Noah is not a failure; Abraham is not a failure; Zachariah, whose body was sawn in two, is not a failure; that prophet whom they threw into the well footnote:s21-4[A reference to the prophet Joseph (Yūsuf), who was cast into the well by his brothers.] is not a failure. It is true that he fell into the well; it is true that in this world he did not see what he longed to see; it is true that Yaḥyā (John), peace be upon him, had his head severed for the tyrant of his age and was sent as a gift. All of this is true — but none of these men were defeated. Why? Because they bore these calamities and afflictions at the price of pushing forward the load of the trust and bringing that load nearer to its destination. This was one point that I made yesterday.

The other point I also touched upon yesterday is this: that in the final outcome, in the overall end of the work, we saw that defeat never awaits the prophets; in the end, all of them triumphed and succeeded, in the sense I have explained. But some of the prophets, besides that final triumph — bringing the load one meter nearer to its destination — beyond this also attained certain successes in this world. Those successes consisted in their being able to bring into existence a society based upon monotheistic thought and upon the school they proposed. The clearest instance is our own Prophet, who — upon the foundation of Islamic thought, the Qur'anic mode of thought, and the inspiration of God — brought into being a society, a polity (madīnah), a community, and an order. And of the same type as the Prophet were many of the past prophets, as I also mentioned yesterday. The conversion of the Roman Empire came after the departure of Jesus; the formation of the virtuous society of the Children of Israel came after the passing of Moses. Abraham, during his own lifetime, established the divine society — of this the Qur'an speaks. Solomon spread divine rule throughout the entire world. The Israelite prophet named Solomon, son of David, gathered the whole world into the form of a single monotheistic, divine society, around one another and around a single axis. So some of the prophets did attain certain successes and triumphs even during their own lifetimes, and these triumphs were summed up in what? In the establishment of a divine, monotheistic order and society.

Some of the prophets did not have this opportunity. Like whom? Like Zachariah, whom I gave as an example; like Yaḥyā, whom I gave as an example — they failed in their lifetimes; in the end they were killed; and like certain others among the prophets before Zachariah and before Yaḥyā. What is this? How is this matter to be analyzed and explained? Why do some of the prophets succeed and others fail? Why can they not all establish the divine and monotheistic society? And why do only some attain this possibility and this success? The answer lies in a single short sentence: everywhere that the divine men, leaders, and great ones won their successes, those successes were contingent upon faith and patience; and everywhere that the great divine leaders and the callers to truth and reality were defeated, it was the result of not having a sufficient measure of faith or of patience — without exception. Everywhere it has been just so. Wherever the companions of the prophet and the believers in him exercised patience in the struggle against the enemies, the opponents, and the adversaries of the prophet's call, they pushed forward.

The requisite for victory and progress is present in the call of the prophets — because the prophets speak in accordance with truth, they act in accordance with truth, and truth is victorious. Truth is in accordance with the nature and disposition of the world, and therefore it is successful and victorious. The prophets too are speaking in accordance with the disposition of the world; therefore the substance and the requisite of success in the movement of the prophets, in the revolution of the prophets, is completely present. If you see that some prophet in history was defeated, do not take this as proof that the word of truth must be defeated — no, the word of truth must be victorious, the order of truth must prevail, the hand of truth must strike falsehood upon the head and shatter it to pieces. So why, then, does a prophet sometimes meet defeat? Because the followers of the prophet either do not have sufficient faith or sufficient patience; they do not exert patience. Otherwise, the verse of the Qur'an — that very verse we recited yesterday — says:

*Qur'an, Sūrat al-Ṣāffāt (37:171–173):* "And Our word has already gone forth to Our servants, the messengers: that it is they who will be helped, and that it is Our host that will surely be the victors."footnote:s21-5[Qur'an, Sūrat al-Ṣāffāt, verses 171–173.]

Our host, Our army, Our people are the ones who prevail — this word of God is not subject to default. If somewhere you saw that those who utter the word of truth were defeated, know that they were not the host of God; the conditions for being the host of God were not present in them. Those others who arranged for that victory, who arranged for that defeat and prepared its ground — they were not the host of God. The Islamic society: on the day that it is the host of God, it pushes forward; on the day that it falls behind, it is not the host of God, it is not the army of God, it is not the troops of God.

The upshot is summed up in two words: the cause of defeats on the one hand, and of victories and triumphs on the other. Wherever the followers of the prophet brought about within themselves complete faith and complete certainty, and stood firm upon the word of truth, they assuredly succeeded and pushed forward; defeat had no place in their affair. Yesterday I related something, on the authority of one of the great ones of our own near-contemporary history,footnote:s21-6[A reference to one of the great figures of our own near-contemporary history, who counseled continued struggle even in the face of certain defeat.] who used to say to his friends: "When you are able, struggle. When you see that you are being defeated, struggle still — until you reach that moment when you become certain that, yes, you will surely be defeated — even then, struggle on. When, at the very moment that you are certain you will be defeated, you still continue your effort, your striving, and your struggle, then victory and triumph will be granted to you." A verse of the Qur'an says almost this same thing — or, indeed, says it definitively. It says:

[role="verse quran",surah="Yūsuf",s=12,a="110"]
[quote]

*Qur'an, Sūrat Yūsuf (12:110):* "…until, when the messengers despaired and thought that they had been denied, Our help came to them…"footnote:s21-7[Qur'an, Sūrat Yūsuf, verse 110.]
____

It reached a point where the pressures, the blows, and the strikes of the opposing front against the prophets were so severe that the prophets themselves grew hopeless. It reached a point where the prophets and the people who were with the prophets had their hearts shaken — not as regards faith; their faith did not vanish, they did not lose their belief in God — but because they had believed that they would be victorious, their faith and certainty in victory fell into decline, and they imagined that they had misunderstood. They had been certain that God had told them, "You will surely be victorious." The matter would reach a point where they began to think that perhaps they had misunderstood, that God had not in fact made them such a promise. _Wa ẓannū annahum qad kudhibū_ — "and they thought that they had been denied" — _jāʾahum naṣrunā_ — "Our help came to them": in that very moment when, from the intensity of the pressure and the severity of the enemy's blows and strikes, the front of truth and the front of religion felt that, yes, it was now surely being destroyed, it was perishing, the enemy was gaining the upper hand, the roads were closed upon it — in that very moment, because it still persevered, because it still strove, the help and succor of God arrived. This is the verse of the Qur'an.

We imagine that if, throughout history, some of the movements calling toward truth were defeated — if Zayd ibn ʿAlīfootnote:s21-8[Zayd ibn ʿAlī rose up during the imamate of Imam al-Ṣādiq and the caliphate of Hishām ibn ʿAbd al-Malik, as the first ʿAlid to take up armed struggle. His aim was to restore the lost rights of the House of Muḥammad to the chosen Imam of the House of Muḥammad. Zayd gathered companions for himself in Kufa and then raised the banner of revolt. Yūsuf ibn ʿUmar al-Thaqafī was commissioned to battle him. Before this confrontation, many of Zayd's companions abandoned the field, and in the end Zayd went into the arena of struggle with only a few companions and was martyred. Imam al-Riḍā said concerning Zayd: "He rose for the truth and was killed in the path of truth, and had he been victorious he would have restored the caliphate to its rightful holders."] was struck in the forehead by an arrow in the mosque of Kufa and fell; if Muḥammad ibn ʿAbdullāh, the one called "the Pure Soul" (al-Nafs al-Zakiyyah),footnote:s21-9[Muḥammad ibn ʿAbdullāh al-Ḥasan, known as "the Pure Soul" (al-Nafs al-Zakiyyah), was among the most excellent of the Banū Hāshim. In the closing days of the Umayyad state, the Banū Hāshim agreed among themselves to give allegiance to "the Pure Soul" as the Mahdi of this Ummah, but Imam al-Ṣādiq did not consent. After Manṣūr came to the caliphate, all the descendants of Imam al-Ḥasan and Imam al-Ḥusayn were taken captive and sent to Iraq and imprisoned in Kufa, where they died one by one in prison. On hearing this news, he rose up in Medina and expelled the governor of Medina from it. Near Medina he fought against an army sent by the caliph and was killed.] was defeated in his struggle against Manṣūr; if Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī al-Ḥasanī, the martyr of Fakhkh,footnote:s21-10[Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī ibn Ḥasan, one of the descendants of Imam al-Ḥasan, lived in Medina during the imamate of Imam al-Kāẓim. With the harshness of the Abbasid caliph and the governor of Medina, a group proposed that he rise up and pledged their support. He first rose up in Medina, and after taking the city set out for Mecca with three hundred men. Near Mecca, at a place called Fakhkh, he met the army of the Abbasid caliph and, after great effort, was martyred. His head and those of a hundred others were severed from their bodies and placed before the pilgrims in Mecca. Their headless bodies lay upon the ground for three days. Imam al-Jawād said concerning him: "After the event of Karbala, there was for us no slaughter-ground greater than the calamity of Fakhkh."] was killed near Medina together with all his companions; if Ibrāhīm ibn ʿAbdullāhfootnote:s21-11[Ibrāhīm ibn ʿAbdullāh, brother of the Pure Soul, at first took allegiance from the people in his brother's name; and when his brother was martyred, he called the people to himself. He rose up in Basra and quickly seized the city. A group of the people of Kufa invited him to that city. On the road to Kufa, Ibrāhīm encountered the army of the Abbasid caliph and was martyred.] was slain in Kufa or Basra — then this ought to produce in us a general lesson, that we say: wherever a movement of truth rose up against falsehood, it is condemned to defeat. A group of ignorant people, unaware of the logic of the Qur'an, hold this very notion. I said that this notion settles, like sweet water, into the hearts of the powerful and the tyrants of history. They ask of the God of both worlds that the people should assume and imagine things in this way. And of course it is evident that they themselves also promote this mode of thought. But this mode of thought is contrary to reality.

If Zayd ibn ʿAlī is martyred there, in that defeated state, this is no proof that truth is condemned to extinction and defeat; rather, it is proof that truth, even though it is truth, has need of work, effort, and striving. Let us not suppose that, because our word is truth, it is therefore unnecessary to strive on its behalf. Let us not think that, because our cause is the Qur'an, God Himself will therefore push the Qur'an forward — no! The word of truth, true, is truth; true, the world is destined and certainfootnote:s21-12[(s-l-m): _musallam_ — surrendered to, accepted, established as certain.] to accept it in the future — but it requires effort, it requires activity, a group must exercise patience for its sake, a group must eat the blood of their own hearts to enthrone it. The episode of Zayd ibn ʿAlī gives us this lesson; it does not give us the lesson that truth is condemned to extinction. Why do they err? Zayd ibn ʿAlī's word was true; Imam al-Ṣādiq, peace be upon him, had also endorsed Zayd ibn ʿAlī's struggle against the apparatus of Hishām ibn ʿAbd al-Malik — they had permitted him that wondrous struggle, and history speaks to this meaning. This noble man set out; he arranged matters well, too — only, a group of people: ignorant and unaware, or fault-seeking, or under the influence of the evil propaganda of a vigilant enemy — at the necessary moment forsookfootnote:s21-13[(kh-dh-l): _makhdhūl_ — rendered abject and humiliated, forsaken.] him and left him alone. This is the lesson we learn from that episode: that even if a word is true, even with the rightfulness of Zayd ibn ʿAlī's word, still, if his followers and supporters left him alone, if they did not exert effort and struggle on his path and at his side, they are defeated; and if they did struggle, they push forward. All the affairs of the world are just so.

How many people in the world do you know of — how many ideas, how many schools of thought — whose followers and friends, through their effort and struggle, enthroned that school of thought? How is it that false words, untrue words, words contrary to the way of the world and the nature of man, are enthroned through effort and struggle, yet the word of truth is not enthroned through struggle? What kind of talk is this? Yet some people do indeed say this very absurd, false thing.

The Commander of the Faithful (Amīr al-Muʾminīn), may God's blessings be upon him, makes this matter entirely clear in one of the sermons of the _Nahj al-Balāghah_ — in a short sermon. I did not, of course, intend today to go into great detail, and I will not go into great detail; I would rather explain these verses. But for now I will recount this sermon for you as well. It is a sermon I have read a number of times until now, in various gatherings, for the brothers who were present. The Commander of the Faithful describes the advances of the soldiers of Islam in the time of the Prophet. He says:

He says: when we were beside the Prophet and in the Prophet's ranks, this is how we would act. When we went onto the battlefield, we would slay our own fathers, our own sons, our own brothers, our own uncles who were in the front of unbelief and polytheism. If our uncle, our father, our son, our brother came under the banner of unbelief to war against the Prophet, we — who had gone forth with the Prophet — would not say, "This is my brother, let me not kill him; this is my son, let me not kill him." Without hesitation we mowed them all down in the way of God. And afterward, when we had killed them and were returning, our hearts were not shaken within us so as to cry out, "Alas, woe! Look what we have done! For the sake of this new religion, this new thought, we have killed our own beloved child!" — no. Our faith, as a result of this sharp and decisive deed, did not lessen; it increased. _Mā yazīdunā dhālika illā īmānan wa taslīman_ — "that only increased us in faith and submission" — this struggle of ours only added to our faith and our submission. _Wa muḍiyyan ʿalā al-ḥaqqi wa ṣabran ʿalā maḍaḍi al-alam_ — this struggle of ours caused us to press on more decisively upon the truth in difficult matters, and to exercise more patience over the pains and torments of the struggle.

Well, then the Commander of the Faithful proceeds to describe the battlefields there in brief. He said: _Wa jiddan fī jihādi al-ʿaduwwi. Wa laqad kāna al-rajulu minnā wa al-ākharu min ʿaduwwinā yataṣāwalāni taṣāwula al-faḥlayni yatakhālasāni anfusahumā: ayyuhumā yasqī ṣāḥibahu kaʾsa al-manūn_ — which I do not wish to expound — namely, how our soldier in battle would leap upon the enemy, how he would grapple and lock with him, how our soldiers and the brave warriors and champions of Islam would race one another toward death — these I will leave aside. Then, at the very end of this same sermon — which is also short — he says:

_Fa-lammā raʾā'llāhu ṣidqanā anzala bi-ʿaduwwinā al-kabta wa anzala ʿalaynā al-naṣr_ — "So when God saw our truthfulness, He sent down humiliation upon our enemy and sent down victory upon us."

We struggled to such an extent that God saw that we were speaking truthfully, saw that we were truly Muslims, saw that we were truly believers — that we demonstrated our deep faith by our deeds; and when we acted in this way, then the crushing of our enemies, and victory and triumph for us, descended upon us from God. He utters one, two, three further sentences, and then he says:

[role="hadith"]
[quote]

_Wa la-ʿamrī law kunnā naʾtī mā ataytum mā qāma li-l-dīni ʿamūdun wa lā-khḍarra li-l-īmāni ʿūdun_ — "And by my life, had we done what you are doing, not a single pillar of the religion would have stood, and not a single branch of faith would have grown green."
____

The Commander of the Faithful says this during the time of his caliphate, when he was confronting a handful of lazy, comfort-seeking men who, when he said, "Come, let us go to the war against Muʿāwiyah," would make excuses; who, when it was time to go to the war against Ṭalḥah and Zubayr, would fabricate a thousand kinds of religious justification for their own staying behind. In short, comfort-seeking, life-seeking, ease-seeking people, far from divine knowledge — it was with such men that he was dealing. Weak, base, slack, abject, surrendered to humiliation, accustomed to comfort — with such men the Commander of the Faithful was contending. He says to them, _Wa la-ʿamrī_ — "by my own life I swear" — _law kunnā naʾtī mā ataytum_ — "had we, in that day with the Prophet, done the very thing you are doing today," O Muslims, _mā qāma li-l-dīni ʿamūdun wa lā-khḍarra li-l-īmāni ʿūdun_ — not one pillar among the pillars of the religion would have stood erect, not one branch among the branches of faith would have grown green.

What do you understand from this sermon? The word of the Commander of the Faithful — which is nothing other than the word of the Prophet, for the path that ʿAlī trod is the very path that his brother the Prophet trod — why, in that day, did the Prophet of God advance so, and why, in the time of the Commander of the Faithful, did affairs come to such a standstill? Why? The Commander of the Faithful is stating its secret. He says: because in that day we exercised patience on the battlefields; in that day we exercised patience over the burning of pain; in that day we were ready to come out of our beds onto the battlefield; in that day we were ready to forgo our material interests and our livelihoods for the sake of God — but today you are not ready. In that day we advanced; today we fall behind. A very simple, clear matter; two times two is four. This is the social analysis of the Commander of the Faithful.

At any rate, the summary of the matter comes to this: that the divine prophets, besides the fact that in the chain of prophethoods their work is accompanied by victory, and that at the conclusion of the work the eternal and final triumph and outcome belongs to them — even in this very world the prophets are certain to attain victory and triumph and to achieve their own aims and the aims of their school — but on condition that their followers, their companions, bring faith, exert real faith, render aid, and exercise patience on the fields of struggle and jihad. On condition of faith and patience — this is the upshot of the discourse.

Now let us return to the verses of the Qur'an. Today I have chosen verses from several places in the Noble Qur'an — the written text of which will, of course, reach your hands tomorrow — and I saw that there are even more verses of glad tiding in the Qur'an than these. One is in Sūrat al-Muʾmin (Ghāfir), where He says — and there is no need for me to read those verses here now:

[role="verse quran",surah="Ghāfir",s=40,a="51"]
[quote]
____
*Qur'an, Sūrat al-Muʾmin / Ghāfir (40:51):* "Indeed, We will surely help Our messengers and those who believe, in the life of this world and on the Day when the witnesses arise."
____

Allow me to read them, there is no harm in it; I would regret not explaining these verses today, since tomorrow too I will not have time to explain them. Sūrat al-Muʾmin, verse 51, according to this Qur'an. _Innā la-nanṣuru rusulanā wa alladhīna āmanū_ — "Indeed, We will surely help Our messengers and those who believe."footnote:s21-15[Qur'an, Sūrat al-Muʾmin (Ghāfir), verses 51–55.] The _lām_ in _la-nanṣuru_ is for certainty, verification, and emphasis; and _innā_ too is in this same sense of verification and emphasis. That is to say: We, definitively and without any doubt, help — _rusulanā_, "Our messengers." Only the messengers? No — _wa alladhīna āmanū_, "and those who believe," the believers as well. The believer who moves along the path of the prophet — this promise is for him too. The pure-natured human beings who set out following the call of the prophets — this promise is for them too: _wa alladhīna āmanū_. Where do We help them? _Fī al-ḥayāti al-dunyā_ — "in the life of this world": right here, in this very worldly life, We help them; We do not leave it for later. _Wa yawma yaqūmu al-ashhād_ — "and on the Day when the witnesses arise." This — that the witnesses arise — is in some commentaries interpreted as the Day of Resurrection.

There is a tradition concerning this noble verse in which the Imam, peace be upon him, said to Jamīl ibn Darrāj — according to that tradition — that what is meant by God's help to the prophets is their being helped in the world of the Return (rajʿah).footnote:s21-16[Imam al-Ṣādiq, in _Tafsīr Nūr al-Thaqalayn_, on verse 51 of Sūrat al-Muʾmin.] That is to say, after the Wali of the Age, may God's blessings be upon him, comes, and that all-encompassing divine government comes into being in this world, and the banner of the Qur'an and Islam is planted across the entire world, and all the people move toward religion, God, and monotheism, and the single divine government comes into being — after that, God the Exalted brings back to life the prophets, the messengers, the executors of the prophets (awṣiyāʾ), the martyrs, and the righteous among the believers — in accordance with verses of the Qur'an interpreted to this meaning, and traditions that are explicit on this meaning. I will not now go into the discussion of the Return. In this narration the Imam says that this is for the Return — this verse is connected to the Return, and the help is for the Return. I myself imagine that the Imam, peace be upon him, does not wish to interpret this phrase — _fī al-ḥayāti al-dunyā_ — as referring to the Return. The Imam does not wish to say that what God has stated — that We help them in the life of this world — means "in the Return within the life of this world." I think that _yawma yaqūmu al-ashhād_ is being interpreted as the Return, and not as the Resurrection — for what helping is there in the Resurrection? In the era of the Return, God the Exalted helps the prophets. This I offer as a probability, and I draw this inference from this narration only as a probability. At any rate,footnote:s21-17[_ʿAlā ayyi ḥāl_ — "in any case, at all events."] even if we set aside this benefit altogether, the phrase _fī al-ḥayāti al-dunyā_ itself, on its apparent meaning, and by the indication of the verses that come afterward as well, shows that God the Exalted gives an explicit promise to the prophets and to the believers that, in this very worldly life, We will help you.

[quote]
____
*Qur'an, Sūrat al-Muʾmin / Ghāfir (40:52):* "The Day when their excuse will not benefit the wrongdoers; and theirs is the curse, and theirs is the evil abode."
____

_Yawma yaqūmu al-ashhād_ — who is it that arises? When do the witnesses arise? At that time when the apology of the wrongdoers will be of no benefit to them. There is another verse in the Qur'an:

[role="verse quran",surah="al-Anʿām",s=6,a="158"]
[quote]
____
*Qur'an, Sūrat al-Anʿām (6:158):* "…the Day when a sign of your Lord comes, no soul will benefit from its faith if it did not believe before…"footnote:s21-18[Qur'an, Sūrat al-Anʿām, verse 158.]
____

This too is interpreted as the era of the coming of the Wali of the Age — it is possible this is the same. _Wa lahumu al-laʿnah_ — the curse is upon the wrongdoers. _Wa lahum sūʾu al-dār_ — theirs is a most ill-omened, evil abode.

Then, to bring a witness to the fact that We help the prophets, He cites the episode of Moses; for since, before this verse, from the early part of Sūrat al-Muʾmin, the discourse is mostly about Moses and his struggles against Pharaoh, here too — where He gives the promise of help to all the prophets — He says, as one type, one instance, one example:

[role="verse quran",surah="Ghāfir",s=40,a="53-54"]
[quote]
____
*Qur'an, Sūrat al-Muʾmin / Ghāfir (40:53–54):* "And We had certainly given Moses guidance, and We bequeathed to the Children of Israel the Book — a guidance and a remembrance for those endowed with understanding."
____

_Wa laqad ātaynā Mūsā al-hudā_ — "We gave Moses guidance and right-guidedness." _Wa awrathnā Banī Isrāʾīla al-kitāb_ — "and We bequeathed the Book to the Children of Israel as an inheritance." The Children of Israel remained possessors of the Book, possessors of the collection of divine knowledge and ordinances — and this shows that they succeeded; otherwise, had the unbelievers and tyrants of the age prevailed over the Children of Israel, they would not have allowed them to act according to their own heavenly Book; they would have destroyed the Book from among them. _Hudan wa dhikrā li-ulī al-albāb_ — that this Book is guidance, is right-guidance, and is awareness for those endowed with intellect and reason.

_Fa-ṣbir_ — then, when this matter is concluded, He addresses the Seal of the Prophets, saying: _Fa-ṣbir_ — "so be patient, persevere, resist the impulses that bring about decline." _Inna waʿda'llāhi ḥaqq_ — without doubt, God's promise is true. This promise that God has given — that He has said you will be victorious; that promise which was in Sūrat al-Ṣāffāt: _Wa laqad sabaqat kalimatunā li-ʿibādinā al-mursalīn, innahum lahumu al-manṣūrūn_ — and likewise the other promises that are in the Qur'an — these are true, these are correct. You will surely be victorious, O Prophet, in accordance with the divine promise; but the condition for it is patience. _Fa-ṣbir_ — you must be patient, you must resist and persevere, you must not turn back from the sacred contest you have undertaken.

[role="verse quran",surah="Ghāfir",s=40,a="55"]
[quote]
____
*Qur'an, Sūrat al-Muʾmin / Ghāfir (40:55):* "So be patient — indeed, the promise of God is true — and seek forgiveness for your sin, and glorify your Lord with praise in the evening and the morning."
____

_Fa-ṣbir inna waʿda'llāhi ḥaqq, wa-staghfir li-dhanbik_ — "and seek forgiveness for your sin." Of course, the Prophet's sin, the sin in the Prophet's category, is not of the type of our sins. The Prophet is infallible — this is certain. By the explicit text of the Qur'anic verses and by the judgment of reason, the Prophet does not sin. This "sin" is of the kind of sins and lapses that, for a human being of that station, count as a lapse — even though, if you and I were to do the very same thing, it would be no lapse at all. It is those sins He speaks of, those lapses He speaks of. _Wa-staghfir li-dhanbik_ — ask pardon, seek forgiveness, and seek to make amends for your small lapses of the past. _Wa sabbiḥ bi-ḥamdi rabbika bi-l-ʿashiyyi wa-l-ibkār_ — "and glorify, and praise your Lord, in every evening and every morning." This verse teaches us very clearly that the outcome of the prophets' work is the help of God.

And now as for the verses of Sūrat al-Anbiyāʾ — that too, again, in the same way, I will read briefly. Sūrat al-Anbiyāʾ is in the seventeenth juzʾ, after Sūrat Ṭā Hā. The verses of Sūrat al-Anbiyāʾ are excellent. I recommend to those friends who have intimacy with the Qur'an to read Sūrat al-Anbiyāʾ carefully. From the beginning of the sura, God the Exalted is constantly saying that the prophets will be victorious, the enemies of the prophets will be crushed, and in this world too — before the next world — they will be punished; this He keeps saying, constantly, from the start of the sura. Then, after repeating these matters somewhat, He turns to history. He recounts the story of Moses — the victory of Moses and the defeat of the power opposing Moses; He recounts the story of Abraham — the victory and success of Abraham and the defeat of the powers opposing Abraham; the story of Noah, the story of Solomon, and other stories. All the episodes recounted in this sura are in this form: the prophet advances, succeeds, is victorious; and the anti-prophet, the counter-revolution against the prophet, the opposition to the prophet's new word — that is, the reaction that opposes prophethood — is overcome, is subdued, is defeated. And this is the way (sunnah) of history.

He says:

[role="verse quran",surah="al-Anbiyāʾ",s=21,a="7"]
[quote]
____
*Qur'an, Sūrat al-Anbiyāʾ (21:7):* "And We did not send before you any but men to whom We revealed…"footnote:s21-19[Qur'an, Sūrat al-Anbiyāʾ, verses 7–18.]
____

— these are the verses at the beginning of the sura — He says: before you, We sent prophets and gave them revelation. _Wa mā jaʿalnāhum jasadan lā yaʾkulūna al-ṭaʿām_ (21:8) — "We did not make them bodies that did not eat food": We did not make them into angels having no body, who require no food. _Wa mā kānū khālidīn_ — _khālidīn_: nor were they immortal, eternal, everlasting. They too died at some point. But — _thumma ṣadaqnāhumu al-waʿd_ (21:9) — "then We fulfilled for them the promise": We made Our promise to them come true, the promise of victory. _Fa-anjaynāhum wa man nashāʾ_ — "We saved them and whomever We willed": We saved them, and whomever We willed We saved along with them. _Wa ahlaknā al-musrifīn_ — "and We destroyed the transgressors": We destroyed the transgressors. _Laqad anzalnā ilaykum kitāban fīhi dhikrukum, a-fa-lā taʿqilūn_ (21:10) — "We have sent down to you a Book in which is your remembrance": now to you too, O believers, We have sent down a Book, a Qur'an, in which is the remembrance, the awareness, and the reminder of you. _A-fa-lā taʿqilūn_ — "do you not, then, use reason? Do you not reflect?"

Then He arrives at verse eleven, and He says:

[role="verse quran",surah="al-Anbiyāʾ",s=21,a="11"]
[quote]
____
*Qur'an, Sūrat al-Anbiyāʾ (21:11):* "And how many a town that was wrongdoing have We shattered…"
____

_Wa kam qaṣamnā min qaryatin_ — this is truly a hymn of the victory of the prophets, a stirring anthem that shows how the prophets, with the divine, unseen aids that lie within the womb of this world — and not unseen aids of the sort that the common people wish for and love, that a hand should come from the unseen and strike the chest of the enemy; no — unseen aids that are concealed within the very womb of this world, in keeping with the disposition of the world and the nature of man and the cosmos: with these unseen aids, how greatly did the Lord of the world grant the prophets success. _Wa kam qaṣamnā min qaryatin_ — how many, and how often, did We shatter — did We shatter the settlement and society that _kānat ẓālimah_, that was wrongdoing. The wrongdoing society, the wrongdoing order, the wrongdoing civilization — that society in whose very construction injustice has been employed, in which class divisions have arisen, in which exploitation has been established, in which the exploitation of man by man has been set up; this is "the wrongdoing town," this is "the wrongdoing society," in whose foundation tyranny has been employed — how many of these did We shatter! _Wa kam qaṣamnā_ — that is, "how many did We shatter, We crushed." _Wa qaṣamnā_ means "We shattered, We pulverized." _Min qaryatin kānat ẓālimatan wa ansh'anā baʿdahā qawman ākharīn_ — "and after them We raised up another people": and after them We brought into being and made manifest another people, another group, another class, and set them to the work.

[role="verse quran",surah="al-Anbiyāʾ",s=21,a="12-15"]
[quote]
____
*Qur'an, Sūrat al-Anbiyāʾ (21:12–15):* "Then, when they perceived Our might, at once they fled from it. [It was said:] Do not flee, but return to the luxury you were given and to your dwellings, that you might be questioned. They said: Woe to us, indeed we were wrongdoers! And that did not cease to be their cry until We made them stubble, extinguished."
____

_Fa-lammā aḥassū baʾsanā_ — a description of the misery of the wrongdoers at the moment they behold the punishment of God. _Fa-lammā aḥassū baʾsanā_ — the very moment those wrongdoers sensed Our wrath and anger — whether, for instance, suppose a heavenly punishment descended, or whether they saw that the believers, alongside their prophet, had charged upon them and were about to bring down the sword of God's wrath upon their heads — the moment they sensed Our wrath, _idhā hum minhā yarkuḍūn_ — suddenly, from those settlements, from that civilization, from that society, they took to flight, they set to fleeing and running away. _Lā tarkuḍū_ — "do not flee"; _wa-rjiʿū ilā mā utriftum fīhi_ — "and return to the luxury you were given": return to your places of luxurious ease, return to those palaces in which you lived in luxury, return into the midst of the society and city in which you lorded over others and boasted — where are you fleeing to? _Wa-rjiʿū ilā mā utriftum fīhi wa masākinikum_ — "and to your dwellings." Return to your dwellings; _laʿallakum tusʾalūn_ — "that you might be questioned": perhaps they wish to interrogate you, perhaps they wish to question you — where are you going? _Qālū yā waylanā innā kunnā ẓālimīn_ — they said, "Woe to us! Woe to us, for we were oppressors and wrongdoers." _Fa-mā zālat tilka daʿwāhum_ — this remained their cry, their speech; _ḥattā jaʿalnāhum ḥaṣīdan khāmidīn_ — "and they kept on speaking in this way until We made them all prey to death and annihilation."

Pay attention. There are two or three more verses; let me read these. These following verses, in truth, state the intellectual foundation of this historical reality. Why did things become this way? Why must the wrongdoers be destroyed and the oppressed take their place? Why must the call of the prophet surely advance and his opponents be crushed — why? The cause is this:

[role="verse quran",surah="al-Anbiyāʾ",s=21,a="16-17"]
[quote]
____
*Qur'an, Sūrat al-Anbiyāʾ (21:16–17):* "And We did not create the heaven and the earth and what is between them in play. Had We wished to take a diversion, We would have taken it from within Ourselves — if We were to do so."
____

_Wa mā khalaqnā al-samāʾa wa-l-arḍa wa mā baynahumā lāʿibīn_ — We did not create the heaven and the earth and what is between them in vain. _Law aradnā an nattakhidha lahwan la-attakhadhnāhu min ladunnā in kunnā fāʿilīn_ — had We wished to do a frivolous thing, We would certainly have done it; We would have done it of Our own self — if We were the doers of such a thing — but We never do. The Lord of the world does not do a vain thing, does not act in accordance with falsehood, does not act in futility. What does this mean? It means that this heaven and earth, and what is between them — if We created them, We created them according to a purpose; We created them for the attainment of an aim and a goal. Truth (ḥaqq) means the line of progression of this heaven and earth, and of whatever is in this heaven and earth, toward that goal — that is, truth. That path which brings the heaven and the earth and their creatures to that destination, that aim and goal for which they were created — that line of progression is truth. Any means that brings human beings to that destination — that means is truth. These things are not made explicit in the Qur'anic verse; this is the import of the verse. That is, by reflection upon the verse, it is quite clear; and the foundation of the Qur'an is this: that mostly, many of the things which are understood and clear and which the human intellect arrives at, it does not bother to make explicit.

Then He continues, saying:

[role="verse quran",surah="al-Anbiyāʾ",s=21,a="18"]
[quote]
____
*Qur'an, Sūrat al-Anbiyāʾ (21:18):* "Rather, We hurl the truth against falsehood, and it crushes it, and at once it vanishes. And woe to you for what you describe."
____

_Bal naqdhifu bi-l-ḥaqqi ʿalā al-bāṭili_ — rather, We strike falsehood with the means of truth. This path of truth, this correct way, this innate and natural way of man and the world, will in the end be victorious over falsehood; rather, We make the truth prevail over falsehood. _Fa-yadmaghuhu_ — so this truth utterly effaces and annihilates falsehood. _Fa-idhā huwa zāhiq_ — then you see falsehood as it perishes, heading toward annihilation. _Wa lakumu al-waylu mimmā taṣifūn_ — then for you, O wrongdoers, O oppressors, O followers of falsehood and deniers of truth, is woe; for you is wretchedness and ruin. _Wa lakumu al-waylu mimmā taṣifūn_ — woe to you for what you have described and asserted.

Then, of course, He continues with a most interesting and beautiful Qur'anic style — which requires that a person become intimate with the Qur'an in order to grasp these interesting and beautiful subtleties of the Qur'an correctly. Mostly, those who do not perceive these delicacies, these fine points and refinements of the Qur'an, have no intimacy with the Qur'an. If a person becomes intimate with the Qur'an, the tone of the Qur'an's speech becomes familiar to his ear, and he understands how the Qur'an is speaking — He discusses and speaks about this matter: the coming of truth, the going of falsehood, the victory of truth, the defeat of falsehood. Then, regarding the creation of the heaven and the earth, He once again takes hold of the point that the Lord of the world is the owner of the heaven, the owner of the earth, the ruler over all the regions of the world of existence. Therefore, in the life of human beings too, He must hold sway; in the system of human life too, He must give the law, He must administer, He must govern. Therefore, those who, in opposition to the prophets, laid claim to government, power, and dominion, were upon falsehood and are condemned to annihilation. I ask you, gentlemen, please remain seated.

By Muḥammad and the household of Muḥammad, draw their kindness and their love toward us.
