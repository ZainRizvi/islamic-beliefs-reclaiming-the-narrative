The subject of our talk today is this: the prophet and messenger — at the moment the burden of messengership (risālat) and prophethood (nubuwwat) is placed upon his shoulders, after he receives the message of God — what state, what condition, comes into being within him and in the world around him? It is, of course, clear that we are not greatly concerned with the inner qualities and conditions of the prophet himself; if we do raise the matter here, it is in order to draw a subtle, precise benefit from an ordinary, common word that is present in the Qur'an and in the usage of the religiously learnedfootnote:s15-2[Aware of and committed to religious and legal (sharʿī) matters.] — a word that, incidentally, illuminates for us one dimension of the question of prophethood.

You know that in the Qur'an, whenever there is talk of a prophet's coming, generally the talk is of his bi'that — his being commissioned and sent forth. As the verse says:

We commissioned Moses(((Moses))), Abraham(((Abraham))), and the other prophets. Bi'that(((Biʿthah))) — what does this bi'that mean? And what is the relationship between bi'that and prophethood?@@CX(the Biʿthah | a bi'that exists within prophethood)@@ You will notice that the title of today's discussion, written on those papers in the possession of you brothers and sisters, reads: "The Bi'that in Prophethood." Within prophethood there is a bi'that. What does bi'that mean? What is this bi'that? If within prophethood there is a bi'that, to whom does this bi'that belong, what is it, what is its benefit, and so on?

Bi'that means a being-roused, a being-stirred-up. What does being-roused mean? It means motion, movement after lethargy, slackness, and stagnation.@@CX(the Biʿthah | means being-roused — motion after stagnation)@@ A dead person who has lain for years in some graveyard, whose bodily parts have become dust, when by the power of the Lord he rises up on the Day of Resurrection — they say he has been raised up. As the verse says:

[role="verse quran",surah="al-Rūm",s=30,a="56"]
[quote]

*Qur'an, Sūrat al-Rūm (30:56):* "This is the Day of the Raising-up."footnote:s15-4[Sūrat al-Rūm (30), verse 56.]

It is the day of being-roused. A human being who had been asleep in his house, or who in the ordinary course of social life merely moved along — in whom there was no fervor, no striving, no activity, nothing contrary to the customary state of society; who was in reality like a plank of wood, like a lifeless plant, like a wisp of straw cast upon the ordinary flood of society, which carried him this way and that — such a person, when he comes to himself, when he emerges from this state of indifference, when he senses that this path, this natural current, this ordinary stream, need not necessarily be one hundred percent reassuring and convincing to him; that perhaps another current can be sought out which will bring that human being to the destination of felicity — when, as a result of these thoughts, the human being awakens, becomes alert, finds striving, finds movement — this human being, they say, has been raised up. In smaller, more particular examples: when you awaken from sleep and rise, you say "he was roused." When you come out of slackness and laziness and listlessness and lethargy and busy yourself with movement, they say "he was roused." This is the meaning of being-roused; ba'th means this.

The Day of Resurrection(((Resurrection, Day of))), as you know, is called "Yawm al-Ba'th," the day of being-roused, or in another expression, the Day of Rising. The Rising — that day of standing up, that day of coming out of slackness and listlessness, that day of movement, the day on which human beings, from the moment they emerge from the grave until the moment their final destiny is determined, are continually in striving, in agitation, in movement; this is what they call Yawm al-Ba'th, the Rising. In prophethood there exists exactly such a state.

I want your vision regarding prophethood(((Prophethood, nubuwwah))) to become a fundamentally new vision.@@CX(Prophethood | demands a fundamentally new vision)@@ Some imagine prophethood in this way: as though a preacher enters a city to expound for the people of that town a few matters of religion or of something other than religion; or, let us suppose, a teacher of religious rulings rises up among a people and stands to tell them a few subsidiary legal questions; or some orator, some speaker — let us suppose a rabble-rouser — enters a gathering to stir up a bit of clamor and hold a rally.footnote:s15-5[A "rally" (mītīng): a gathering of groups for discussion or for expressing views on social and political matters.] They usually imagine the prophet to be a person of this sort — like a learned, clerical, well-bred man among a group of people. Except that these people, too, sometimes recognize his worth — these are good folk, and they are called believers; and sometimes they do not recognize his worth — these become unbelievers, and they are called polytheists. We have imagined that the prophet is something of this kind.

In prophethood there is a transformation and an upheaval — I should say two transformations and upheavals. The first is in the very being of the prophet himself.@@CX(the Biʿthah | two transformations — within the prophet, then within society)@@ The bi'that, the rising, the revolution, the transformation, first comes into being within the inner depths and the essence of the prophet himself, within the prophet's own inner self. First he is changed; first he emerges from the state of stagnation and lethargy. After a resurrection has been set up within his spirit, within his inner self — after a rising has come into being within the essence and the soul of the prophet — after all the abundant, brimming reserves deposited by God within the prophet have been awakened — like a spring from which, at every instant, billions of streams of water are drawn forth and pour out (whereas before this instant, all these waters were hidden, concealed within it) — after, in short, the prophet himself becomes a Muslim, when he himself comes under the influence of the transformation of divine revelation — then this very spring, this very transformation, this very fervor, this very revolution, this very rising, pours out from this overflowing, surging fountain that is the spirit and inner self of the prophet into society; it is transmitted into the very text of human society. After a transformation has come into being within him, a transformation comes into being in society. After a mighty rising has been set up within his inner self, a still mightier rising is set up within the text of society. After a revolution has come into being within his heart, by his hand a revolution comes into being in society, and the bi'that in its true sense is realized.@@CX(the Biʿthah | inner revolution pours out into society)@@ So you see that in prophethood, whatever there is, is fervor and transformation and upheaval and rousing and being-roused.footnote:s15-6["Being-roused" (inbiʿāth), from the root b-ʿ-th, "to be raised up / stirred up."]

What is the prophet's condition before prophethood? Two points concerning the life of every prophet, before prophethood, exist in a manner that seems contradictory. Of course, when I say "contradictory," I do not mean that there is a real contradiction; it may merely appear contradictory. The first is that the prophet, even though he has not yet been commissioned, possesses very strong and deep human reserves — more than the rest of the people. His capacity for understanding, his capacity for movement, his capacity for outburst, is not comparable to that of other people, other human beings. His capacity for the worship and servitude(((servitude, ʿubūdiyyah))) of God, his readiness to be a servant of God, is to a degree that is inconceivable for other human beings.@@CX(Prophethood | prophet possesses deeper human reserves than others)@@ All the reserves that draw a human being out of the abasementfootnote:s15-7[From the root ḥ-ḍ-ḍ, meaning "lowness, debasement" (ḥaḍīḍ).] of being mere dust and raise him to the summit of:

Now, why are they more than in others? Did God commit some injustice here, some discrimination here? That is another matter, and in answer to it we can offer a brief, summary reply, in this way: namely, that in the end, in order to bear the burden of the responsibility of prophethood, a heavier, stouter frame and a more powerful reserve was required. The burden of messengership is no penny-trifle of a task.footnote:s15-9[An idiom (lit. "the work of a one-shāhī, half-penny") signifying something of trifling worth.] The burden of prophethood, conveying God's message to the people and transforming a society from one of ignorance (jāhilī)(((jāhiliyyah))) into one of monotheism (tawḥīdī)(((Tawhid))) — this is a task of enormous magnitude and a very heavy burden, and in the end it is necessary that some group bear this burden.@@CX(Prophethood | mission is to transform a jāhilī society into a tawhidi one)@@ Who will bear it? Ordinary human beings? Or a human being who, by virtue of the conditions and characteristics of his environment, family, climate, and kin, possesses greater capacities and greater possibility — and because God sees the greater possibilities in this being, He helps him, grants him grace, bestows favor upon him, places at his disposal whatever is a means of nurturing, so that these two hundred maunds of burden may rest upon a hundred maunds of the prophet's bonefootnote:s15-10[Drawn from this Persian proverb: "Two hundred maunds of bone are needed to bear a hundred maunds of burden."] — He makes of him a person ready for this work. In the end, if the Messenger of God had not taken up this heavy burden, this heavy burden would have remained on the ground; it was not the work of you and me to lift it. It was not the work of Gandhi(((Gandhi, Mahatma)))footnote:s15-11[Mahatma Gandhi (1869–1948 CE), spiritual and political leader of the Indian nation in the cause of freedom from the rule of the British Empire. A frame more powerful than all these frames is necessary to be able to lift this heavy burden — which is named messengership and prophethood and bi'that — and the detail of this is, of course, very extensive. The prophet is among the people with such reserves as these. This is one point, and this was the summary of the first point: that the reserves which exist in the prophet, in the messenger, are more brimming, richer, deeper, and more usable than the reserves of ordinary people.

The second point is this: that the prophet, before the bi'that and before prophethood, is, in the ordinary course of life, a companion and partner of the people; they are moving along in one and the same current. From the beginning he is not preoccupied with thinking, "What shall I do to overturn this society?" It is possible that he is discontented — and indeed he is discontented. An intelligent human being is, of course, discontented with the state of class division of his time, with the state of ignorance of his time, with the poverty of his time, with the oppression of his time, with the foolishness of his time, and — to put it in Islamic terms — with the jāhiliyya (state of ignorance) of his time; he is of course discontented. But this discontent is only at the level of discontent; it is not at the level of bringing about a transformation, a revolution, of creating a path counter to the ordinary social path.@@CX(Prophethood | before the bi'that the prophet shares the people's current, only discontented)@@

*Qur'an, Sūrat al-Ḍuḥā (93:7):* "And He found you lost (astray), and guided you."

And He found you lost and guided you. Here there are several narrations — though some of these narrations are also weak — and several are sayings of the commentators that have become mixed in with the narrations. It says: "And He found you lost, and guided you." What is meant is not intellectual or spiritual lostness; rather, what is meant is that once the Prophet, in his childhood, had become lost in the mountains of Mecca, and then God guided his grandfather, who was able to find him; or in another episode he had become lost, and a certain woman came and found him; or "you were unknown among the people of Mecca, no one recognized you, and God guided the people of Mecca to you." They construed it in these senses. We do not deny these senses — pay close attention, grasp the point of the words. These senses may indeed be the case; if a sound narration in accordance with any one of these senses should come, then with all respect, very well. It is possible that, from the root, the narrations that exist are weak; it is possible that they are sound. And if sound narrations in this regard should come for any one of these meanings, of course it is acceptable, and one can accept it. But despite our accepting any one of these meanings in accordance with a sound narration, the apparent meaning of the verse is other than this. And accepting a narration that gives us this meaning does not conflict with accepting the apparent meaning of the verse.

The apparent meaning of the verse is something else; the apparent meaning of the verse, plain and explicit, in plain Persian, is saying: "You were astray, you were lost; we guided you." What does "you were astray" mean? Does it mean you were an idolater? Never! Does it mean you were a deviant person? Never! Does it mean you were a sinner? Never! Then what? It means: this straight path that was presented to you through the bi'that and prophethood was not at your disposal. Is it not so?@@CX(guidance | the straight path was not the prophet's before the bi'that)@@ Those bodies of knowledge, those laws, those thoughts, those ideas, that became clear and manifest to his holy heart with the coming of revelation upon the Prophet — did they exist for that great one before prophethood and before the bi'that? Certainly not. "You were lost" means this, and this is the apparent meaning of the verse. And there is no problem at all in our construing the noble verse — whose apparent sense is this — from the standpoint of the taʾwīl meaning, as referring to "having been lost among the people of Mecca," "getting lost in the mountains around Mecca," "getting lost while herding certain sheep along a path," for example — just as is found in some of the narrations and reports. In sum, then, it is after this inner and innermost rousing that the path of the prophet changes and his striving takes on another color, and with continual effort and struggle he strives. He strives that what may come about? That in society and in the very text of human beings' lives, a rising and a transformation may, from the foundation up, be brought into being. After a transformation has come into being within himself, he endeavors to create a transformation in society — and this is precisely the responsibility of messengership, and the Prophet exists for this work.@@CX(the Biʿthah | messengership exists to bring a foundational rising into society)@@
