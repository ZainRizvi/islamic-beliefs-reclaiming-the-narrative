The matter we are raising here today is a matter called Wilayat (ولایت).(((Wilayah)))

The matter of Wilayat, in the form in which we raise it and derive it from the Qur'an, is most often given little attention. Of course, the name "Wilayat" and the word "Wilayat" are familiar to a Shia ear. In our supplications, in our requests from God, in our narrations, in our common and widespread thoughts, there is a matter called Wilayat — bound up with complete sanctity and reverence, and with the longing to secure something called Wilayat. As Shia, we always consider ourselves to possess Wilayat; and if a Shia is somewhat scrupulous, precise, and careful, he always asks God, he yearns, that God keep him alive with Wilayat and cause him to die with Wilayat, and make Wilayat understood to him. So a word called "Wilayat" is familiar to the ear.

Ultimately,footnote:s23-2[Finally; in the end.] I want to speak about this word and the concept of Wilayat very much from its roots. We will arrive at the Wilayat of Ali ibn Abi Talib(((Ali ibn Abi Talib))) (upon him be blessings and peace); but for now I want to speak about the stages that come before that. We will draw Wilayat out of the Qur'an and from the noble verses of the Qur'an — we will derive and extract it — and you will see what a modern, advanced, fascinating principle the principle of Wilayat is, and how a nation, a community, the followers of one thought and one creed, if they do not possess Wilayat, are aimlessly abandoned and left idle. You will grasp this and you will feel it.

In the light of this discussion it can be well understood why someone who does not have Wilayat — his prayer is not prayer, his fasting is not fasting, and his acts of worship are not worship.@@CX(Wilayah | without it prayer, fasting and worship are void)@@ With this discussion it can be well understood why a society and an Ummah that does not have Wilayat — even if it spends its whole life in prayer and fasting and in giving away all its wealth in charity — is still not worthy of God's forgiveness and grace. And in short, in the light of this discussion, the meaning of the narrations concerning Wilayat can be understood — among them this well-known hadith that has come from several of the Imams (peace be upon them), some of whose sentences and words I will repeat here: «If a man were to stand in prayer through his nights, and fast through his days, and give all his wealth in charity, and perform Hajj every year of his life, yet did not recognize the Wilayat of the friend (walī) of God so as to take him as his guardian and have all his deeds be by that one's guidance...»footnote:s23-3[Hadith no. 33. The Arabic, in part: «If a man were to stand in prayer through his nights, and fast through his days, and give all his wealth in charity, and perform Hajj every year of his life, yet did not recognize the Wilayat of the friend of God so as to take him as guardian and have all his deeds be by his guidance...»] — if someone fasts his whole life, not only the month of Ramadan; if he stays awake all his nights until morning; if he gives away all his wealth in the way of God; yet does not have Wilayat with the friend (walī) of God — pay attention to this expression — does not have Wilayat with the friend of God, or does not possess the Wilayat of the friend of God — such a person, all that he has done is vain, fruitless, and null.

The matter of Wilayat is a continuation of the discussion of Prophethood;(((Prophethood, nubuwwah))) it is not something separate from the discussion of Prophethood. The matter of Wilayat is, in truth, the completion, the appendix, and the conclusion of the discussion of Prophethood. Now we will see that if there is no Wilayat, Prophethood too remains incomplete.@@CX(Wilayah | completion and conclusion of Prophethood)@@ Therefore we are obliged to briefly repeat the past discussions regarding Prophethood — let me state its broad outlines for you so that we may arrive, little by little, entering from the margins into the body of the matter of Wilayat. Of course, let me also say this: raising this matter is very difficult, and expounding this matter is even more difficult. The reason is that so many weak, flimsy, and illogical ideas about Wilayat have become entrenched in the minds of ordinary people that when you want to say the correct thing — the thing that conforms to the text of the Qur'an and the text of the hadith — about Wilayat, one of two problems arises: either it is confused with those very things that are already in people's minds, or a feeling of estrangement arises toward what is being said as "Wilayat." This happens in two groups; therefore this discussion is very difficult and troublesome. But I seek aid from the grace of the Most Exalted Lord, and I will try to complete this discussion within two or three days, God willing. And you brothers too, pay attention, take heed, do not grow weary from the monotony of the discussion, so that I can follow the discussion through completely.

For what does the Prophet of God come? We said that the Prophet comes to bring human beings to perfection; he comes to imbue human beings with the character of God (akhlāq Allāh); he comes to complete and bring to fullness the noble traits of character. What I said is the substance of the narrations: «I was sent to bring the noble traits of character to completion.»footnote:s23-4[Hadith no. 6: «I was sent to bring the noble traits of character to completion» (buʿithtu li-utammima makārim al-akhlāq).] The Prophet comes to build human beings; he comes to bring to firmness this substance whose name is "the human being."

By what path and by what means does the Prophet work to build the human being? How does the Prophet build human beings? Does he set up a school? Does he set up a philosophical doctrine? Does the Prophet set up a monastery and a place of worship? We said: no. The Prophet, in order to build the human being, sets up a human-building factory. The Prophet prefers to succeed ten years later, twenty years later; but what he builds should not be one human being and two human beings and twenty human beings — rather, he should set up a human-building factory that automatically delivers complete human beings, the kind of human beings the Prophet desires. So the Prophet, in order to build human beings and bring the human substance to firmness, makes use of a human-building factory.

What is the human-building factory, sir? The human-building factory is the Islamic society and order. Here is the crux, the point on which the essential attention and emphasis of the discussion rests.@@CX(Islamic society | is the human-building factory)@@ Everyone says the Prophet wants to build human beings; everyone says the Prophet comes for teaching and training, and everyone understands this. What must be understood — what must be understood with precision — is that the Prophet does not take human beings one by one to some secluded corner to whisper the love of God into their ears. The Prophets do not form a scientific and philosophical school in order to produce a handful of students whom they then send out to guide people across the regions of the world. The Prophet's work is firmer and more steadfast than that, sturdier and more deeply rooted. What does he do? He builds a factory from which nothing comes out but human beings — and that factory is the Islamic society.footnote:s23-5[For more information about the discussions related to Islamic government, the conditions and characteristics of Islamic rulers, and government during the era of Occultation, you may refer to the book _Wilayat and Government_ (Vilāyat va Hukūmat).]

What is the Islamic society? What sort of element and nature is it? These are other discussions. Now, just so that this corner of the matter is briefly clarified, I will say — though, in the course of our discussion, it is not very relevant. The Islamic society means that society, that civilization, at the head of which God governs; the laws of that society are God's laws; the divine bounds are carried out in that society; the appointing and dismissing is done by God.@@CX(Islamic society | one at whose head God governs and whose laws are God's laws)@@ In the social cone — if we suppose and draw it in the shape of a cone, as is customary and common among a number of sociologists — at the apex of the cone is God, and beneath God, all of humanity and all human beings. The structure is brought into being by the religion of God; the law of peace and war is established by divine ordinances; social relations, the economy, government, rights — all and all and all of them are determined by the religion of God, and the religion of God carries them out, and the religion of God stands behind this law. This is the Islamic society.

Like where, sir? Like Medina.(((Medina))) Just as the Prophet came to Medina and formed a society. Well, they were in Mecca too; they could have scattered to the regions of the lands and worshipped God there as well — no, that was not enough. They came to Medina and formed a society, at the head of which God governed. The vicegerent of God who governed there — who, in practice, was he? The Prophet. Who established the ordinances? Who legislated them? Who enacted them? The Prophet. Who carried out the laws? Who compelled the people to carry them out? The Prophet.

In such a society — where everything is from God — the congregational prayer, and the sermon after the congregational prayer, are one and the same as the war-song of the battlefield. In that very mosque where the Prophet of God would stand and lead the congregational prayer, and ascend the pulpit for the people and speak and teach and purify and instruct — in that very mosque the banner of jihad would be brought, and the Prophet would bind it and place it in the hand of Usama ibn Zayd,footnote:s23-6[Usama ibn Zayd, son of Zayd ibn Haritha, the freed slave of the Prophet, and of Umm Ayman, the maidservant of Lady Amina, the mother of the Messenger of God. After returning from the Farewell Pilgrimage, the Prophet, while Usama was still young, made him commander of the army of the Muslims for the battle against the Romans. In the time of the Commander of the Faithful, he first pledged allegiance to the Imam, but he was not present in the wars of the Commander of the Faithful. On account of his remorse for this failing in the latter part of his life, Imam al-Baqir, in a hadith, recommended that nothing but good be said about him.] place it in the hand of so-and-so, [saying] "Go forth!" «Set out in the name of God!»footnote:s23-7[Hadith no. 7: «Set out in the name of God» (Inṭaliqū ʿalā ismi-llāh).] — Move in the name of God, corner the enemy at such-and-such place, do such-and-such. In that very mosque, the Prophet of God would carry out the prescribed punishments (ḥadd). In that very mosque, the Prophet's judiciary was convened. In that very mosque, the Prophet's administration of labor and economy was convened. In that very mosque, the zakat was collected and distributed. There was teaching, there was prayer and supplication, there was the war-song of the battlefield, there was wealth and economy — and in short, everything, both this world and the hereafter, was unified and gathered into one, under the leadership of the Prophet, in the house of God. This is the Islamic society. The Prophets come to set up such a society. In this society, whoever comes becomes a human being. Even if he does not become a complete human being, he is compelled to move according to the conduct of human beings. Whoever wishes to be good can be good in the Prophet's society; in non-divine societies it is not like this.

In non-Islamic and non-divine societies, people want to be good but cannot. You want to be religious, but you cannot. You want not to give or take usury, but you find that it cannot be done. A woman wants not to step outside Islamic chastity, but the environment pressures her. All the factors and motives drive a person away from the remembrance of God. Pictures and shows and excessive entertainments and comings and goings and dealings and conversations — all of them drive a person away from God and estrange the remembrance of God from a person's heart. In the Islamic society, the matter is the reverse. In the Islamic society, its marketplace, its mosque, its seat of government, its friend, its kinsman, the father of the household, the youth of the household — all and all of them turn a person toward the remembrance of God, draw him toward God, reconcile him with God, establish a relationship for him with God, make of him a servant of God, and keep him away from servitude to other than God.@@CX(servitude | Islamic society makes one a servant of God and frees one from servitude to other than God)@@

If the Islamic society of the Prophet's time had continued for fifty years; if the same leadership had remained at work; or if, after the Prophet, Ali ibn Abi Talib (peace be upon him) had sat in the Prophet's place with the very leadership that the Prophet had appointed — rest assured of this: after fifty years, all those hypocrites would have been transformed into true believers. If they had allowed the Prophetic government and the Alid government to continue, one after the other, that human-building society would, of necessity, have made all the adulteratedfootnote:s23-8[Flawed; tainted (lit. having "adulteration" / impurity).] people pure of adulteration, made all the hypocritical hearts believing, and made all those whose spirits were not acquainted with faith likewise acquainted with God and with faith. This is the natural character of the matter pertaining to the Islamic society. The Prophets come to set up such a thing. When this is set up, like a human-building factory, people become Muslims — not one by one, not ten by ten, not a hundred by a hundred, but group by group, host by host: both outwardly Muslim, in the outward conduct of Islamic matters, and inwardly Muslim and believing, truly and inwardly. Is this clear? So the Prophet comes for such a work.

I said we want to discuss Wilayat from its roots. At the very beginning, when the Prophet brings the Islamic thought; at the very beginning, when the Prophet's call commences — can the Prophet, single-handedly, administer a society? Does a society not need an organization? Does it not need a group of people to administer this organization? Does it not need a soldiery to defend this society and put the enemies of this society in their place? Are people who would assist the Prophet and spread the call of the Prophet not necessary? We see that, yes [they are]. Every task must be done through ordinary means. The Prophets too, in most of their activities, made use of ordinary means. When the Prophet comes, in order to be able to form the intended society — which is that very human-building factory — for this work he needs a bonded, unified group, possessing firm faith, from the depths of the hearts of those who believe in and are committed to this doctrine, with steadfast steps, dynamic and seeking that goal. The Prophet needs such a group at the beginning of the work. Therefore the Prophet begins to gather and form this group. With the verses of the Qur'an, with goodly exhortations — «Call to the way of your Lord with wisdom and good admonition»footnote:s23-9[Qur'an, Sūrat al-Naḥl (16:125): "Call to the way of your Lord with wisdom and goodly admonition, and dispute with them in the manner that is best."] — with goodly exhortations, with the verses of the Qur'an, with the penetrating power of the Prophetic word, he brings into being, around himself at the beginning of the work, the hearts of certain Muslims. These constitute a community. So the Prophet, at the beginning of the work, when he raises his call, brings into being a community, brings into being a rank, creates a front against the front of unbelief. This front is composed of whom? Of steadfast Muslims, of believers committed and penetrating of heart — of those of whom [it is said] «no blame of any blamer turns them back»footnote:s23-10[Hadith no. 22: «no blame of any blamer affects them» (lā taʾkhudhuhum fī-llāhi lawmatu lāʾim).]: no censure of any blamer turns them away from the path of God. These are the Muslims of the first step.

(I am speaking very briefly and in compressed form, for minds that are willing to grasp. I feel it does not need much explanation and elaboration; moreover, if I were to explain a great deal, we would stray entirely from the thread of the discourse, and the main point would be lost. If on other days I see that there is a need, it is possible I will explain more. For now, let me state the main matter.)

A group and a front are brought into being by the Prophet. In the midst of what society are these living? In the midst of the society of ignorance (jāhiliyya).(((jāhiliyya))) For example, the Muslims of early Islam are living there in the midst of the ignorant society of Mecca. If this thin current — which has come into being under the name of Islam and the Muslims — is to remain in the midst of that conflict-ridden and troublesome ignorant society; if this very community, this very rank, this very front is not to be annihilated, not to perish, not to be digested, not to be dissolved — then this group of Muslims must be welded to one another like tempered steel. These Muslims must be so connected and bound to one another that no factor can separate them from each other. In the words of today's people and today's cultures, a very intense and hard party discipline must be established among these Muslim individuals. They must be stuck together more and more, welded and knotted together more and more, and kept further and further from other fronts, from other currents, from opposing motives; because these are in the minority. A community that is in the minority — it is possible that its thought may be overshadowed by the thought of the majority. Its deeds, its dignity, its character may be lost amid the dignities and characters and deeds of the rest of the people, who at times are opposed to them; [it may] be annihilated, be digested, perish. So that these are not digested, so that these are not annihilated, so that they can remain as a single group — so that in the future the Islamic society may be built and administered and continued by the hands of these steadfast ones, and so that they may be helpers of the Prophet, and so that they can endure — they are connected to one another more and more, and separated from the other fronts more and more.

Like what? Like a group of mountaineers passing along a difficult, treacherous mountain path. Ten people in the midst of the snows, staves in hand, must traverse this narrow, danger-filled path — these twists and turns, this mountain pass, this defilefootnote:s23-11[A passage and crossing-place (the mountain defile / ford).] — to reach the mountain's summit. They are told: stick together, tie your belts to one another, do not move separately and one by one — for if you are left alone, there is the danger of slipping. They are firmly welded to one another. Besides welding them to one another, it is said: do not take much baggage with you, do not look this way and that, keep your attention only on your own path and your mind on your own work. They also bind them firmly to one another — their waists and hands — so that if one of them falls, if two fall, the rest can hold him up. The intense interconnection of the mountaineers is the indicator and exhibitor of the state of interconnection and welded-togetherness of the Muslims at the beginning of the work. Does this intense interconnection have a name or not? This bondedness of the Muslims of the front at the beginning of the religion — who have been welded to one another, knotted to one another, who are inseparable from one another, who are completely cut off from the other fronts, who are ever more hand-in-hand and clasping one another — does it have a name in the Qur'an and hadith or not? Yes; this name is Wilayat — Wilayat.@@CX(Wilayah | is the Qur'anic name for the welded interconnection of the believing front)@@

There is still a long way to go before we reach the Wilayat of Ali ibn Abi Talib (peace be upon him), and the [hadith] «And the Wilayat of Ali ibn Abi Talib is my fortress.»footnote:s23-12[Hadith no. 43: «And the Wilayat of Ali ibn Abi Talib is my fortress» (wa wilāyatu ʿAliyyi-bni Abī Ṭālib ḥiṣnī).] We will reach that after an hour or two of speaking. We have not yet reached those points; you must listen.

So, what does Wilayat mean in its first Qur'anic sense? In its first Qur'anic sense, Wilayat means the interconnection, the shared-frontedness, and the intense bonding of a group of human beings who possess a single thought and seek a single goal, who are stepping forward on one path, who are striving and moving toward one objective, who have accepted one thought and one creed.@@CX(Wilayah | first Qur'anic sense — interconnection of those of one thought and one goal)@@ The individuals of this front must be connected to one another more and more, and must separate and hold themselves apart from the other fronts, the other poles, the other sections. Why? So that they do not perish, do not get digested. This is what is called Wilayat in the Qur'an.

The Prophet brings into being the Muslim community of the beginning of the work with this bondedness and welded-togetherness; he connects them to one another, makes them brothers of one another, turns them into a single body, and by means of them forms the Islamic Ummah and brings the Islamic society into being. From the bonding of these with the enemies, with the opponents, with the hostile, with the other fronts — which, God willing, you will see in the verses tomorrow — he prevents them. Between them and the other fronts he creates separation; from joining the front of the Jews, from joining the front of the Christians, from joining the front of the polytheists, he restrains them, and more and more he strives to make their ranks compact and welded together. For what? Because if they are not in this state, if they do not have Wilayat, if they are not one hundred percent interconnected, discord will arise among them, and they will be incapable of bearing the burden of the trust that is upon their shoulders. They will not be able to carry this burden to its destination.

Of course, later, when the Islamic society is transformed into a great Ummah, Wilayat is still needed. How Wilayat is needed within an Ummah, and why it is needed — this I will expand upon. At this very point in the matter, let us come and pay attention to the Wilayat of which the Shia speak. We said: a small group, in the midst of a dark world, in the midst of an ignorant world, must be connected and bound to one another in order to be able to remain. If they are not stuck and welded together, their remaining and the continuation of their life is not possible. We gave the example of the Muslim community at the beginning of Islam, in the midst of the ignorant society of Mecca, or in the early period when they had come to Medina.

Is there another example as well, or not? Yes. When? The small group of Shi'ism, during the anti-Shia and anti-Islamic caliphates in history. Do you imagine that the Shia could easily remain? Do you imagine that the propaganda weapons on one side, suffocation and strangulation on another, the prisons and tortures and killings on another — could possibly have allowed a group to remain? An intellectual group like the Shia, which was so contending with the powers of its own time and such a cause of trouble for them — how did it come to remain? It remained on account of Wilayat.@@CX(Wilayah | enabled the Shia minority to survive the anti-Shia caliphates)@@ A strange interconnection and shared-frontedness had been created among the Shia of the time of the nominal caliphates by the Imams (peace be upon them), so that in the light of this Wilayat this Shi'i current could remain protected amid the various other currents.

Imagine a vast river, into which water enters from several directions. The waters are moving with swift currents. The surface of the river is uneven; whirlpools form; the waters tumble over one another; various kinds of opposing currents are sinking into one another in this river, in this bed; they neutralize one another, strike against one another's heads — this water is moving forward. Amid these currents, in the midst of these turbid, dusty, muddy waters, a current of sweet, clean, pure, clear water is continuing along its way; and in this riverbed of flood and torrent, this remarkable channel moves forward. This thin current — it happens, in some seas and rivers, perhaps some have seen it: two colors of water, two kinds of water moving side by side, owing to natural factors that exist, which I do not wish to explain. In the midst of this remarkable channel where swift currents are moving, one thin current of water is going forward amid these swift currents, and it remains sound and goes on. The astonishing thing is this: it never mixes in, its color is never ruined, it never takes on the taste of the other salty and bitter waters, but continues and preserves the same sweet taste, the same clear color, the same purity and limpidity and undisturbedness, and moves forward.

Liken the world of Islam in the time of the Umayyad and Abbasid caliphs to this very river, in which various kinds of intellectual, political, and practical currents were running and moving against one another. When you look from beginning to end, you see the current of Shi'ism. You see that it is a thin stream of water. Amid this remarkable tempest it appears insignificant and small and comes to the eye [as such], but it has held itself together, has never become turbid, its taste has never been ruined, it has never lost its purity, has never taken on the color and taste and smell of the other waters; it has remained and gone on. What has held this together? What has been able to become the factor of the survival of this Shi'i current? The existence of that walī who counsels Wilayat among the people and among his own followers, who binds them to one another, makes them affectionate toward one another, promotes Wilayat — and this too is in the very same sense as the Wilayat of the Prophet's time.@@CX(Wilayah | the walī binds his followers together and is the factor of the current's survival)@@ The Shi'i Wilayat, that Wilayat upon which so much emphasis has been placed — this is one of its dimensions; it has other dimensions too, which I will also state. What I have said is not yet the whole matter; this is one dimension and one side of the matter. Wilayat means interconnection.

The Qur'an considers the believers to be the allies (awliyāʾ) of one another. Those who possess true faith are the shared-fronted and bonded ones of one another — and they call this "Shia."@@CX(Faith | true believers are the awliyāʾ — shared-fronted allies — of one another)@@ In the narrations, the Shia is referred to as "believer" (muʾmin).(((muʾmin))) If you look in the words of the earliest jurists,footnote:s23-13[(q-d-m) earlier, former; the predecessors — i.e., the earliest/foremost jurists.] and in many of the narrations, in some Islamic offices they mention certain conditions — among them they say "Islam," and after Islam, "faith (īmān)." One says: Sir, what does faith mean? What is meant by "faith" is possessing the particular mode of thought of the Shia — that is, seeing Islam from the perspective from which the Shia sees it, and establishing it by the logic with which the Shia establishes it: this is the meaning of "faith." We see that in the time of the Imams (peace be upon them) they made the Shia cohesive, interconnected, brotherly, and bonded with one another in this way, so that they could preserve the Shi'i current in history; otherwise the Shia would have perished a thousand times over; a thousand times over its ideas would have been digested — just as some other sects became this way, lost their color, perished, and were annihilated.

In any case, this is one of the dimensions of Wilayat, and tomorrow, God willing, I will present another of its dimensions, which is perhaps from one aspect of even greater importance — and because, after this dimension, it must certainly be expounded, I will mention it, and that is the Wilayat of the friend of God (walī Allāh). The Wilayat of the Shia with one another has become clear; what does the Wilayat of the friend of God mean? What does the Wilayat of Ali ibn Abi Talib mean? What does the Wilayat of Imam al-Sadiq mean? This [matter] that you and I must today have the Wilayat of the Imams — what does it mean? For a number of people imagine that the Wilayat of the Imams means only that we love the Imams; and how greatly they err; how greatly they err! It is not merely loving; otherwise — is there anyone to be found in the world of Islam who does not love the Imams, the infallible ones of the Prophet's household? So does everyone have Wilayat? Is there anyone who is an enemy of these [Imams]? Were even those who fought against them in early Islam all their enemies? Many of them loved these [Imams]; but for the sake of the world they were prepared to fight against them. Many of them knew what stations and ranks these [Imams] possessed. When they brought the news of the passing of Imam al-Sadiq(((Imam al-Sadiq))) to Mansur,(((Mansur al-Dawaniqi)))footnote:s23-14['Abdullah ibn Ahmad, surnamed al-Mansur; because he was very miserly and calculating, he became known as Mansur al-Dawaniqi. The deviant people of their own time spoke in this same way. Imam al-Sajjad(((Imam al-Sajjad))) and his companions spoke this way with the deviant people of their time. In the Bihar al-Anwar(((Bihar al-Anwar))) there is a hadith which says: Yahya ibn Umm Tawil,(((Yahya ibn Umm Tawil)))footnote:s23-22[Yahya was the son of the foster-nurse (wet-nurse) of His Holiness al-Sajjad (Imam al-Sajjad); through the blessing of his close connection with the Imam and his benefiting from the Imam's teachings, he was steadfast in defending the rightfulness of the Imams and in struggling against the dominance of the culture of the Taghut.] the disciple of the Fourth Imam, used to come to the mosque of Medina and stand facing the people. The very people among whom Imam al-Husayn(((Imam al-Husayn))) and Imam al-Hasan(((Imam al-Hasan))) had lived for twenty years; the very people who were neither Umayyad nor affiliated with the Banu Umayya(((Banu Umayya))) — so what were they? They were cowards who, in the matter of Ashura(((Ashura))) and Karbala,(((Karbala))) out of fear of the suffocation that the Banu Umayya had brought about, had left the side of the family of Muhammad empty; yet they were people of [some] belief.

Yahya ibn Umm Tawil would stand before these very people and repeat this very Qur'anic word: «He would say: We have disbelieved in you, and there has appeared between us and you enmity and hatred.»footnote:s23-23[Hadith no. 40: «He would say: We have disbelieved in you, and there has appeared between us and you enmity and hatred» (yaqūlu: kafarnā bikum wa badā baynanā wa baynakumu l-ʿadāwatu wa l-baghḍāʾ).] We have disbelieved in you, and between us and you wrath and rancor have become manifest — that is, the very saying that Abraham would say to the unbelievers of his own time and to the polytheists and deviants and misguided ones of his own age. See that Wilayat is the very same Wilayat. Abraham too possesses Wilayat; the Shia of Imam al-Sajjad too, in their own time, possess Wilayat.@@CX(Wilayah | the same in every age — from Abraham to the Shia of Imam al-Sajjad)@@ They must have one another and be separate from the enemies. If a Shia of the Shia of Imam al-Sajjad, in the time of Imam al-Sajjad, out of fear or greed, went after the enemy's front, he is outside the Wilayat of Imam al-Sajjad and is not connected to the front of Imam al-Sajjad.@@CX(Wilayah | one who joins the enemy's front out of fear or greed falls outside it)@@ Therefore the close student of Imam al-Sajjad says to them: «We have disbelieved in you, and there has appeared between us and you enmity and hatred»; and Yahya ibn Umm Tawil was among the very good and choice companions of Imam al-Sajjad.

The end of the affair of this choice Muslim was this: Hajjaj ibn Yusuf(((Hajjaj ibn Yusuf))) seized him, cut off his right hand, cut off his left hand, cut off his right foot, cut off his left foot; yet he still began to speak with his tongue, so they cut out his tongue too, until he departed this world. And this was while he had set the Shia in order and had provided and made firm the essential pillars of the edifice of Shi'ism after Imam al-Sajjad.
