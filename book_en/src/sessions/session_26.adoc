The subject that, today, in continuation of our previous discussions, we decided to raise is the statement of several issues from among the matters that we encounter around the question of Wilayat. These are not part of that original framework and the statement of the position of this question itself. The discussion about Wilayat — about what Wilayat is, where in the Qur'an it is derived from, and how many dimensions, aspects, and sides it has — was the discussion already presented earlier. However, there is a series of issues which, for the question of Wilayat, are subsidiary matters; even though each of these matters, in its own place, is itself a fundamental and decisive issue, which, like a principle, must be used for the orientation of Islamic directions and for determining the path of the Muslim society.

These several matters we will mention in order. Two of them we have noted on today's sheet,footnote:s26-1[Please refer to the mimeographed sheet at the end of the session.] and one or two more, God willing, we will present tomorrow.

One of them is this: well, you established from the Qur'an that the preservation of internal bonds and the negation of external dependencies — I repeat: the preservation of internal bonds and the negation of external dependencies — for a Muslim society, is contingent upon there existing a center, a concentrated power, in the Islamic society, so that it may lead and administer all the activities and efforts, and all the orientations and stances of the various poles and factions of this society.@@CX(Wilayah | requires a central concentrated power to lead the Muslim society)@@ And the name of this is the _wali_(((wali))). The _wali_ — with the doubled _ya'_ — means the ruler, the sovereign; that is, the one from whom all forces must take their inspiration, to whom all affairs must return, and, in short, the one with whom the management of the Islamic society lies, both on the intellectual side and on the practical side — this one is called the _wali_. These things we established from the Qur'an. Now tell me, let us see: who is the _wali_ of the Islamic society? Do we have a concise word in this regard, so that if they say to us, "Very well, now we wish to know the _wali_; who can be the _wali_? Which power is it to which all the forces of the Islamic society must return, which must exercise rule?" — if this question is put to us, do we have an answer or not?

Yes, we have. Of course, I have presented the answer to this matter, the response, from time to time within the previous discussions, and you yourselves know it too; it is not as though the matter were unknown. We wish, from the standpoint of logical method and the natural sequence of the subject, to have examined this too.

The Qur'an's answer here is a single word. It says: the one who is the true _wali_ of the Islamic society is God. The ruler in the Islamic society, other than God Most High, is no one else.@@CX(Wilayah | the true wali and ruler of the Islamic society is God)@@ This is a matter that Tawhid(((Tawhid))) too was telling us, that Prophethood(((Prophethood, nubuwwah))) too was establishing as a certainty for us; and now you see that Wilayat too tells us this same thing.@@CX(Tawhid | each principle of the school yields the same conclusion as the others)@@ Let me state this sentence here by way of indication and pass on: the principles of a school and a doctrine must always be like this — that each of the principles yields a conclusion which the other principles also yield. It must not be that from one of the principles of the school we draw an inference whose opposite we infer from another principle. And unfortunately, in the Islam that exists today in the mind and heart of some simple-hearted Muslims, this is exactly what happens: from some of its principles, things are inferred and deduced that are exactly the opposite of what is deduced from some of its other principles.

Therefore, the one who, in the Islamic society, has the right of command and prohibition and decree, and the right to execute commands, and the right to give orders and to determine the path of the society, and, in short, the right of rule over all the particulars of human beings' lives, is God:

I have tracedfootnote:s26-3[(t-b-') _tatabbu'_: to follow after, to investigate.] the verses in the Qur'an that contain the expression _wali_ and _awliya'_. I made an approximately summary study of them all, and I saw that this expression — that God is the _wali_ of the Islamic society, that the believers have no _wali_ and helper other than God, that God must be the ruler of all the affairs of mankind — this is a matter that, in the Qur'an, is among the established certainties. Of course, the brothers are aware of this; but nonetheless it is good for me to bring it to the attention of some in whose minds the matter might become confused. The discussion is not about the creational (_takwini_) sovereignty of the Lord; that is preserved in its own place — it is clear that God Most High arranges the revolution of the earth and the sky by His overpowering will.footnote:s26-4[(q-h-r) _qahira_: overpowering, dominant.] The discussion is about this: that the laws of human life and the individual and social relations of human society too must take inspiration from God; that is, the legal ruler and sovereign, in the divine, Islamic, Qur'anic society, and in the shadefootnote:s26-5[(z-l-l) _zill_: shade, shadow.] of the Islamic order — which we always refer to as the Alawi order(((Alawi order))), and this is an expression that I would like to remain in minds: the Alawi order, the Alawi government, this order whose name we set down as the Alawi order and the Islamic order, and "the Qur'anic society" can be a name for it for us — in such a society and such an order, the ruler is God alone.@@CX(Wilayah | the legislative sovereignty over human life belongs to God alone, distinct from His creational sovereignty)@@

Well, here we arrive at another matter: "Sir, what does it mean that the ruler is God? God Most High does not come face to face with the people so as to command and prohibit. Human beings need a human being to govern over them; a human being is necessary to take the reins of human affairs into his hand." When I say "a human being," I do not insist on the "one"; I do not wish to negate collective leadership — no. A human being is necessary to take the reins of human affairs into his hand. Otherwise, if, among the human and social society, there were only law — even if that law were from God's side — and there were no commander, in the expression of the Commander of the Faithful, peace be upon him, which I shall now present; if there were no sovereign, or there were no body to exercise sovereignty; in short, if there were no overseer over the execution of the law in human society, then the order of human society would again be disrupted. Who is this human being to be?

That human being, or those human beings, who are to be — over mankind and human society — to practically exercise sovereignty, to practically be the _wali_ of the society, to practically take charge of the Wilayat of the society — who are these to be? The answer of the various schools to this question has been varied. The answer of historical realities to this question too has been varied. One group has said: _al-mulk li-man ghalab_ — "Sovereignty belongs to whoever conquers"; that is, the law of the jungle. One group has said: whoever has the greater management. One group has said: whoever is accepted by the people. One group has said: whoever is from such-and-such a dynasty. One group has said other things; they have expressed other logics and approachesfootnote:s26-6[(m-sh-y) _mamsha_: manner of conduct, way of proceeding.] and tastes.

The answer of religion and the school of religion to this question is this:

The one who practically takes the rein of command and of command-and-prohibition into his hand in the society, on behalf of the Lord of the world, is His Messenger.@@CX(Prophethood | the Messenger practically exercises God's rule over the society)@@ Therefore, in a society, when a Prophet has come, it makes no sense that, with the Prophet present, a ruler other than the Prophet should govern over the people. "Prophet" means precisely the one who must take the rein of power in the society into his hand, who must be the ruler. When the Prophet departed from this world, what is the duty? When the Prophet of God, like all other human beings, departed from this world and surrendered his soul to the Soul-Creator, what do we do there? Again a verse of the Qur'an answers this matter:

Which believers? Whoever believes in the school of religion is the _wali_ and ruler of the Islamic society? Well, this would require that we have, to the number of all believing souls, a ruler! What do we do here? Believers who have a special sign. It is evident that the verse of the Qur'an, while it wishes to designate a particular person, to put its finger upon a specific, known human being and say "your ruler is this one," also wishes, implicitly, to provide the criterion. Pay attention! It wishes that human being who, in the view of the Lawgiver and the legislator of Islam, is specified — it wishes to give him rule over the people; but at the same time it also wishes to state the criterion: the reason we said your ruler should be this one is this criterion. This is the cause of his being chosen by the Lord, or of his being appointed by the Lord. Therefore it says:

The establishment of the prayer(((prayer, establishing vs. performing))) in a society means that the spirit of the prayer comes alive in the society, that the society becomes a praying society; and you know that a praying society means that society in which, in all its corners and edges, the remembrance of God and the recollection of God surge completely; and you know that in a society in which the remembrance of God and the recollection of God surge, in this society no catastrophe occurs, no crime, no treachery, no blow against human values occurs in this society.@@CX(Faith | establishing prayer makes a society of God-remembrance in which crime and treachery cannot occur)@@ That society in which the remembrance of God surges, in which the people are mindful of God — its orientation is a divine orientation; in this society, all the people's deeds are done for God.

The cause of thefts, the cause of degradations, the cause of injustices, the cause of submitting to injustice — from both sides — the cause of the transgressionsfootnote:s26-10[('-d-w) _ta'addi_: to transgress, to exceed bounds.] that occur, and likewise the cause of submitting to the transgressions that occur, is entirely remoteness from the recollection of God and the remembrance of God.@@CX(Faith | both committing and submitting to injustice stem from remoteness from the remembrance of God)@@ That society which possesses the remembrance of God — its ruler is like 'Ali ibn Abi Talib((('Ali ibn Abi Talib))), blessings of God upon him, who does not commit injustice and crushes injustice; its subject is like Abu Dharr al-Ghifari(((Abu Dharr al-Ghifari))), who, even though he is beaten, even though he is exiled, even though he is threatened, even though he remains a stranger and friendless, yet does not submit to the burden of injustice, does not turn back from the path of God.@@CX(Faith | a God-remembering society produces rulers who crush injustice and subjects who never submit to it)@@ This is the society in which the remembrance of God exists, this is the society in which the establishment of the prayer exists.

This too confirms that very matter that I presented yesterday — which comes to my mind as a possibility, which I suppose: that "zakat"(((zakat))) is the absolute of all acts of charity (_infaqat_); because the ring that the Commander of the Faithful gave while in the state of bowing was not the technical zakat — it was a charity in the way of God, and the term "zakat" has been applied to it.@@CX(Infaq | "zakat" here denotes the absolute of all charitable spending, not the technical alms)@@

— meaning a human being so attached to equality,footnote:s26-11[(s-w-y) _musawat_: equality, sameness.] so devoted to charitable spending, for whom the sight of poverty and the poor is so painful, that he does not wait for his prayer to finish, lest this beggar leave and another poor one come. He gives his only and sole ring — he says, "Let me set it aside, then I will give it." So great is this human being's attraction toward charity, so absorbed is this man in the path of fulfilling this duty, that he has no patience to wait, no room to bear it. He has seen a poor one, has seen a poverty, has seen an appearance that God does not love — he too does not love it — and he has nothing but the ring; so, in the state of the prayer, he takes it off and gives it to the beggar.

This is a reference to a special and specific episode in history, which the Commander of the Faithful, blessings of God upon him, brought about; that is, this noble one was in the state of prayer, a poor one came, and this noble one gave charity, and the verse was revealed. So, as you observe, the verse, by way of indication, designates 'Ali ibn Abi Talib, peace be upon him, as the _wali_ of the affair (_waliyy al-amr_); but not in the manner of the designation of the history's bullies — when, say, Mu'awiya(((Mu'awiya))) wishes to designate a successor for himself, he says, "This son is my successor; he is my child, so he must, after me, reach this station." But God Most High does not designate a successor for the Prophet in this way. He says 'Ali ibn Abi Talib must reach government after the Prophet, because the qualification for government exists in him to an intense degree. What is it? Complete faith in God, the establishment of the prayer in the society, attachment to charity and the giving (_ita'_)footnote:s26-12[('-t-y) _ita'_: giving.] of zakat to the point of being beside oneself. While appointing the successor, while designating the successor — who is 'Ali ibn Abi Talib, peace be upon him — it also clarifies the criterion, the linchpin,footnote:s26-13[(n-w-t) _manat_: the criterion, the basis/aim upon which a thing hinges.] and the philosophy of his caliphate. This is a verse of the Qur'an.

Therefore, in Islam, the _waliyy al-amr_(((waliyy al-amr))) is the one who is the envoy of God, the one whom God Himself designates; because the premise is that no human being, by the very nature of his creation and formation, has the right of rule over other human beings.@@CX(Wilayah | no human being by his nature has the right of rule over others; only God-designation confers it)@@

The only one who has the right of rule is God; and since God has the right of rule, God can, according to the welfare of human beings, give this right to whomever He wills. And we know that the work of God is not outside of welfare, is not dictatorship, is not bullying and coercion; the work of God is according to the welfare of human beings. Since it is according to the welfare of human beings, therefore He designates and we submit. He designates the Prophet, He designates the Imam; after the Imam, He designates those who conform to particular criteria and qualifications. The one who possesses this quality, possesses this quality, possesses this quality — He designates qualities and says: these, after the rightly-guiding, infallible Imams, are the rulers over the Islamic society. So the _wali_ is designated by God: He Himself is the _wali_, His Prophet is the _wali_, the Imams are the _wali_. The Imams of the Prophet's household(((Ahl al-Bayt))) have been appointed — twelve Imams. In the rank after that, those who conform to and match particular criteria and qualifications — they have been designated for government and caliphate.@@CX(Wilayah | the chain of God-given Wilayat — God, His Prophet, the twelve Imams, then the qualified)@@ The question regarding the designation of the _wali_ in Islam is this.

Of course, this was one verse that I mentioned for you; there are other verses in the Qur'an too, some of which we have brought on this sheet, and some you must search out and find for yourselves in your own Qur'an. We have many verses here.

That upon which emphasis is placed in Islam is this: that the rein of the people's affairs not fall into the hands of those who drag human beings to hell. Did history not show us this matter? Did we not see what they did to the Islamic society a little after the radiantfootnote:s26-14[(sh-'-sh-') _musha'sha'_: radiant, brilliant.] dawn of the beginning of Islam, and what happened, and what befell this society? That society in which the people do not recognize the worth of good men, that society in which the people mistake the criteria of goodness, that society in which the people cannot recognize the one who is their reformer and their compassionate counselor — how much must have been worked upon this society for it to have reached this point and to have acquired such a condition? The poisonous propaganda that was carried out, on the part of the apparatuses of unjust and tyrannical power, within the Islamic society, so changed the horizon of the people's knowledge and insight, did such a thing to these people, that the people were as if they saw black as white and white as black; they had come into such a state.@@CX(Taghut | tyrannical propaganda reverses a people's insight until they see black as white)@@ It was for this reason that, in the second and third centuries of the Hijra, when one looks at history, one sees the atrocities of the apparatus of power and caliphate, and one sees the heedlessness and indifference of the people, and one is astonished. Are these the same people? Are these the same people who, before 'Uthman((('Uthman))), did not show patience, who surrounded him from all sides and then, finally, with that gruesome circumstance, removed and deposed him from the caliphate? Are these the same ones? Are these the same ones who now, on the wedding night of the Abbasid caliph, see what enormous expenditure — which could have administered a vast section of the Islamic society — is being spent, see on what debaucheries it is being spent, for what kinds of things it is being consumed, how the treasury of the Muslims (_bayt al-mal_)(((bayt al-mal))) is being spent on personal matters? I am not concerned with whether it is right or wrong; it is a personal matter — it is money for a thousand persons, and a single person spends this money only on himself. We do not say he spends it on his own debauchery — no indeed, even if he spends it on his own prayer and fasting, is this act permissible? And they saw that, in the very fabric of the reality of the Islamic society, this act was being done, and yet, at the same time, they were heedless and uninformed.

Perhaps I have once said this: that on the wedding night of Ja'far al-Barmaki(((Ja'far al-Barmaki))), the beloved vizier of Harun al-Rashid(((Harun al-Rashid))) — he was a young man of twenty-eight or nine, thirty years old, Ja'far al-Barmaki. When Ja'far al-Barmaki departed from the world, he was thirty-four or five years old, when they killed him. At the peak of his popularity Ja'far became a son-in-law — Harun al-Rashid too had abundant affection for Ja'far al-Barmaki — on the wedding night, in place of the sweets (_nuql_) that they scatter over the bride and groom, the noble, high-ranking guests of the rank of the Barmakis and Harun al-Rashid saw that some things were scattered over the bride and groom. When they went near and picked them up — well, they had been scattered, they picked them up, they picked up two, three, five — they saw, yes, these are not sweets, nor are they coins. They looked carefully: they are delicate little boxes the size of a fingertip; they are boxes, made of pure gold; then, when you opened the box, from inside it came out a paper, a very thin and delicate paper. When you unfolded this paper, you saw — how astonishing! — it became a large sheet. You read the sheet, and you saw that on this sheet, the decree of ownership of such-and-such a part of the country had been given to you. It is a decree of land-grant (_iqta'_),footnote:s26-15[(q-t-') _iqta'_: a parcel (of land); a land-grant.] it is a decree of fiefdom (_tiyul_).footnote:s26-16[_Tiyul_ (fiefdom): the assignment of land and property to a person by the king, whereby that person provides himself an income through the taxation of that property.]

In a single night, God knows, five hundred, eight hundred, a thousand decrees of fiefdom on very thin papers, in golden boxes, were scattered over a bride and groom, and people picked them up whom the caliph did not know who they would be. For example, suppose that the estates of the broad plain of such-and-such a region fell to a child, fell to the hand of a drunkard, fell to the hand of a knife-wielder, fell to the hand of an incompetent man. These the caliph no longer knew; he had scattered them — whoever took whatever, plundered it, picked it up, went off, devoured, carried away. But in that region whose land-grant has now fallen to the hand of this man, how many people will be trampled, how much wealth will be wasted, how many rights will be lost, ruined and trampled — these they did not think about. In those very circumstances, class disparity is to this degree; in those very circumstances in which these gifts and grants are being made, these extravagances and luxuriesfootnote:s26-17[_Turaf_: indulgence, luxurious living.] are being carried out — Yahya the Alawi(((Yahya the Alawi)))footnote:s26-18[Yahya ibn 'Abd Allah, one of the descendants of Imam Hasan, who in the time of Harun al-Rashid, in various regions of the Islamic government, gathered allegiance (_bay'a_) for himself and made his call openly in Tabaristan. My complaint is not against Harun, there is no grievance against Harun. If Harun does not do these things, he is not Harun. The grievance is against the people who had forgotten the sensitivity of the early days of Islam, who had lost the alertness and understanding that they had in the early days of Islam, who, in the face of this situation, did not feel any duty, did not feel any responsibility, did not feel any pain.@@CX(Faith | faith requires a sense of duty; the people's loss of it let tyranny go unopposed)@@ What is this for? These propagandistic hands and propaganda apparatuses, in the regionsfootnote:s26-19[(q-t-r) _aqtar_: corners, sides, regions.] of the Islamic society and the Islamic country, had for years worked upon the people's brains, upon the people's souls, upon the people's spirits — they had worked and worked until it had come to this point. So you see how important it is who the ruler of the Islamic society is. He must be the one whom God designates.
