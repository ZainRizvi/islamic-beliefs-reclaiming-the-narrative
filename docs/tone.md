# Tone & Style Guide

Editorial guidance for the English edition to offer a consistent voice across the book

## Goal

**Keep the charged, spoken voice of the lectures; adapt transcribed speech into print.**

The source is a transcript of delivered lectures:

1. It has real rhetorical force — a speaker's heat, contempt, urgency, memorable
   phrasing. **This is our strength. Protect it.**
2. It also carries the artifacts of live speech. **Adapting them to text form is the editor's job.** Fidelity is to the speaker's *meaning and force*, not to a transcript's sentence boundaries.

Optimize for a reader who is both **moved** and **able to finish**.

## Desired voice

What the finished translation should read like:

- **Hot, concrete diction.** Vivid, indicting, specific words, not cool abstract
  ones
- **Emotional charge.** The speaker's urgency and conviction come through; the
  prose has heat, not the flatness of a neutral paraphrase.
- **Oral directness.** Asides, direct address, and
  repetition-for-emphasis remain where they carry energy (see "Repetition" below).

## What to fix (the print adaptation)

Spoken-transcript artifacts. Resolving them costs nothing and makes the text
easier to absorb.

### 1. Lead with the point — re-branch left-branching syntax

Persian is head-final: the verb comes last, and modifiers stack *before* the
heads they modify (left-branching). English is head-first (right-branching). A
sentence that keeps the Persian order strands the main clause at the end, behind
a wall of qualifiers — a **periodic** sentence that reads as translationese.

Recast it **loose**: state the main assertion first, then let the qualifications
follow. A telltale shape is a sentence whose grammatical subject is a long
"To do X…" or "That Y…" phrase and whose main verb ("is the central problem,"
"must be our aim") arrives only at the very end, after a dash-bracketed
definition — rotate it so the verb and its claim come first.

- This is about **order, not length**. A short sentence can still be wrongly
  periodic. Test: does the reader reach the point early, or hold a clause open to
  the end?

### 2. Split the run-on sentences

A strong main clause plus several stacked qualifiers is the main barrier to
absorption: the idea hangs open across 20+ words before it resolves.

- A sentence past ~40 words or stacking three-plus qualifiers is a candidate to
  split.
- Break it into a punchy assertion plus a second sentence carrying the qualifier.
  **Keep every idea — just give each its own breath.**

### 3. Make the logical connections explicit

Speech conveys how one thought relates to the next through pauses, emphasis, and
tone — all of which the page loses. So where a paragraph turns, qualifies, or
concludes, that relationship needs to live in the words: a back-reference to the
prior point, or a connective that names the actual relation (cause, contrast,
consequence). Build the transition into the sentence.

### 4. Prefer accessible vocabulary where it costs nothing

Where a plainer word carries the same meaning and force, use it — a single common
word over an academic circumlocution. This is *not* license to flatten the hot diction; it applies to the
connective, explanatory prose, not the lines doing rhetorical work.

### 5. Chunk multi-part structures for scanning

When the text enumerates ("First… Second… Third…"), give each point its own clean,
scannable unit, not a longer subordinated sentence. Let the reader skim the spine
of the argument.

### 6. Collapse synonym-padding coordination

Persian coordinates near-synonyms for rhetorical fullness ("X and Y and Z" where
one word carries the sense). Carried into English literally, the series reads as
padding: the reader processes three words for one idea, and because everything is
tripled, nothing stands out. Where the coordinated terms are near-synonyms, keep
the one that lands and drop the rest (e.g. "the duties and obligations and
responsibilities" → "the duties").

This is the [Repetition](#repetition--a-judgment-call) judgment call applied to
coordination — so it is *not* blanket compression:

- **Collapse** a series whose members are interchangeable synonyms adding no
  distinct meaning — pure translation padding.
- **Keep** a series whose members name genuinely different things ("believed and
  emigrated and struggled") or that hammer a point for deliberate force. Emphatic
  repetition is the voice (see Desired voice → oral directness).

Test: does each term add meaning or heat? If a term is just a synonym filling out
the rhythm of the original, cut it; if it adds a distinct idea or builds force,
keep it.

## Deep structure — argument architecture

Rules 1–6 are line-level. The next two operate on how the *argument itself* is
built across clauses. They are harder to see — every sentence is grammatical and
idiomatic, so a copy-editor sweeps right past them — and harder to fix, because
the repair is re-ranking or re-ordering, not rewording. They matter because they
change what *shape* of argument the reader reconstructs.

### 7. Rank the argument joints (hypotaxis, not flat coordination)

Persian oratory chains clauses paratactically (and… so… because… then…) and lets
intonation supply the logical rank. English grammaticalizes rank through
**subordination** (although / because / whereas / so that / only when). Carried
over literally, our prose has connectives in abundance, but they *coordinate*
rather than *rank* — so premise, support, concession, and conclusion all arrive as
equal-weight links in a chain. The reader reconstructs the wrong architecture:
accumulation where the original built entailment, a flat heap of objections where
it defeated one ranked counter-argument, two coincidental observations where it
asserted a law.

Watch the four load-bearing joints and subordinate the supporting material instead
of coordinating it:

- **Consequence** — one governing result, with sub-results ranked or absorbed
  beneath it, not a row of co-equal "then it is that…" clauses.
- **Inference** — spend a "because" only on a genuinely distinct premise; collapse
  restated grounds into one, so a one-step deduction doesn't masquerade as a
  multi-rung syllogism.
- **Concession** — subordinate the single strongest counter-evidence ("though…")
  and demote the rest, so the rebuttal visibly defeats a named objection rather
  than an undifferentiated list.
- **Conditional** — encode the actual operator ("only when," "because,"
  "whereas"); don't balance the two directions of a law with a flat "and."

This register is already available in the book — it ranks correctly wherever the
source gave genuinely distinct material — so the flattening is a transfer artifact
to repair, not a house style.

**Voice caveat:** some paratactic series are deliberate rhetorical drumbeat, not
disguised logic (see [Repetition](#repetition--a-judgment-call)). Rank a joint
when the coordination hides argumentative structure; keep it when it is emphatic
hammering. Where it is *both*, subordinate the logic but preserve one beat of the
repetition.

### 8. Put the point in the end-focus slot (theme–rheme)

English is end-weighted: the reader reads the clause-final position as the new,
important element. Persian topic-prominence here produces a bidirectional
displacement — a claim is announced behind an empty cataphoric placeholder ("X is
this: that…"), the real content is delivered in the middle, and the unit is then
closed by a resumptive demonstrative that points back at the *topic* ("This too is
one of…"). The end-focus slot ends up holding given material while the new claim is
buried or deferred.

The reader's positional cue for "here is the point" is aimed at the topic, not the
assertion — so progressive reasoning reads as circular self-confirmation, and a
block the text *verbally* marks as the most important can be *syntactically* filed
exactly like every other block, flattening the climax into an inventory entry.

The fix re-orders information rather than trimming a word: cut the cataphoric
placeholder so the claim is asserted directly; let the sentence end on its new
content; and delete the resumptive topic-echo so a block closes on its point, not
on a pointer back to its label. Where rank genuinely matters, mark it in the
frame ("the most important manifestation is…") instead of filing the climax under
the same flat tag as the rest.

(Distinct from the line-level "is this:" pointer: the diagnostic here is the
*bidirectional* placeholder-plus-echo wrapping a whole block, a cross-sentence
information-flow pattern, not a single trailing word.)

## Repetition — a judgment call

Some repetition is the voice; some is just speech buffering that reads as padding.

- **Keep** repetition that builds force or rhythm (the speaker hammering a point).
- **Trim** repetition that merely restarts a thought with no added emphasis.

When unsure, read it aloud: if it would land in a lecture hall, keep it; if it
only fills a pause, cut it.

## The test

Two questions for any paragraph:

1. **Would it move a listener?** If the force is gone, restore the hot diction and
   directness.
2. **Can a reader get through it without re-reading?** If a sentence needs a
   second pass to parse, split it.

Pass both and it's on target: moving *and* easy to finish.

## Quick checklist

- [ ] Hot, concrete diction preserved (not softened to neutral abstraction)
- [ ] Sentences lead with the point (loose/right-branching), not verb-last periodic
- [ ] Sentences past ~40 words / three-plus stacked qualifiers split into shorter ones
- [ ] Logical connections between thoughts made explicit on the page
- [ ] Plainer vocabulary used in the connective/explanatory prose
- [ ] Enumerations chunked into scannable units
- [ ] Synonym-padding coordination collapsed; meaningful/emphatic series kept
- [ ] Argument joints ranked by subordination, not flattened to equal-weight coordination
- [ ] The new claim sits in the end-focus slot (no cataphoric placeholder + topic-echo wrapper)
- [ ] Emphatic repetition kept; buffering repetition trimmed
- [ ] Reads aloud with the speaker's force intact
