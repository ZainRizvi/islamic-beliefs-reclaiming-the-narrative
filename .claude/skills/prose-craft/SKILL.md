# Prose Craft

Craft patterns for the English edition. This book is a **faithful translation of
transcribed lectures**, so the governing aim is in `docs/tone.md`: keep the
charged, spoken voice; finish adapting transcribed speech into print. This skill
covers the line-level techniques that serve that aim. Read `docs/tone.md` first —
it is the project's voice contract; this skill is the how.

## Sentence rhythm

Rhythm carries a reader the way a speaker's cadence carries a listener. Vary
sentence length and structure to match the moment.

- Short sentences for emphasis, indictment, the turn of an argument.
- Longer cumulative sentences for exposition and built-up reasoning.
- When every sentence runs the same length and shape (subject–verb–object,
  repeat), the prose flattens regardless of content. A passage of fierce rebuke
  and a passage of quiet exposition should not read the same way.

This is the constructive twin of `docs/tone.md` rule #2: *splitting* run-ons
removes friction; *varying* what remains restores the speaker's music. Do both.

## Concrete over abstract

Specific, sensory, concrete words do the work; abstractions slide past the
reader. The lecturer reaches for named people, places, and physical images where
a translator might be tempted to substitute a tidy abstraction ("those in
difficulty," "various circumstances"). Preserve the concreteness; never trade a
vivid image for a summary category.

Where the original names a thing plainly, name it plainly. One vivid, specific
detail does more than a paragraph of general statement.

## Show the force, don't label it

The original demonstrates contempt, urgency, conviction through its words; it
rarely names the emotion. Keep that. A charged sentence that *enacts* an
attitude (an accusation stated in concrete terms) loses its force when softened
into a neutral report that merely *names* the attitude ("this was sometimes
misused").

When translating a charged passage, ask: does the English still *do* what the
speaker did, or has it stepped back to merely *report* it?

## Don't flatten to the safe middle

Default machine/AI prose sits at a flat, even register: smooth, controlled, the
same temperature whether the content is mundane or devastating. The lectures are
not even-tempered — they rise into rebuke and fall into patient explanation. Let
the English rise and fall with them. Match the heat of the prose to the heat of
the moment; don't sand every passage to the same polite middle.

## What does NOT apply here

This is nonfiction translation, not fiction. Ignore fiction-only machinery from
the upstream source: invented POV-character interiority, free indirect discourse,
fabricated sensory detail, dialogue scene-construction. Fidelity to the source
overrides all of it — never *add* color, image, or emotion the lecturer did not
deliver. The craft here is to carry his force across, not to author new prose.
